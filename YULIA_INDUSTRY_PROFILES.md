## 1. HVAC (Heating, Ventilation, and Air Conditioning)
**NAICS Code:** 238220
**Industry Category:** Essential Service

### Business Characteristics
Typical HVAC businesses serve residential and/or commercial customers with installation, repair, and maintenance services. Revenue model combines project-based installation (higher margin but lumpy) with recurring service agreements (lower margin but predictable). Seasonal peaks in summer (cooling) and winter (heating) with shoulder-season dips in spring/fall. Most operate within a 50-mile radius. Staffing: 5–50 technicians plus office/dispatch. Licensing required in most states. Businesses with strong maintenance contract bases (30%+ of revenue) command premium valuations.

### Financial Profile
- Typical Revenue Range: $800K–$15M
- Typical SDE Margin: 15%–25%
- Typical EBITDA Margin: 10%–18%
- Labor as % of Revenue: 30%–45%
- Key Expense Drivers: Labor, parts/equipment, vehicle fleet, insurance
- Capital Intensity: Medium (fleet, tools, inventory)
- Working Capital Requirements: Moderate — parts inventory plus A/R on commercial jobs

### Valuation Benchmarks (2025-2026)
- SDE Multiple: 2.5x–4.5x
- EBITDA Multiple: 4.0x–7.0x (PE roll-up deals pushing higher)
- Revenue Multiple: 0.5x–1.2x
- What earns premium multiple: 30%+ recurring maintenance revenue, diversified customer base, licensed workforce that stays post-sale, strong commercial mix, modern fleet, digital dispatch/CRM
- What earns discount multiple: Owner-operated with owner as lead tech, residential-only with no contracts, aging fleet, key-man concentration, seasonal-only revenue

### Buyer Universe
- Most common buyer type: PE-backed platform consolidators and experienced operators
- PE activity level: Very Active — HVAC is one of the most consolidated home services verticals
- Major consolidators: Wrench Group, Apex Service Partners, Hoffman Family (ARS), numerous regional platforms
- SBA eligibility: Strong — predictable cash flows, essential service, clear asset base

### M&A Market Dynamics
- Deal velocity: Fast — good HVAC businesses sell in 60–120 days
- Buyer competition: Dense — multiple PE platforms competing for quality assets
- Typical time on market: 3–6 months for well-positioned businesses
- Consolidation thesis: Recurring revenue, essential service, fragmented market (50,000+ operators), labor arbitrage through scale, brand doesn't matter (customers buy comfort, not brand)

### Due Diligence Focus Areas
1. Maintenance contract base — count, renewal rate, average revenue per contract, contract terms
2. Technician retention — tenure, licensing, compensation structure, non-competes
3. Customer concentration — no single customer >10% of revenue for residential, >20% for commercial
4. Fleet age and condition — replacement CapEx requirements
5. Warranty liability exposure — outstanding manufacturer warranty obligations

### Common Deal Structures
- Typical closing structure: Asset sale (90%+ of deals)
- Seller note prevalence: Common (10%–20% of deal value)
- Earnout prevalence: Uncommon for established businesses, sometimes used for high-growth
- Real estate: Typically excluded and leased back; some deals include shop/warehouse

### Key Risks and Red Flags
1. Owner is the top-producing technician — post-close revenue decline risk
2. No written maintenance contracts — "handshake" service agreements don't transfer
3. Refrigerant regulatory changes (R-22 phaseout complete, R-410A transition) — equipment obsolescence
4. Technician poaching post-acquisition — especially in tight labor markets
5. Seasonal cash flow gaps creating working capital stress

### Yulia's Current Recommendation
**For sellers:** This is a seller's market. PE demand is insatiable. If you have $1.5M+ revenue with 25%+ recurring maintenance contracts, you're in the sweet spot. Clean up your books, formalize your service contracts, and you'll see strong multiples. Don't accept the first offer — run a process.

**For buyers:** Expect to pay up for quality. The days of buying HVAC businesses at 2.5x SDE are fading except for small, owner-dependent shops. Focus on businesses with strong service contract bases and retained technicians. SBA financing works extremely well here.

## 2. Pest Control
**NAICS Code:** 561210
**Industry Category:** Essential Service

### Business Characteristics
Pest control businesses provide recurring treatment services (monthly/quarterly/annual) for residential and commercial properties. The business model is highly attractive because of predictable recurring revenue — top operators have 70%+ of revenue from recurring contracts. Technicians follow route-based schedules. Licensing required in all states. Low capital requirements. Geographic density matters for route efficiency. Seasonal peaks vary by region (termites in spring, mosquitoes in summer, rodents in fall/winter).

### Financial Profile
- Typical Revenue Range: $500K–$10M
- Typical SDE Margin: 18%–30%
- Typical EBITDA Margin: 12%–22%
- Labor as % of Revenue: 35%–50%
- Key Expense Drivers: Labor (technicians), chemicals/materials, vehicles, insurance, licensing
- Capital Intensity: Low (basic vehicles, spray equipment)
- Working Capital Requirements: Low — most customers pre-pay or pay monthly

### Valuation Benchmarks (2025-2026)
- SDE Multiple: 3.0x–5.0x
- EBITDA Multiple: 5.0x–8.0x (premium for recurring revenue)
- Revenue Multiple: 0.8x–1.5x
- What earns premium multiple: 70%+ recurring revenue, low customer churn (<10% annual), route density, diversified services (termite, mosquito, wildlife), commercial contracts
- What earns discount multiple: One-time treatment focus, high churn, thin route density, owner-as-sole-technician, regulatory issues

### Buyer Universe
- Most common buyer type: PE-backed platforms and strategic acquirers
- PE activity level: Very Active — one of the hottest roll-up verticals
- Major consolidators: Rentokil (Terminix), Anticimex, ABC Home & Commercial, HomeTeam, numerous regional platforms
- SBA eligibility: Strong — recurring revenue model is ideal for SBA underwriting

### M&A Market Dynamics
- Deal velocity: Very fast — quality pest control businesses often have multiple offers within 30 days
- Buyer competition: Very dense — strategic acquirers, PE platforms, and owner-operators all competing
- Typical time on market: 2–4 months for businesses with strong recurring base
- Consolidation thesis: Predictable recurring revenue, route density economics, chemical purchasing scale, low customer switching cost, essential service (pests don't stop)

### Due Diligence Focus Areas
1. Customer churn rate — monthly and annual cancellation rates, reasons for churn
2. Route density and efficiency — revenue per route day, drive time between stops
3. Chemical licensing and regulatory compliance — EPA compliance, state licensing, chemical storage
4. Contract terms — auto-renewal vs manual renewal, pricing escalation clauses
5. Customer acquisition cost and channels — referral vs digital vs door-to-door

### Common Deal Structures
- Typical closing structure: Asset sale
- Seller note prevalence: Common (10%–15%)
- Earnout prevalence: Uncommon — recurring revenue provides certainty
- Real estate: Usually excluded (small office/warehouse, often leased)

### Key Risks and Red Flags
1. High customer churn (>15% annually) — suggests service quality issues
2. Revenue concentrated in one-time treatments vs recurring
3. Owner personally services top accounts — relationship dependency
4. Regulatory violations or chemical handling issues
5. Thin route density — high drive time erodes margins

### Yulia's Current Recommendation
**For sellers:** You're sitting on gold. Pest control recurring revenue is the most sought-after asset class in home services M&A. If you have $1M+ revenue with 65%+ recurring and <12% churn, expect competitive interest. Document your churn metrics and route economics — buyers will pay a premium for transparency.

**For buyers:** Be prepared for premium pricing. Quality pest control businesses rarely trade below 4x SDE. The economics justify it — 85%+ gross margin on recurring stops, low capital requirements, and recession-proof demand. Focus on route density and churn rates over revenue size.

## 3. Landscaping / Lawn Care
**NAICS Code:** 561730
**Industry Category:** Essential Service

### Business Characteristics
Landscaping businesses range from basic lawn maintenance to full-service design/build operations. Revenue models include recurring maintenance contracts (weekly/biweekly mowing, seasonal treatments) and project-based work (hardscaping, design, installation). Highly seasonal in northern climates with potential for snow removal offset. Labor-intensive with crew-based operations. Most serve within 30-mile radius. Ranges from solo operators to 100+ employee operations. Commercial contracts provide stability; residential provides margin.

### Financial Profile
- Typical Revenue Range: $400K–$8M
- Typical SDE Margin: 12%–22%
- Typical EBITDA Margin: 8%–16%
- Labor as % of Revenue: 40%–55%
- Key Expense Drivers: Labor (crews), equipment, fuel, materials, insurance
- Capital Intensity: Medium-High (mowers, trucks, trailers, heavy equipment for design/build)
- Working Capital Requirements: Moderate — seasonal cash flow variation, equipment financing

### Valuation Benchmarks (2025-2026)
- SDE Multiple: 2.0x–3.5x
- EBITDA Multiple: 3.5x–5.5x
- Revenue Multiple: 0.3x–0.8x
- What earns premium multiple: 60%+ recurring maintenance contracts, commercial accounts, year-round revenue (snow removal), strong crew retention, owner not on trucks
- What earns discount multiple: Project-heavy revenue, seasonal-only, owner as crew leader, H-2B visa dependency, aging equipment fleet

### Buyer Universe
- Most common buyer type: Owner-operators and emerging PE platforms
- PE activity level: Active — growing consolidation interest, especially in commercial maintenance
- Major consolidators: BrightView (large commercial), emerging regional roll-ups
- SBA eligibility: Moderate — seasonal cash flow can complicate DSCR, but recurring contracts help

### M&A Market Dynamics
- Deal velocity: Moderate — good businesses sell in 4–8 months
- Buyer competition: Moderate — less intense than HVAC/pest control but growing
- Typical time on market: 4–8 months
- Consolidation thesis: Recurring maintenance revenue, labor pool aggregation, equipment purchasing power, geographic density, cross-selling (maintenance + enhancement + snow)

### Due Diligence Focus Areas
1. Contract base — recurring vs project split, contract terms, renewal rates
2. Labor force — H-2B dependency, crew retention, seasonal staffing model
3. Equipment fleet — age, condition, remaining useful life, replacement schedule
4. Customer concentration — especially in commercial accounts
5. Insurance and safety record — Workers' comp mod rate, OSHA compliance

### Common Deal Structures
- Typical closing structure: Asset sale
- Seller note prevalence: Common (15%–25%)
- Earnout prevalence: Sometimes for design/build component
- Real estate: Excluded (equipment yard often leased)

### Key Risks and Red Flags
1. H-2B visa dependency — policy changes can eliminate 40%+ of workforce overnight
2. Owner drives the largest crew or manages key commercial relationships
3. Equipment fleet requiring $200K+ in near-term replacement
4. Seasonal concentration without snow removal or other offset
5. Undocumented workers — massive regulatory and legal exposure

### Yulia's Current Recommendation
**For sellers:** The market values recurring maintenance revenue and commercial contracts. If you're project-heavy, consider shifting mix toward maintenance before selling — even 12 months of building contract base moves multiples. Labor documentation is critical; buyers will scrutinize your workforce legality.

**For buyers:** Landscaping offers attractive entry points for first-time buyers. Smaller operations ($500K–$1.5M) are often undermanaged and respond well to professional management. Focus on businesses with recurring maintenance contracts and avoid project-dependent operations. H-2B risk is real — understand the labor model completely.

## 4. Plumbing
**NAICS Code:** 238110
**Industry Category:** Essential Service

### Business Characteristics
Plumbing businesses provide residential and commercial installation, repair, and service. Mix of emergency/demand work (higher margin, unpredictable) and planned work (new construction, remodeling). Service agreements becoming more common but less established than HVAC. Licensed trade — plumber licensing required in all states. Staffing: journeyman plumbers are expensive and scarce. Typical radius 30–60 miles. New construction exposure adds cyclicality.

### Financial Profile
- Typical Revenue Range: $600K–$10M
- Typical SDE Margin: 15%–25%
- Typical EBITDA Margin: 10%–18%
- Labor as % of Revenue: 35%–48%
- Key Expense Drivers: Licensed labor, parts/fixtures, vehicles, insurance, bonding
- Capital Intensity: Low-Medium (vehicles, tools, some specialized equipment)
- Working Capital Requirements: Moderate — new construction has longer payment cycles

### Valuation Benchmarks (2025-2026)
- SDE Multiple: 2.5x–4.0x
- EBITDA Multiple: 4.0x–6.5x
- Revenue Multiple: 0.4x–1.0x
- What earns premium multiple: Service/repair focus (vs new construction), recurring service agreements, strong online reviews/reputation, multiple licensed plumbers, low owner dependency
- What earns discount multiple: New construction dependency, owner-as-sole-master-plumber, no service agreements, poor online reputation, license concentration risk

### Buyer Universe
- Most common buyer type: PE-backed home services platforms and experienced operators
- PE activity level: Active — following HVAC's consolidation playbook
- Major consolidators: Emerging (plumbing is 2–3 years behind HVAC in consolidation)
- SBA eligibility: Strong — essential service, good cash flows

### M&A Market Dynamics
- Deal velocity: Moderate-Fast — quality plumbing businesses with service focus sell quickly
- Buyer competition: Growing — PE platforms expanding from HVAC into plumbing
- Typical time on market: 4–6 months
- Consolidation thesis: Essential service, licensed labor scarcity creates barrier, fragmented market, cross-sell with HVAC/electrical, recurring service potential untapped

### Due Diligence Focus Areas
1. License holders — who holds the master plumber license, will they stay post-sale
2. Revenue mix — service/repair vs new construction vs remodel percentages
3. Warranty callbacks and liability — rework rate, insurance claims history
4. Online reputation — Google/Yelp reviews directly impact demand generation
5. Technician pipeline — apprenticeship program, journeyman retention

### Common Deal Structures
- Typical closing structure: Asset sale
- Seller note prevalence: Common (10%–20%)
- Earnout prevalence: Uncommon
- Real estate: Usually excluded

### Key Risks and Red Flags
1. Master plumber license held only by owner — license doesn't transfer, business can't operate
2. Heavy new construction dependency — cyclical, margin compression in downturns
3. Warranty liability — poor work creates long-tail exposure
4. Key technician departure risk — in a labor-scarce trade
5. Under-reported cash revenue — common in residential plumbing

### Yulia's Current Recommendation
**For sellers:** Plumbing is the next HVAC in terms of PE interest. If you're ahead of the consolidation wave, you can command strong multiples. Critical: ensure your master plumber license isn't the only one in the business — buyers will discount heavily or walk if the license leaves with you. Build service agreements now.

**For buyers:** Plumbing is an attractive buy right now because consolidation is earlier-stage than HVAC, meaning lower multiples for comparable quality. Look for businesses with service/repair focus (not new construction) and multiple license holders. The labor scarcity creates a natural moat once you have a team.

## 5. Electrical Contracting
**NAICS Code:** 238210
**Industry Category:** Essential Service

### Business Characteristics
Electrical contractors serve residential, commercial, and industrial markets with installation, repair, and maintenance of electrical systems. Revenue splits between service/repair (higher margin, recurring), new construction (volume, cyclical), and specialty work (data centers, EV charging, solar — growing rapidly). Licensed trade requiring master electrician licensure. Workforce ranges from 5 to 200+ electricians. Growing demand from EV infrastructure, data center expansion, and energy efficiency retrofits.

### Financial Profile
- Typical Revenue Range: $700K–$20M
- Typical SDE Margin: 12%–22%
- Typical EBITDA Margin: 8%–15%
- Labor as % of Revenue: 35%–50%
- Key Expense Drivers: Licensed labor, materials (copper/wire), vehicles, insurance, bonding
- Capital Intensity: Low-Medium
- Working Capital Requirements: Moderate-High for commercial (long payment cycles, bonding requirements)

### Valuation Benchmarks (2025-2026)
- SDE Multiple: 2.5x–4.0x
- EBITDA Multiple: 4.0x–6.0x
- Revenue Multiple: 0.4x–0.9x
- What earns premium multiple: Commercial/industrial mix with recurring maintenance, EV/solar specialty, multiple license holders, government contracts, strong safety record
- What earns discount multiple: New construction only, single license holder (owner), union labor dependency in non-union market, bonding capacity constraints

### Buyer Universe
- Most common buyer type: PE-backed platforms and strategic acquirers
- PE activity level: Active — accelerating due to electrification trends
- Major consolidators: IES Holdings, MYR Group, emerging regional platforms
- SBA eligibility: Strong for service-focused; challenging for large commercial (bonding requirements)

### M&A Market Dynamics
- Deal velocity: Moderate — 4–8 months
- Buyer competition: Growing — electrification tailwinds attracting new buyers
- Typical time on market: 4–8 months
- Consolidation thesis: Electrification mega-trend (EV, solar, data centers), licensed labor scarcity, bonding capacity aggregation, cross-sell with HVAC/plumbing

### Due Diligence Focus Areas
1. License holders — master electrician license transfer or retention
2. Revenue mix — service vs new construction vs specialty
3. Safety record — OSHA violations, EMR (Experience Modification Rate)
4. Bonding capacity and surety relationship
5. Backlog quality — contracted but unperformed work

### Common Deal Structures
- Typical closing structure: Asset sale (small/mid), stock sale (larger with bonding/contracts)
- Seller note prevalence: Common (10%–20%)
- Earnout prevalence: Sometimes tied to backlog completion
- Real estate: Usually excluded

### Key Risks and Red Flags
1. Master electrician license concentration — same risk as plumbing
2. Large project concentration — single project >25% of annual revenue
3. Safety violations or high EMR — affects insurance costs and biddability
4. New construction cyclicality — revenue can drop 30%+ in downturns
5. Material cost volatility (copper) — margin compression on fixed-price contracts

### Yulia's Current Recommendation
**For sellers:** The electrification trend is your friend. If you've built EV charging, solar, or data center capabilities, your multiple expands significantly. Pure residential service shops also sell well. Avoid going to market if you're heavily dependent on one large construction project.

**For buyers:** Electrical is benefiting from structural tailwinds that other trades don't have. EV infrastructure alone will drive decades of demand. Look for service-focused operations with emerging specialty capabilities. The license transfer issue is the same as plumbing — solve it before closing.

## 6. Commercial Cleaning / Janitorial
**NAICS Code:** 561210
**Industry Category:** Essential Service

### Business Characteristics
Commercial cleaning companies provide recurring janitorial services to offices, medical facilities, schools, industrial sites, and retail. Revenue is almost entirely contract-based with monthly recurring billing. Low-skill labor model with high turnover. Operations are shift-based (often nights/weekends). Geographic density is critical for supervisor efficiency. Post-COVID demand for enhanced sanitization protocols created new revenue streams. Typical operations: 20–500+ cleaners.

### Financial Profile
- Typical Revenue Range: $500K–$15M
- Typical SDE Margin: 10%–18%
- Typical EBITDA Margin: 8%–14%
- Labor as % of Revenue: 55%–70%
- Key Expense Drivers: Labor (dominant), cleaning supplies, insurance, transportation
- Capital Intensity: Very Low (basic equipment, no fleet)
- Working Capital Requirements: Low-Moderate — monthly contract billing

### Valuation Benchmarks (2025-2026)
- SDE Multiple: 2.0x–3.5x
- EBITDA Multiple: 3.0x–5.5x
- Revenue Multiple: 0.3x–0.7x
- What earns premium multiple: Long-term contracts (3+ years), specialized verticals (medical, cleanroom), low customer concentration, strong middle management, documented SOPs
- What earns discount multiple: At-will contracts, high customer concentration, owner manages daily operations, high labor turnover (>100% annually), minimum wage pressure

### Buyer Universe
- Most common buyer type: Owner-operators and strategic acquirers
- PE activity level: Emerging — interest growing in specialized segments
- Major consolidators: ABM Industries, Marsden Holding, regional platforms emerging
- SBA eligibility: Strong — predictable recurring revenue, low capital requirements

### M&A Market Dynamics
- Deal velocity: Moderate — 4–8 months
- Buyer competition: Moderate
- Typical time on market: 4–8 months
- Consolidation thesis: Recurring revenue, geographic density, supply purchasing power, quality differentiation in a commoditized market

### Due Diligence Focus Areas
1. Contract terms — length, auto-renewal, termination clauses, price escalation
2. Customer concentration — no single customer >15% of revenue
3. Employee documentation — I-9 compliance is critical in this industry
4. Insurance coverage — general liability, workers' comp, bonding
5. Quality control systems — inspection scores, customer satisfaction tracking

### Common Deal Structures
- Typical closing structure: Asset sale
- Seller note prevalence: Common (15%–25%)
- Earnout prevalence: Sometimes tied to customer retention
- Real estate: Not applicable (service-based)

### Key Risks and Red Flags
1. I-9 compliance issues — undocumented workforce exposure
2. Single customer >20% of revenue — contract loss is devastating
3. No written contracts — handshake agreements don't transfer
4. Minimum wage increases — direct margin compression with limited pass-through
5. Workers' comp claims — high injury rates in physical labor

### Yulia's Current Recommendation
**For sellers:** Your recurring contracts are your value. Get them formalized with auto-renewal and escalation clauses. Clean up your I-9 files — this is the #1 DD killer in commercial cleaning. If you're specialized (medical, cleanroom), highlight that differentiation aggressively.

**For buyers:** Commercial cleaning is a great first-time buyer business — low capital requirements, simple operations, recurring revenue. But labor management is the entire business. Expect to spend your time on HR, not sales. Specialized segments (healthcare, industrial) offer higher margins and stickier contracts.

## 7. Residential Cleaning
**NAICS Code:** 561720
**Industry Category:** Essential Service

### Business Characteristics
Residential cleaning companies provide recurring maid/housekeeping services to homeowners. Revenue model is recurring (weekly/biweekly/monthly) with some one-time deep cleans. Staffing ranges from sole operators to 50+ cleaners. Low barriers to entry create intense competition. Brand and trust matter — customers let cleaners into their homes. Growing demand from dual-income households and aging demographics. Many operate as franchise (Molly Maid, Merry Maids, The Cleaning Authority) or independent.

### Financial Profile
- Typical Revenue Range: $200K–$3M
- Typical SDE Margin: 15%–28%
- Typical EBITDA Margin: 10%–20%
- Labor as % of Revenue: 45%–60%
- Key Expense Drivers: Labor, supplies, transportation, insurance, marketing
- Capital Intensity: Very Low
- Working Capital Requirements: Very Low — customers pay per visit or monthly

### Valuation Benchmarks (2025-2026)
- SDE Multiple: 1.5x–3.0x
- EBITDA Multiple: 2.5x–4.5x
- Revenue Multiple: 0.3x–0.6x
- What earns premium multiple: Strong recurring customer base with low churn, professional brand/marketing, team-based (not owner cleaning), online booking system, 100+ active recurring customers
- What earns discount multiple: Owner does the cleaning, no recurring contracts, Craigslist-dependent marketing, cash-heavy unreported income

### Buyer Universe
- Most common buyer type: First-time buyers and existing cleaning operators
- PE activity level: Low (too small for institutional interest in most cases)
- Major consolidators: Franchise brands acquire independents occasionally
- SBA eligibility: Moderate — small deal sizes often below SBA minimums

### M&A Market Dynamics
- Deal velocity: Slow-Moderate — 6–12 months
- Buyer competition: Thin — fewer sophisticated buyers at this size
- Typical time on market: 6–12 months
- Consolidation thesis: Limited — fragmented and commoditized; brand-based roll-ups have mixed results

### Due Diligence Focus Areas
1. Customer retention rate — monthly churn is the critical metric
2. Employee background checks and bonding — trust-based service in private homes
3. Revenue documentation — cash transactions must be verifiable
4. Online reviews and reputation — Google/Yelp directly drive acquisition
5. Scheduling system and operational efficiency

### Common Deal Structures
- Typical closing structure: Asset sale
- Seller note prevalence: Very common (20%–30% — often necessary to get deal done)
- Earnout prevalence: Sometimes tied to customer retention
- Real estate: Not applicable

### Key Risks and Red Flags
1. Owner does the cleaning — no business to transfer
2. Cash revenue that can't be documented — reduces bankable SDE
3. No background checks on employees entering homes — liability exposure
4. Single-channel customer acquisition (word of mouth only) — growth ceiling
5. High customer churn (>5% monthly) — business is a treadmill

### Yulia's Current Recommendation
**For sellers:** If you've built a team-based operation with 100+ recurring customers and you're not cleaning houses yourself, you have a sellable business. Document every dollar — cash revenue you can't prove doesn't exist for valuation purposes. Invest in online presence before listing.

**For buyers:** Great entry-level business if you want to learn management. Low capital, simple operations. But be realistic: most residential cleaning businesses are lifestyle businesses, not scalable enterprises. Buy one with a team already in place and a proven customer acquisition engine.

## 8. Roofing
**NAICS Code:** 238160
**Industry Category:** Essential Service

### Business Characteristics
Roofing contractors install and repair residential and commercial roofs. Revenue is project-based with limited recurring component (some maintenance contracts on commercial). Highly seasonal in northern climates. Storm-driven demand creates windfall years in affected geographies. Labor model: crews of 3–8 workers per job. Mix of employee and subcontractor labor. Insurance restoration work (storm damage) is a significant revenue source. Growing solar integration opportunity.

### Financial Profile
- Typical Revenue Range: $800K–$15M
- Typical SDE Margin: 12%–22%
- Typical EBITDA Margin: 8%–16%
- Labor as % of Revenue: 35%–50% (higher if employee-based, lower with subs)
- Key Expense Drivers: Labor/subcontractors, materials (shingles, membranes), equipment, insurance
- Capital Intensity: Medium (equipment, vehicles, safety gear)
- Working Capital Requirements: Moderate — material purchases before payment received

### Valuation Benchmarks (2025-2026)
- SDE Multiple: 2.0x–3.5x
- EBITDA Multiple: 3.0x–5.0x
- Revenue Multiple: 0.3x–0.7x
- What earns premium multiple: Commercial focus with maintenance contracts, insurance restoration specialty, diversified revenue (not storm-dependent), solar integration capability, strong safety record
- What earns discount multiple: Residential re-roof only, storm-chasing model, subcontractor-only (no employee base), OSHA violations, high warranty claims

### Buyer Universe
- Most common buyer type: Owner-operators and home services platforms
- PE activity level: Emerging — following HVAC/plumbing into roofing
- Major consolidators: Limited but growing; some HVAC platforms adding roofing
- SBA eligibility: Moderate — project-based revenue creates DSCR variability

### M&A Market Dynamics
- Deal velocity: Moderate — 4–8 months
- Buyer competition: Moderate
- Typical time on market: 4–8 months
- Consolidation thesis: Home services platform extension, insurance restoration specialization, commercial maintenance contracts, solar integration cross-sell

### Due Diligence Focus Areas
1. Revenue composition — organic vs storm-driven vs insurance restoration
2. Safety record — falls are the #1 cause of construction fatalities; OSHA scrutiny intense
3. Warranty obligations — outstanding warranty exposure on completed jobs
4. Subcontractor vs employee classification — misclassification risk
5. Insurance and bonding — workers' comp rates, general liability limits

### Common Deal Structures
- Typical closing structure: Asset sale
- Seller note prevalence: Common (15%–25%)
- Earnout prevalence: Sometimes, especially if storm-driven revenue inflates recent years
- Real estate: Usually excluded

### Key Risks and Red Flags
1. Storm-inflated revenue — if last 2 years include a major storm event, normalize out
2. Safety violations — OSHA fines and high EMR rates
3. Subcontractor misclassification — IRS and state audit exposure
4. Warranty liability on defective installations — long-tail risk
5. Owner runs all sales/estimates — customer acquisition dependency

### Yulia's Current Recommendation
**For sellers:** Buyers are suspicious of roofing revenue — they'll normalize out storm years and discount project-based income. Build commercial maintenance contracts and prove recurring revenue exists. If you've done insurance restoration work, document your adjustor relationships and process.

**For buyers:** Roofing can be a good acquisition if you understand the revenue quality issue. Look for commercial maintenance contracts and diversified revenue sources. Avoid storm-chaser operations. The solar integration opportunity is real and creates a growth narrative that supports higher multiples.

## 9. Pool Service
**NAICS Code:** 561790
**Industry Category:** Essential Service

### Business Characteristics
Pool service businesses provide recurring maintenance (weekly cleaning, chemical balancing), repair, and equipment replacement for residential and commercial pools. Highly recurring revenue model — pool owners need weekly service year-round in warm climates, seasonal in northern markets. Route-based operations similar to pest control. Low complexity, high customer retention. Equipment repair/replacement provides supplementary margin. Growing automation (robotic cleaners, smart chemical systems) changing service model.

### Financial Profile
- Typical Revenue Range: $300K–$5M
- Typical SDE Margin: 20%–35%
- Typical EBITDA Margin: 15%–28%
- Labor as % of Revenue: 30%–45%
- Key Expense Drivers: Labor (route technicians), chemicals, equipment/parts, vehicles
- Capital Intensity: Low (vehicles, basic equipment)
- Working Capital Requirements: Low — monthly billing, minimal inventory

### Valuation Benchmarks (2025-2026)
- SDE Multiple: 2.5x–4.0x
- EBITDA Multiple: 3.5x–5.5x
- Revenue Multiple: 0.5x–1.0x
- What earns premium multiple: 90%+ recurring revenue, year-round climate, low churn, route density, commercial pool contracts, equipment repair capabilities
- What earns discount multiple: Seasonal-only market, owner on routes, thin route density, no equipment repair capability, high churn

### Buyer Universe
- Most common buyer type: Individual operators and small PE platforms
- PE activity level: Emerging — consolidation beginning in Sun Belt markets
- Major consolidators: Pool Corp (supply side), emerging service platforms in Florida, Texas, Arizona
- SBA eligibility: Strong — high recurring revenue, predictable cash flows

### M&A Market Dynamics
- Deal velocity: Moderate — 4–8 months
- Buyer competition: Growing in Sun Belt markets, thin elsewhere

### M&A Market Dynamics
- Deal velocity: Moderate — 4–8 months
- Buyer competition: Moderate to Dense
- Typical time on market: 3–8 months
- Consolidation thesis: Demographic tailwind (aging population), pre-need contract value, operational efficiency, pricing optimization, insurance/product purchasing power

### Due Diligence Focus Areas
1. Call volume trends — annual cases served, market share in service area
2. Pre-need contracts — trust fund balances, contract obligations, attrition
3. Facility condition — preparation room compliance, public areas, parking
4. License holders — funeral director licenses, will they stay post-sale
5. Cremation vs traditional mix — revenue per case implications

### Common Deal Structures
- Typical closing structure: Entity sale (to preserve licenses, pre-need contracts, insurance assignments)
- Seller note prevalence: Common (10%–20%)
- Earnout prevalence: Uncommon — stable demand
- Real estate: Almost always included (purpose-built facilities)

### Key Risks and Red Flags
1. Declining call volume — population decline or competitive entry
2. Cremation shift reducing average revenue per case
3. Pre-need trust fund underfunding — contractual obligations exceed trust assets
4. Facility code violations — preparation room compliance, ADA, environmental
5. Reputation damage — single negative incident can devastate a funeral home's community standing

### Yulia's Current Recommendation
**For sellers:** Funeral home valuations remain strong driven by demographic tailwinds and corporate consolidator demand. Your community reputation is your most valuable asset — protect it through the transition. Pre-need contract books are highly valued; if you don't have a pre-need program, consider starting one 2+ years before sale. Owned real estate adds significant asset backing to your valuation.

**For buyers:** Funeral homes are one of the most recession-proof, demographically-driven acquisitions available. The cremation trend is a headwind to revenue per case but a tailwind to margins (lower COGS). Focus on facilities with strong community reputation, growing service area demographics, and modern facilities. The business is as much pastoral as operational — you need the right temperament, not just the right capital.

## 34. Childcare / Daycare Center
**NAICS Code:** 624410
**Industry Category:** Specialized

### Business Characteristics
Childcare centers provide care and early education for children ages 0–5 (infant, toddler, preschool), before/after school care, and summer programs. Revenue model: weekly/monthly tuition per child. Licensed capacity determines revenue ceiling. Heavily regulated: state licensing (ratios, qualifications, facility requirements), health/safety codes, background checks. Labor-intensive: teacher-to-child ratios mandated by law (1:4 for infants, 1:10 for preschool). Waitlists common in quality centers. Growing demand from dual-income households.

### Financial Profile
- Typical Revenue Range: $300K–$3M
- Typical SDE Margin: 12%–22%
- Typical EBITDA Margin: 8%–16%
- Labor as % of Revenue: 55%–70%
- Key Expense Drivers: Teacher salaries (dominant), facility, food, supplies, insurance, licensing
- Capital Intensity: Medium (facility buildout, playground equipment, furniture)
- Working Capital Requirements: Low — tuition collected in advance

### Valuation Benchmarks (2025-2026)
- SDE Multiple: 2.0x–3.5x
- EBITDA Multiple: 3.5x–6.0x
- Revenue Multiple: 0.4x–0.8x
- What earns premium multiple: Full enrollment with waitlist, strong reputation/NAEYC accreditation, owned facility, infant program (highest revenue per child), stable teacher retention, growing market area
- What earns discount multiple: Under-enrolled, compliance deficiencies, high teacher turnover, short lease, aging facility, declining birth rate market

### Buyer Universe
- Most common buyer type: Experienced childcare operators, small chains, mission-driven buyers
- PE activity level: Emerging — growing interest in childcare as essential infrastructure
- Major consolidators: KinderCare (KLC), Bright Horizons, Learning Care Group, Spring Education
- SBA eligibility: Strong — essential service, predictable revenue, community need

### M&A Market Dynamics
- Deal velocity: Moderate — 4–8 months
- Buyer competition: Moderate — growing
- Typical time on market: 4–10 months
- Consolidation thesis: Teacher recruitment efficiency, curriculum standardization, compliance management, real estate optimization, employer partnership programs

### Due Diligence Focus Areas
1. Licensing compliance — state licensing history, violations, corrective actions
2. Enrollment capacity and utilization — licensed capacity vs actual enrollment, waitlist
3. Teacher retention — turnover rate, qualifications, compensation vs market
4. Facility condition — playground safety, building codes, accessibility
5. Parent satisfaction and reputation — reviews, referral sources, tour-to-enrollment conversion

### Common Deal Structures
- Typical closing structure: Asset sale or entity sale (to preserve licensing)
- Seller note prevalence: Common (15%–25%)
- Earnout prevalence: Sometimes tied to enrollment maintenance
- Real estate: Often included if owned; lease assignment if rented

### Key Risks and Red Flags
1. Licensing violations — any history of serious compliance failures
2. Teacher turnover >40% annually — destabilizes enrollment and parent confidence
3. Single-building, leased facility with short remaining term
4. Under-enrollment without clear cause — marketing issue vs market issue
5. Safeguarding incident — child safety incident can close the business

### Yulia's Current Recommendation
**For sellers:** Childcare centers with waitlists, clean licensing records, and stable teachers are in demand. The post-COVID childcare crisis has actually increased buyer interest as communities recognize childcare as essential infrastructure. Document your enrollment history, teacher tenure, and parent satisfaction metrics. Owned real estate adds significant value.

**For buyers:** Childcare is a mission-driven business with solid economics if managed well. The labor challenge is your primary operational concern — you need to recruit, pay, and retain qualified teachers in a competitive market. Focus on centers with full enrollment, clean compliance records, and strong community reputation. The regulatory environment is complex — understand your state's licensing requirements thoroughly before acquiring.

## 35. Franchise Business (General Framework)
**NAICS Code:** Multiple
**Industry Category:** Specialized

### Business Characteristics
Franchise businesses operate under a franchisor's brand, systems, and processes. Covers all industries: food service (McDonald's, Subway), fitness (Orangetheory, F45), services (Servpro, The Cleaning Authority), automotive (Midas, Meineke), and more. Revenue model determined by franchise concept. Franchisee pays: initial franchise fee ($25K–$100K+), ongoing royalty (4%–8% of revenue), marketing fund contribution (1%–3% of revenue). Franchise agreement governs territory, operations, standards, and transfer rights. Resale requires franchisor approval.

### Financial Profile
- Typical Revenue Range: Varies widely by concept ($300K–$10M+)
- Typical SDE Margin: 12%–25% (after royalties and marketing fund)
- Typical EBITDA Margin: 8%–18%
- Labor as % of Revenue: Varies by concept (15%–60%)
- Key Expense Drivers: Royalty fees, marketing fund, concept-specific COGS, labor, facility
- Capital Intensity: Varies (low for service franchises, very high for restaurant)
- Working Capital Requirements: Varies by concept

### Valuation Benchmarks (2025-2026)
- SDE Multiple: 1.5x–4.0x (varies dramatically by brand)
- EBITDA Multiple: 3.0x–7.0x
- Revenue Multiple: 0.3x–1.0x
- What earns premium multiple: Strong brand (nationally recognized), multi-unit operation, top-quartile unit economics, exclusive territory, modern facility, remaining franchise term 10+ years
- What earns discount multiple: Weak/unknown brand, under-performing unit, franchise agreement expiring soon, franchise system in decline, territory competition, franchisee-franchisor disputes

### Buyer Universe
- Most common buyer type: Multi-unit franchisees, first-time buyers (smaller concepts), PE-backed franchise platforms
- PE activity level: Active for multi-unit franchise operations
- Major consolidators: Concept-specific (multi-unit operators within each brand system)
- SBA eligibility: Strong — SBA Franchise Directory pre-approval for most concepts

### M&A Market Dynamics
- Deal velocity: Moderate — 4–8 months (franchisor approval adds time)
- Buyer competition: Moderate — limited to franchisor-approved buyers
- Typical time on market: 4–10 months
- Consolidation thesis: Multi-unit operating efficiency, district/area development rights, proven system execution

### Due Diligence Focus Areas
1. Franchise agreement terms — remaining term, renewal conditions, transfer provisions, territorial rights
2. Franchisor relationship — standing with franchisor, compliance history, any default notices
3. FDD (Franchise Disclosure Document) — Item 19 financial performance, litigation (Item 3), fees (Items 5-6)
4. Territory protection — exclusive vs non-exclusive, delivery/digital territory issues
5. Unit economics — revenue vs FDD median, COGS variance, comparison to top performers

### Common Deal Structures
- Typical closing structure: Asset sale (with franchise agreement assignment)
- Seller note prevalence: Common (10%–20%)
- Earnout prevalence: Uncommon — franchise systems provide operational predictability
- Real estate: Usually excluded (leased); some concepts include real estate

### Key Risks and Red Flags
1. Franchise agreement expiring within 5 years — renewal not guaranteed
2. Franchisor financial distress — brand support and innovation degrades
3. Under-performance vs FDD Item 19 benchmarks — systemic operational issues
4. Transfer fee and training requirements — add $25K–$75K to acquisition cost
5. Franchisor right of first refusal — can block or delay your acquisition

### Yulia's Current Recommendation
**For sellers:** Franchise resales are governed by your franchise agreement — review it immediately to understand transfer provisions, franchisor approval requirements, right of first refusal, and any transfer fees. Your best buyer is often an existing franchisee in the system who wants to add units. Document your unit economics relative to the FDD Item 19 — top-performing units sell at significant premiums.

**For buyers:** Franchise acquisitions offer a proven system but at a cost (royalties, marketing fund, operational constraints). Read the FDD cover to cover — especially Items 3 (litigation), 5-6 (fees), 12 (territory), 17 (renewal/termination), and 19 (financial performance). Always compare the target unit's performance to the system average. The franchise system de-risks operations but limits upside — you're buying a job with a proven playbook, not a blank canvas.

# APPENDIX E: INDUSTRY-SPECIFIC DD CHECKLISTS

## Environmental Risk Industries — Mandatory Phase I Assessment
These industries REQUIRE environmental assessment before closing:
- Auto Repair / Auto Body — soil contamination from oils, solvents, fluids
- Dry Cleaning — perchloroethylene (PERC) contamination, often extends beyond property
- Manufacturing (all types) — machining fluids, plating chemicals, waste disposal
- Gas Station / Car Wash — underground storage tanks, fuel contamination
- Trucking — fuel storage, vehicle maintenance contamination
- Roofing — chemical storage, disposal practices

**Cost:** Phase I: $2,000–$5,000. Phase II (if Phase I identifies concerns): $5,000–$25,000+.
**Timeline:** Phase I: 2–4 weeks. Phase II: 4–8 weeks.
**Consequence of skipping:** Buyer inherits unlimited environmental liability under CERCLA.

## License-Critical Industries — Transfer or Retention Planning
These industries have licenses that DO NOT automatically transfer with sale:
- **Plumbing** — Master plumber license held by individual, not business
- **Electrical** — Master electrician license, same issue
- **HVAC** — EPA certifications, some state contractor licenses
- **Dental** — Dental license held by DVM, practice operates under that license
- **Veterinary** — DVM license, DEA license for controlled substances
- **Optometry** — OD license, dispensing permits
- **Pharmacy** — Pharmacist license, DEA registration, state board of pharmacy
- **Physical Therapy** — PT/DC license, state practice requirements
- **Funeral Home** — Funeral director/embalmer license, state-specific requirements
- **Childcare** — State childcare license, often tied to individual + facility
- **Home Healthcare** — Medicare certification, state licenses (these DO transfer with entity sale)
- **Insurance** — Producer licenses (individual, not transferable)
- **Trucking** — MC authority, DOT registration (transfer with entity sale, not asset sale)

**Critical planning item:** For every license-critical industry, the buyer must either:
1. Hold the required license themselves, OR
2. Hire/retain a licensed individual who will stay post-close, OR
3. Structure the deal as an entity sale to preserve existing licenses

| Largest customer 10%–20% | Moderate risk | 5%–15% discount |
| Largest customer 20%–35% | High risk | 15%–30% discount |
| Largest customer >35% | Critical risk | 30%–50% discount or deal-breaker |

**Industries with inherently higher concentration (acceptable thresholds are higher):**
- Contract Manufacturing — up to 25% acceptable if contractually protected
- Staffing — up to 20% acceptable with long-term MSA
- 3PL / Logistics — up to 20% acceptable with multi-year contract
- Food Manufacturing — up to 30% acceptable for private label with major retailer

**Industries where concentration is especially deadly:**
- Insurance Agency — loss of one key account can be absorbed; loss of one carrier is devastating
- Commercial Cleaning — single contract loss eliminates 20%+ of revenue overnight
- Staffing — single client loss means immediate layoffs

# APPENDIX F: INDUSTRY-SPECIFIC DEAL STRUCTURE NORMS

## What "Normal" Looks Like by Industry — Yulia's Reference

### Home Services (HVAC, Plumbing, Electrical, Pest Control, Landscaping, Pool, Roofing)
**Typical deal structure for $1M–$3M SDE business:**
- Sale type: Asset sale (95%+ of deals)
- Cash at close: 70%–85%
- Seller note: 10%–20%, 3–5 year term, 5%–7% interest, subordinated
- Earnout: Rare except for high-growth businesses
- Transition period: 3–6 months (owner stays as consultant)
- Non-compete: 3–5 years, 25–75 mile radius (varies by metro density)
- Working capital: Normalized WC included at close, calculated as trailing 12-month average
- Training: 40–80 hours of structured training included
- Equipment: Included in asset sale at FMV or book value
- Vehicles: Included; leased vehicles may need lender consent
- Real estate: Usually excluded; if owner-occupied, negotiate lease-back at FMV
- Employee transfer: Offer letters to key employees, at-will in most states
- Customer notification: Seller introduces buyer to key customers within first 30 days
- Key risk allocation: Seller warrants no undisclosed liabilities pre-close

### Healthcare Practices (Dental, Veterinary, Optometry, PT/Chiro)
**Typical deal structure for solo-to-small practice:**
- Sale type: Asset sale (practice assets, patient records, goodwill)
- Cash at close: 65%–80%
- Seller note: 15%–25%, 3–7 year term, 5%–7% interest
- Earnout: Common in DSO/corporate deals — tied to post-close production
- Transition period: 6–18 months (seller stays to transition patients; critical)
- Non-compete: 3–5 years, 10–25 mile radius (tighter in urban, broader in rural)
- Working capital: A/R from insurance usually excluded (seller collects pre-close A/R)
- Patient records: Transfer governed by state privacy laws, patient notification required
- Equipment: Included, typically at depreciated book value
- Real estate: Usually excluded; lease assignment required
- Licenses: Buyer must hold required professional license before close
- Employee transfer: Key hygienists/techs critical; offer retention bonuses
- Insurance panels: Credentialing timeline (60–120 days) must be planned
- Controlled substances: DEA license transfer/new registration for buyer

**Typical deal structure for DSO/corporate acquisition:**
- Sale type: Asset or entity (depends on structure)
- Day-one payment: 60%–75% in cash
- Equity rollover: 20%–30% into parent entity (the "second bite")
- Earnout: 12–24 months, tied to seller's continued production at target level
- Employment agreement: 2–5 years, specified compensation, production requirements
- Management fee: DSO charges 15%–25% of collections for management services
- Capital improvements: DSO typically funds facility/equipment upgrades
- Governance: DSO controls business operations; seller controls clinical decisions

### Professional Services (CPA, Insurance, IT/MSP, Marketing, Staffing)
**Typical deal structure for $500K–$3M revenue practice:**
- Sale type: Asset sale or practice merger
- Cash at close: 50%–75% (lower than other industries due to client retention risk)
- Seller note: 20%–40%, often contingent on client retention
- Earnout/holdback: Very common — 10%–25% held back for 12–24 months based on retained revenue
- Transition period: 12–24 months (critical for warm client handoffs)
- Non-compete: 2–5 years, defined by geography and client solicitation
- Non-solicitation of clients: Universally required, often 3–5 years
- Non-solicitation of employees: Universally required, typically 2–3 years
- Client notification: Joint letters from seller and buyer, followed by personal introductions
- Key employee retention: Bonuses or equity participation to retain key producers
- Revenue guarantee: Seller may guarantee X% client retention at 12/24 months
- Technology transition: Migration timeline for client data, software, processes

### Manufacturing & Industrial (Contract Mfg, Food Mfg, Trucking, 3PL)
**Typical deal structure for $2M–$10M revenue business:**
- Sale type: Asset sale (environmental liability isolation) or entity sale (preserve contracts/certifications)
- Cash at close: 75%–90%
- Seller note: 10%–15%, 3–5 year term, secured by assets
- Earnout: Sometimes tied to customer retention or backlog execution
- Transition period: 3–12 months (depends on technical complexity)
- Non-compete: 3–5 years, often national scope for specialty capabilities
- Working capital: Detailed working capital peg with true-up mechanism
- Equipment: Included at FMV; independent equipment appraisal required for large deals
- Inventory: Included at lower of cost or market; physical count at close
- Real estate: Often included (purpose-built facilities); independent appraisal required
- Environmental: Phase I required; Phase II if concerns identified; environmental escrow if issues
- Certifications: Entity sale preserves ISO, AS9100, ITAR; asset sale requires re-certification
- Contracts: Customer consent may be required for assignment
- Permits: Environmental, zoning, operating permits must transfer or be re-obtained
- Key employee retention: Critical for machinists, plant managers, quality engineers

### Technology (SaaS, eCommerce)
**Typical deal structure for bootstrapped $1M–$5M ARR SaaS:**
- Sale type: Entity sale (stock/membership interest purchase)
- Cash at close: 70%–85%
- Seller note: 10%–20%, 2–3 year term
- Earnout: Common — tied to ARR retention, customer churn, or growth targets
- Transition period: 3–12 months (knowledge transfer, customer introductions)
- Non-compete: 3–5 years, typically broad (not geography-limited for software)
- IP assignment: Comprehensive — all source code, trademarks, domains, customer data
- Technology escrow: Source code escrow for large deals
- Reps and warranties: Extensive — IP ownership, no open source contamination, data privacy compliance, no security breaches
- Customer contracts: Review all subscription agreements, ToS, privacy policies
- Employee/contractor: IP assignment agreements from all developers, contractor agreements
- Hosting/infrastructure: Transfer of cloud accounts (AWS, GCP, Azure), DNS, SSL
- Data privacy: GDPR, CCPA compliance verification; DPA agreements with customers

**Typical deal structure for eCommerce brand:**
- Sale type: Asset sale (brand, inventory, supplier relationships, customer list)
- Cash at close: 60%–80%
- Earnout: 15%–30%, 12–18 months, tied to revenue or profitability
- Inventory: Purchased at cost; physical count or 3PL inventory report at close
- Supplier relationships: Key supplier introductions during transition
- Platform accounts: Amazon Seller Central, Shopify, etc. transfer or new account setup
- Marketing assets: Ad accounts, pixel data, email list, social media accounts
- Trademark: Assignment of all registered and common law trademarks

**Strategic guidance:**

- If you don't have industry expertise, buy a boring essential service business with proven systems

# APPENDIX K: INDUSTRY MULTIPLE DRIVERS — WHAT MOVES THE NEEDLE

## Understanding Why Two Identical-Revenue Businesses Get Different Multiples

The difference between a 2.5x and a 4.5x SDE multiple is NOT random. These are the specific, quantifiable factors Yulia evaluates:

### Universal Multiple Drivers (Apply to All Industries)

**Premium drivers (+0.5x to +1.5x to base multiple):**
1. **Recurring revenue >50%** — Monthly contracts, subscriptions, maintenance agreements
   - Each 10% increase in recurring revenue = approximately +0.2x to multiple
2. **Owner independence** — Owner works <20 hours/week, business runs without them
   - Full owner independence = +0.5x to +1.0x
3. **Revenue growth >15% annually** — Demonstrated trend over 3+ years
   - Each 5% growth above flat = approximately +0.15x to multiple
4. **Customer diversification** — No customer >5% of revenue
   - Moving from 20% concentration to 5% = +0.3x to +0.5x
5. **Clean financial documentation** — 3 years of accountant-prepared/audited statements
   - Clean books vs messy = +0.3x to +0.5x
6. **Stable, tenured workforce** — Key employees with 3+ years tenure
   - Proven team stability = +0.2x to +0.4x
7. **Strong systems and processes** — Documented SOPs, CRM, scheduling, quality control
   - Systematized business = +0.2x to +0.4x

**Discount drivers (-0.5x to -1.5x from base multiple):**
1. **Owner dependency** — Owner is primary producer/sales/manager = -0.5x to -1.5x
2. **Customer concentration** — Single customer >20% = -0.5x to -1.0x
3. **Revenue decline** — Declining revenue over past 2 years = -0.5x to -1.0x
4. **Poor documentation** — Tax returns only, no management financials = -0.3x to -0.5x
5. **Key employee risk** — Critical employees with no non-compete = -0.2x to -0.5x
6. **Deferred maintenance** — Equipment/facility requiring near-term capital = -0.3x to -0.5x
7. **Regulatory/compliance issues** — Open violations, pending actions = -0.5x to -1.0x

### Industry-Specific Multiple Accelerators

**Home Services (HVAC, Plumbing, Electrical, Pest Control):**
- PE roll-up premium: +1.0x to +2.0x if business qualifies as PE add-on
- Maintenance contract density: Each $100K in recurring contract revenue ≈ +0.1x
- Brand reputation (Google rating 4.5+): +0.2x to +0.3x
- Modern fleet/equipment (average age <3 years): +0.2x to +0.3x

**Healthcare Practices (Dental, Veterinary, Optometry):**
- Multi-provider production: Each associate generating $500K+ independent revenue ≈ +0.3x
- Specialty services: Adding orthodontics, oral surgery, specialty vet ≈ +0.3x to +0.5x
- DSO/corporate buyer premium: +1.0x to +3.0x over individual buyer pricing
- Modern facility/technology: +0.2x to +0.4x

**Professional Services (CPA, Insurance, IT/MSP):**
- Client retention >95%: +0.3x to +0.5x (especially insurance and CPA)
- MRR/ARR model maturity (MSP): Each 10% increase in MRR mix ≈ +0.2x
- Vertical specialization: Niche expertise ≈ +0.3x to +0.5x
- Technology platform: Cloud-first, automated ≈ +0.2x to +0.4x

**Technology (SaaS):**
- Net Revenue Retention >110%: +2.0x to +5.0x revenue multiple
- Gross margin >80%: +1.0x to +2.0x revenue multiple
- Rule of 40+ (growth rate + margin): Baseline for premium valuation
- Low logo churn (<5% annual): +1.0x to +3.0x revenue multiple

**Manufacturing:**
- Aerospace/defense certifications: +0.5x to +1.0x
- Reshoring beneficiary (domestic production advantage): +0.3x to +0.5x
- Owned real estate (purpose-built): +0.3x to +0.5x (plus real estate value)
- Technology/automation investment: +0.2x to +0.4x

### The "PE Arbitrage" — Why Multiples Jump at Scale

The most important concept in SMB M&A valuation:

| Business Size (Revenue) | Typical Buyer | Typical Multiple (SDE/EBITDA) |
|--------------------------|--------------|-------------------------------|
| $300K–$750K | Individual buyer | 2.0x–3.0x SDE |
| $750K–$2M | Individual/small PE | 2.5x–4.0x SDE |
| $2M–$5M | Small PE platform | 3.5x–5.0x SDE / 4.0x–6.0x EBITDA |
| $5M–$15M | PE platform | 5.0x–8.0x EBITDA |
| $15M–$50M | Large PE | 7.0x–12.0x EBITDA |
| $50M+ | Strategic/mega PE | 8.0x–15.0x+ EBITDA |

**The arbitrage opportunity:** Buy a $1M-revenue business at 3x SDE ($300K SDE = $900K purchase). Grow it to $3M revenue through organic growth and add-on acquisitions. Now it trades at 5x EBITDA ($600K EBITDA = $3M enterprise value). You created $2.1M in value primarily through size-driven multiple expansion.

This is the PE playbook. Yulia should help both sellers (understand what PE is willing to pay) and buyers (understand the arbitrage opportunity) navigate this dynamic.

### What the Silver Tsunami Means for Yulia's Advice

**For buyers:**
