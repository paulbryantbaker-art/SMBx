# FOR SELLERS

## Section 1: Pre-Market Positioning

### Setting the Right Asking Price
The asking price is the single most important strategic decision in the sell-side process. Get it wrong and you either leave money on the table or scare away every qualified buyer.

**The Goldilocks Zone:**
- **Too low:** You'll get fast offers but leave 15%–30% on the table. Buyers assume there's a problem.
- **Too high:** You'll sit on market for 6+ months. The listing goes "stale" and buyers assume there's a problem.
- **Right:** 10%–20% above your realistic minimum acceptable price. Creates room for negotiation without discouraging engagement.

**Pricing strategies by market condition:**
- **Strong seller's market (your industry is hot):** Price at the top of comparable range. Let buyers compete. You can always negotiate down from a strong anchor.
- **Balanced market:** Price at midpoint of comparable range with 10% cushion. Show flexibility in structure, not price.
- **Buyer's market (many sellers, few buyers):** Price competitively. Speed to close matters more than maximum price. Emphasize deal certainty over headline number.

**The "not for sale" premium:**
If a buyer approaches you unsolicited with "I want to buy your business," your leverage is maximum. You weren't looking to sell. The buyer has to create motivation. In this scenario:
- Never give a price first. Ask: "What makes you think my business would be interesting to you?"
- Let them anchor. Their first number tells you their ceiling.
- If their anchor is within 20% of your walk-away, engage. If not, say: "I appreciate the interest. That's not a number that would make it worth the disruption to my life and business. If your thinking evolves, you know where to find me."

### Building a Bulletproof Add-Back Schedule
Your SDE/EBITDA calculation IS your valuation. Every dollar of legitimate add-back increases your sale price by 2.5x–5x (depending on your multiple). This is the highest-ROI activity in the entire sell-side process.

**The add-back hierarchy (most defensible to least):**
1. **Tier 1 — Unquestionable:** Owner salary above market replacement cost, owner benefits (health, retirement, vehicle), depreciation/amortization (non-cash), interest expense, one-time legal/accounting fees with documentation
2. **Tier 2 — Strong with documentation:** Family members on payroll not working full capacity, personal travel/entertainment clearly separated, above-market rent to self (with independent appraisal)
3. **Tier 3 — Requires narrative:** Discretionary R&D or marketing experiments, non-recurring repairs/upgrades, charitable contributions, community sponsorships
4. **Tier 4 — Aggressive (expect pushback):** Informal personal expenses through business, "consulting fees" to family, below-market pricing to friends/family customers

**Yulia's rule:** Present Tier 1 and 2 confidently. Present Tier 3 transparently with context. Never present Tier 4 — clean it up before going to market.

### Creating a Competitive Process
A competitive process is the single most effective way to maximize sale price. Even the perception of competition changes buyer behavior.

**How to create real competition:**
1. Engage 3–5 qualified buyers simultaneously (not sequentially)
2. Set a clear timeline: "We are accepting IOIs through [date]"
3. Provide consistent information to all parties (data room, CIM)
4. Never reveal specific terms from other parties
5. Let buyers know that others are in process: "We are in conversations with multiple interested parties"

**What you CAN say:**
- "We've received interest from multiple parties"
- "We are evaluating proposals that reflect the full value of the business"
- "Our timeline for accepting IOIs is [date]"
- "We are looking for a buyer who represents the best combination of price, terms, and cultural fit"

**What you CANNOT say (legal/ethical exposure):**
- Specific dollar amounts from other offers (creates auction liability)
- Names of other buyers
- Terms of other IOIs/LOIs
- Fabricated interest that doesn't exist

### Handling Unsolicited "I Want to Buy Your Business" Approaches
This happens frequently, especially in PE-consolidating industries. How to respond:

**If you're not ready to sell:**
"I'm flattered by the interest. The business isn't on the market, and I'm not actively exploring a sale. That said, I'm always open to understanding what a partnership or transaction might look like if the fit is right. Why don't you tell me what attracted you to my business?"

**If you ARE considering selling:**
"I appreciate you reaching out. As you can imagine, I get these inquiries from time to time. I'd be happy to have an exploratory conversation. Before we go further, I'd want to understand: What's your acquisition experience? How are you financing? What's your timeline? I'd also need you to sign an NDA before we discuss any business specifics."

**Red flags in unsolicited approaches:**
- Buyer asks for financials before signing NDA
- Buyer can't articulate financing plan
- Buyer is a broker fishing for listings, not an actual buyer
- Buyer pressures for exclusivity before making an offer

## Section 2: IOI Evaluation Framework

### The Five Dimensions of IOI Evaluation

Every IOI should be scored across five dimensions. Price is important but is only one of five:

**Dimension 1: Price (Weight: 30%)**
- Is the headline number within your realistic range?
- Is it based on reasonable assumptions about your financials?
- Does the buyer's valuation methodology make sense (multiple of what)?
- Are there deductions or adjustments buried in the IOI?

**Dimension 2: Terms and Structure (Weight: 25%)**
- Cash at close percentage — how much do you actually receive on day one?
- Seller note terms — amount, interest rate, term, subordination, conditions for payment
- Earnout — size, metrics, measurement period, dispute resolution
- Working capital treatment — is there a peg? Who absorbs the risk?

**Dimension 3: Buyer Quality (Weight: 20%)**
- Can they actually close? Financing confirmed or contingent?
- Track record — have they closed similar deals before?
- Cultural fit — will your employees and customers be treated well?
- Management plan — do they plan to operate or flip?

**Dimension 4: Certainty of Close (Weight: 15%)**
- Financing status — approved, pre-qualified, or aspirational?
- DD requirements — reasonable or exhaustive/invasive?
- Contingencies — how many outs does the buyer have?
- Timeline — realistic or aggressive/impossible?

**Dimension 5: Post-Close Terms (Weight: 10%)**
- Transition period — length, compensation, role clarity
- Non-compete scope — geographic and temporal boundaries
- Employment agreement (if applicable) — terms, compensation, termination provisions
- Representations and warranties — scope and survival period

### The Effective Net Proceeds Formula

Don't evaluate an offer by headline price alone. Calculate what you actually receive:

```
Effective Net Proceeds =
  Cash at Close × (1 - applicable tax rate)
  + NPV of Seller Note (discounted at 8% risk rate)
  + Expected Value of Earnout (probability-weighted)
  - Value of Your Transition Time (opportunity cost)
  - Estimated Closing Costs (legal, accounting, broker)
```

**Example:**
- Headline price: $2.0M
- Cash at close: $1.5M (75%)
- Seller note: $300K (15%) at 5% over 5 years
- Earnout: $200K (10%) if revenue maintained for 12 months
- Your tax rate: 20% (long-term capital gains)
- Transition: 6 months at $10K/month (your time value)
- Closing costs: $80K

```
Cash: $1,500,000 × 0.80 = $1,200,000
Note NPV: $300,000 × 0.85 (risk-adjusted) = $255,000
Earnout EV: $200,000 × 0.70 (70% probability) = $140,000
Transition: -$60,000
Closing: -$80,000
NET: $1,455,000
```

vs. a $1.85M all-cash offer:
```
Cash: $1,850,000 × 0.80 = $1,480,000
Transition: -$30,000 (3 months)
Closing: -$70,000
NET: $1,380,000
```

The $2.0M offer with structure actually nets MORE than the $1.85M "all-cash" offer. But the all-cash offer has LESS RISK. Yulia should present both scenarios.

### Buyer Quality Signals — What to Look For

**Strong buyer signals:**
- Financing pre-approved (SBA commitment letter or PE fund capital call)
- Has closed 2+ similar acquisitions before
- Asks sophisticated questions about operations, not just financials
- Willing to sign NDA promptly and provide proof of funds
- References from sellers of previous acquisitions
- Has advisors (attorney, accountant) already engaged

**Weak buyer signals:**
- "I'm exploring options" with no specific financing plan
- First-time buyer with no advisor team assembled
- Asks for exclusivity before making a formal offer
- Won't provide proof of funds or financing confirmation
- Makes verbal offers without putting anything in writing
- Renegotiates price before DD even begins

### Red Flags Predicting Deal Failure

Based on patterns in closed and failed deals, these IOI characteristics predict failure:

1. **Financing contingency with no pre-approval** — 60%+ failure rate
2. **Earnout >30% of total deal value** — signals buyer doesn't believe in the valuation
3. **Excessive DD timeline (>120 days)** — buyer is window-shopping
4. **No specific management plan** — buyer hasn't thought beyond the purchase
5. **Price at or below your floor** — no room for the inevitable re-trade attempt
6. **Buyer has never closed a deal** — 40%+ failure rate for first-time buyers without advisors

### Counter-Offer Language by Gap Size

**Gap <5% ($50K on a $1M deal):**
"Your valuation is in the range we anticipated. We'd like to close the gap on a few structural terms — specifically [working capital / transition period / non-compete scope]. If we can align on those, I think we have a deal."

**Gap 5%–15% ($75K–$150K on a $1M deal):**
"We appreciate the offer. Your number is below where we believe the market supports based on [comparable transactions / our financial performance / competing interest]. We'd be willing to meet in the range of $[your number] if you can strengthen the terms — specifically [more cash at close / shorter earnout / reduced non-compete]."

**Gap 15%–25% ($150K–$250K on a $1M deal):**
"Thank you for the IOI. We see significant value that your number doesn't capture — specifically [recurring revenue value / growth trajectory / market position]. We'd need to see $[your number] to move forward, but we're flexible on structure. Can you walk us through the assumptions behind your valuation?"

**Gap >25%:**
"We appreciate your interest in the business. The gap between your indication and our expectations is substantial. Before we go further, can you help me understand how you arrived at your number? I want to make sure we're working from the same set of facts, because the comparable transactions we've seen support a significantly higher valuation."

## Section 3: Running a Competitive Process

### Notifying Buyers of Competition Without Legal Exposure

**Safe language:**
- "We are evaluating multiple expressions of interest"
- "There is active buyer interest in the business"
- "We've set a deadline of [date] for final proposals"
- "We're looking for the proposal that best balances price, terms, and transition fit"

**Dangerous language (avoid):**
- "We have an offer for $X" (disclosing specific terms)
- "Another buyer has offered more" (creates pressure that may be false)
- "We'll share your offer with other buyers" (auction liability)
- "You need to beat $X to stay in the process" (price shopping)

### BAFO Process (Best and Final Offer)

**When to call BAFO:**
- You have 2+ serious offers (signed IOIs) within 15% of each other
- At least 2 buyers have confirmed financing
- You're ready to move to LOI with the winner

**How to run BAFO:**
1. Notify all parties simultaneously: "We are entering a best-and-final phase. Please submit your final proposal by [date, typically 5–7 business days]. This should represent your strongest offer on both price and terms."
2. Provide identical instructions to all parties
3. Do NOT share other offers or give hints about competitive positioning
4. Evaluate all BAFO submissions simultaneously using the 5-dimension framework
5. Select winner and notify all parties simultaneously

**BAFO evaluation priorities:**
1. Certainty of close (can they actually do this?)
2. Effective net proceeds (not headline price)
3. Cultural fit and business continuity
4. Transition terms (your quality of life for 6–18 months)
5. Post-close obligations (non-compete, reps & warranties)

### Advance/Eliminate Decision Framework

After receiving IOIs, Yulia should help the seller categorize each buyer:

**Advance** if:
- Price within 10% of your target
- Financing confirmed or highly probable
- Buyer has relevant experience
- Terms are workable (even if not perfect)
- Cultural fit is acceptable

**Hold** if:
- Price is borderline (within 20% but needs improvement)
- Financing is probable but not confirmed
- Buyer shows genuine interest but needs more information
- Could be a strong backup if primary deal falls through

**Eliminate** if:
- Price is >25% below your target with no flexibility
- Financing is speculative or contingent on factors outside buyer's control
- Buyer is clearly unqualified (can't provide proof of funds)
- Red flags: history of failed deals, unrealistic expectations, bad reputation

### Managing Buyer Relationships During the Process

**The best buyer is sometimes the slowest buyer.** How to handle:
- Set firm deadlines and enforce them: "We need your response by [date] or we'll proceed with other parties"
- Offer reasonable accommodations: "We can extend by 3 business days, but we need a commitment to the revised timeline"
- If they're the strongest buyer, give them one extension. But only one.
- A buyer who can't meet deadlines during courtship won't improve during DD or closing

## Section 4: LOI Negotiation

### Every Material LOI Term — Yulia's Default Position

| Term | Seller's Default | Notes |
|------|-----------------|-------|
| Purchase price | Firm, not subject to adjustment except WC | Price should be locked; don't leave "subject to valuation" |
| Structure | Asset sale (tax flexibility for seller) | Stock sale may be required for certain assets (licenses, contracts) |
| Cash at close | ≥75% of total consideration | Push for 80%+ if possible |
| Seller note | ≤15% of total, 3–5 year term, 5%–7% interest | Resist subordination to acquisition debt if possible |
| Earnout | ≤10% of total, based on revenue (not EBITDA) | Revenue is harder for buyer to manipulate than EBITDA |
| Working capital | Normalized WC target, trailing 12-month average | Include clear definition of current assets and current liabilities |
| Exclusivity | 45 days maximum | Resist 60+ day exclusivity — it removes your leverage |
| DD scope | Financial, legal, operational only | Resist "unlimited access" — define scope in LOI |
| Break-up fee | $0 from seller to buyer | You should NOT pay buyer's costs if deal fails |
| Reverse break-up fee | Negotiate if possible | Buyer pays you $25K–$50K if they walk without cause after LOI |
| Non-compete | 3 years, reasonable geography | Push for narrower; resist 5+ years or nationwide |
| Transition | 3–6 months, compensated at fair rate | Not free. Your time has value. |
| Reps & warranties | Standard, 12-month survival | Resist "fundamental" reps surviving longer than 18 months |
| Indemnification cap | ≤ purchase price, ideally ≤50% | Resist unlimited indemnification — your exposure should be bounded |
| Closing conditions | Standard (regulatory, financing, no MAC) | Resist broad "MAC" (Material Adverse Change) definitions |

### Terms Sellers Must Hold Firm On

1. **Price floor** — Know your walk-away number and don't go below it
2. **Cash at close ≥70%** — Anything lower and you're financing the buyer's deal
3. **Exclusivity ≤60 days** — Beyond that, the buyer has no urgency
4. **Defined DD scope** — "Full access to everything" is an invitation for re-trading
5. **Non-compete reasonableness** — 5 years, 100-mile radius is extreme for most SMBs

### Terms Sellers Should Be Willing to Concede

1. **Working capital true-up mechanism** — This is standard and fair
2. **Representations about known liabilities** — If you know about a liability, disclose it
3. **Transition period within reason** — 3–6 months is fair; push back on 12+ months
4. **Standard indemnification** — 12-month survival, capped at purchase price
5. **Reasonable inspection contingencies** — Environmental Phase I, lease assignment

## Section 5: During Due Diligence — Seller's Guide

### What to Provide and When

**Provide immediately upon request (standard DD items):**
- 3 years of tax returns and financial statements
- Current-year P&L and balance sheet
- Customer list with revenue by customer (without contact details initially)
- Employee census (roles, tenure, compensation — without names initially)
- Equipment list with age and condition
- Lease agreements and material contracts
- Insurance policies and claims history

**Provide after initial DD confirms serious intent (sensitive items):**
- Customer contact information
- Employee names and detailed compensation
- Supplier agreements with pricing
- Proprietary processes and trade secrets
- Pending litigation details
- Environmental reports

**Never provide (protect yourself):**
- Personal tax information beyond business schedules
- Bank account access
- Customer access (until after LOI and well into DD)
- Employee notification (until closing is certain)

### Handling Uncomfortable DD Questions

**"Your revenue declined 15% in 2023. Why?"**
Don't hide it. Explain it factually: "We lost our largest customer in March 2023 when they were acquired. We've since diversified — our largest customer is now 8% of revenue, and we've grown 20% year-over-year since then."

**"Your owner's compensation seems high."**
"I'm the CEO, COO, and primary sales executive. Replacing me would require at minimum a GM at $X and a sales director at $Y. My compensation reflects the total value of those roles."

**"These add-backs seem aggressive."**
"Every add-back is documented with supporting evidence in the data room. I'm happy to walk through any specific item. Our accountant has prepared a reconciliation from tax returns to adjusted SDE."

**"Can we talk to your customers?"**
"I'm open to customer references, but only after we have a signed LOI and are well into DD. My customer relationships are my most valuable asset and I need to protect them until we have a high-certainty deal."

### Legitimate vs. Bad Faith Re-Trade Requests

**Legitimate (accept or negotiate):**
- "We found $50K in unrecorded warranty obligations — we'd like to adjust the price by $50K"
- "The equipment appraisal shows $75K less value than represented — can we discuss?"
- "The lease assignment requires a $30K deposit — who covers this?"

**Bad faith (push back firmly):**
- "Now that we've spent 60 days in DD, we think the business is only worth 20% less than our IOI"
- "We need to reduce the price because interest rates went up since our IOI"
- "We've decided the industry multiples should be lower"
- "We want to change from asset sale to stock sale" (shifting tax burden to you)

### Response Language for Re-Trade Attempts

**For legitimate findings:**
"We understand you've identified [specific issue]. Let's quantify the actual financial impact and discuss how to address it — whether through a price adjustment, escrow, or representation. We want to get this done, and we're willing to work through legitimate findings constructively."

**For bad faith re-trades:**
"We entered this LOI in good faith based on mutually agreed terms. The information available to you today was substantially the same information available when you made your offer. A [20%] reduction based on [non-specific concerns / macro conditions] isn't a negotiation — it's a new offer. If you'd like to proceed at the agreed terms, we're ready to close. If your offer has fundamentally changed, we need to understand that now so we can evaluate our options."

**The nuclear option (when they know you have alternatives):**
"We have other interested parties who we put on hold when we entered exclusivity with you. If your indication has changed materially from the LOI, I need to let them know we're available for conversations. I'd rather close with you, but not at terms that don't reflect the value of this business."

### When to Extend vs. Walk from Exclusivity

**Extend if:**
- Buyer has legitimate DD complexity (environmental, regulatory) requiring time
- Financing is in progress with clear timeline from lender
- Buyer is communicating proactively about progress
- All other deal terms remain agreed

**Walk if:**
- Buyer has gone silent for 10+ business days
- Buyer has re-traded without legitimate DD findings
- Financing has been denied or is clearly unlikely
- Buyer is using exclusivity to prevent you from talking to others while they shop

# FOR BUYERS

## Section 6: Thesis Development and Target Evaluation

### Speed to Conviction — The 30-Minute First Call Framework

You can evaluate 80% of a deal's viability in the first 30-minute call with the seller or broker. Here's what to learn:

**Minutes 1–5: Motivation**
- "What's prompting you to consider a sale?" (retirement, burnout, health, opportunity cost)
- "What's your ideal timeline for a transition?"
- "Have you been in conversations with other buyers?"

**Minutes 5–15: Business Overview**
- Revenue, trend, customer count
- How many employees, key roles, tenure
- Owner's role — hours/week, specific functions
- What makes this business defensible (moat, competitive advantage)

**Minutes 15–25: Financials**
- SDE or EBITDA — rough number is fine
- Revenue split (recurring vs project, top customers)
- Any known add-backs
- Asking price or expectations

**Minutes 25–30: Red Flag Triage**
- Any pending lawsuits?
- Any customer concentration >20%?
- Any key person besides the owner?
- Reason for any revenue decline?
- Environmental issues?

**After the call, you should know:**
- Pursue (schedule follow-up, request CIM)
- Pass (politely decline)
- Investigate (need one more data point before deciding)

### Red Flag Triage — Before Spending 45 Days in DD

Before committing to an LOI, do fast triage on these deal-killers:

| Red Flag | Investigation Time | Kill Threshold |
|----------|-------------------|----------------|
| Revenue declining 2+ years | 5 minutes (review financials) | >15% decline with no turnaround |
| Single customer >30% | 5 minutes (ask seller) | Single customer >40%, no contract |
| Owner does everything | 10 minutes (ask about team) | Zero employees in management roles |
| Environmental risk | 30 minutes (check industry, history) | Former gas station, dry cleaner, manufacturing with chemicals |
| License concentration | 10 minutes (ask about licenses) | Owner holds only license, no backup |
| Litigation pending | 10 minutes (ask directly) | Unresolved material litigation |

## Section 7: IOI Strategy

### When You Know Enough to Make an IOI

You have enough information when you can answer these questions:
1. What is the business worth? (Your independent valuation, not the asking price)
2. Can you finance the acquisition? (SBA, conventional, equity)
3. Can you operate this business? (skills, time, team)
4. What's your walk-away number? (Maximum you'll pay under any terms)
5. What's your management plan? (Who runs it day 1 post-close)

### Building a Winning IOI on Quality, Not Just Price

Sellers don't always choose the highest price. They choose the buyer they trust most to close and treat their business well. Your IOI should communicate:

**Your story:** "I'm [background]. I've spent [X years] in [relevant industry/role]. I'm looking to acquire and operate a business like yours because [genuine reason]."

**Your financing:** "I have $[X] in equity confirmed, and I have [SBA pre-approval / PE backing / conventional lending commitment] for the balance."

**Your management plan:** "My plan post-close is to [retain all employees / invest in growth / maintain your reputation / continue your legacy]. I plan to be [hands-on operator / hire GM / maintain current management]."

**Your timeline:** "I'm prepared to move quickly. I can complete DD within [45–60 days] and am targeting a close within [90 days]."

### Price Anchoring in Competitive Processes

**If you're bidding against others:**
- Your first number sets the floor, not the ceiling — don't lowball
- Show financing certainty: "Our offer is not contingent on financing" (if true) is the most powerful sentence in an IOI
- Add a personal letter to the seller explaining why you're the right buyer
- Offer a shorter DD period (30–45 days vs standard 60–90) if you can execute

## Section 8: LOI Strategy

### LOI as a Strategic Document

Your LOI should protect you while keeping the seller comfortable:

**Protective provisions (must have):**
- Financing contingency (if SBA, include timeline)
- Standard DD out — right to terminate if DD reveals material misrepresentation
- Working capital true-up mechanism
- Material Adverse Change (MAC) clause
- Landlord consent / lease assignment contingency

**Seller-friendly provisions (builds trust):**
- Exclusivity limited to 45–60 days (not 90+)
- Clear DD request list (not open-ended fishing)
- Deposit in escrow (shows seriousness — typically $10K–$25K)
- Transition terms that compensate the seller fairly

### Working Capital Peg — The Most Commonly Misunderstood Term

Working capital (WC) = Current Assets – Current Liabilities, normalized for the business.

**Why it matters:** If the seller drains working capital before close (collects all A/R, delays paying suppliers), you inherit a cash-starved business.

**How to handle it:**
1. Calculate trailing 12-month average NWC from monthly balance sheets
2. Set this as the "target" in the LOI
3. Include a true-up mechanism: if actual WC at close is above/below target, purchase price adjusts dollar-for-dollar
4. Define exactly which accounts are included/excluded (exclude cash, long-term debt)

## Section 9: Due Diligence — Buyer's Master Framework

### Walk-Away Triggers — Set BEFORE DD

Before you start DD, decide what findings would kill the deal. Write them down. Don't change them mid-process.

**Automatic walk-aways (non-negotiable):**
- Seller fraud or material misrepresentation in financial statements
- Environmental contamination without remediation plan
- Undisclosed material litigation with potential exposure >10% of purchase price
- Key license held solely by departing owner with no replacement plan
- Revenue decline >20% during DD period (business deteriorating in real-time)

**Price-adjustment triggers (negotiate, don't walk):**
- Customer concentration higher than represented
- Equipment condition worse than represented (quantify replacement cost)
- Working capital below target
- Undisclosed liabilities with quantifiable impact
- Employee compensation above market (future cost pressure)

### Three-Tier Finding System

Every DD finding should be categorized:

**Tier 1: Deal-Killer** — Issues that make the business uninvestable regardless of price
- Active fraud
- Environmental contamination without remediation
- Regulatory violations threatening operating license
- Revenue collapse with no turnaround path

**Tier 2: Price Lever** — Issues that reduce value and justify price adjustment
- Customer concentration (quantify lost-customer scenario)
- Equipment replacement needs (get vendor quotes)
- Below-market lease with expiration approaching
- Undisclosed liabilities (quantify exposure)
- Owner dependency greater than represented (quantify transition risk)

**Tier 3: Accepted Risk / Noise** — Issues you acknowledge but don't let derail the deal
- Minor regulatory items easily remedied
- Employee turnover within industry norms
- Small customer losses in normal course
- Market conditions affecting all competitors equally
- Cosmetic facility issues with modest repair costs

### The Re-Trade Conversation — When and How

**When to re-trade (legitimate):**
- DD reveals material facts that contradict representations
- You can quantify the specific dollar impact of findings
- The findings are things the seller knew or should have known
- You're willing to close if the adjustment is made

**How to re-trade without destroying the relationship:**
"Our DD has identified [specific finding]. Based on [quantified analysis], this represents approximately $[X] in additional risk/cost that wasn't reflected in our original offer. We'd like to discuss adjusting the purchase price by $[Y] to reflect this finding. We remain committed to closing this transaction and believe this adjustment is fair based on the facts."

**How NOT to re-trade:**
- "We've been thinking about it and want to reduce our offer by 20%"
- "Our lender says we need to lower the price"
- "We think the market has changed since our offer"

### The Professional Walk-Away

Sometimes the right answer is to walk. Here's how:

"After completing our due diligence, we've concluded that this opportunity isn't the right fit for us at this time. Specifically, [brief, honest reason — e.g., 'the customer concentration risk exceeds our comfort level' or 'the capital expenditure requirements are beyond our current capacity']. We appreciate your time and transparency through this process. We wish you the best in finding the right buyer."

**Why professional walk-aways matter:**
- The M&A community is small — your reputation follows you
- Sellers talk to other sellers — burning one seller burns future opportunities
- The deal might come back to you if the next buyer fails

## Section 10: BAFO Tactics

### Differentiating Beyond Price in Competitive BAFO

When you're in a BAFO against other buyers, price alone rarely wins. Differentiate on:

1. **Certainty of close:** "Our financing is fully committed. We can close in 30 days."
2. **Cultural fit:** "I've met your team and I'm committed to retaining them all."
3. **Transition flexibility:** "I'm willing to keep you on as a consultant for as long as you'd like."
4. **Speed:** "We can start DD tomorrow. Our advisors are standing by."
5. **Clean structure:** "Our offer is simple: 85% cash at close, 15% seller note. No earnout. No complexity."

### Certainty of Close as Currency

Sellers fear deal failure more than anything. After months of process, the worst outcome is starting over. If you can offer certainty, it's worth money:

- **Financing committed (not contingent)** = worth 5%–10% price discount
- **No due diligence out (or limited out)** = worth 3%–5% price discount
- **Compressed timeline (30 vs 90 days)** = worth 2%–5% price discount

This means you can potentially "win" at a lower price if your certainty package is strong enough.

# FOR BOTH SIDES

## Section 11: Common Deal Patterns

### Pattern 1: Buyer Underbids → Seller Counters → Meet in the Middle
**Frequency:** 70% of completed deals
**How it plays out:** Buyer IOIs 10%–20% below asking. Seller counters 5%–10% above IOI. After 1–2 rounds, they meet within 5% of the midpoint. Seller gets structural concessions (more cash at close, shorter earnout) in exchange for price flexibility.

**Yulia's advice:** This is the normal, healthy pattern. If you're the seller, don't be offended by a reasonable underbid. If you're the buyer, don't lowball so aggressively that the seller disengages.

### Pattern 2: Financing Falls Through — Survival Structuring
**Frequency:** 15%–20% of deals face financing issues
**How it plays out:** Buyer's SBA loan gets denied or reduced after LOI. Deal seems dead.

**Survival options:**
- Seller increases seller note to bridge the financing gap (most common)
- Buyer brings in equity partner (friend, family, co-investor)
- Deal restructured as installment sale (tax benefits for seller)
- Smaller deal scope (exclude real estate, carve out division)

### Pattern 3: Key Employee Revelation During DD
**Frequency:** 10%–15% of deals
**What happens:** DD reveals that one employee is critical to operations, and they haven't been told about the sale, or they have a history of departing during ownership transitions.

**Response:** Buyer requests retention agreement with bonus (typically 6–12 months salary, payable at 6/12 month mark post-close). Cost is split between buyer and seller (seller bears 50%+ as it's their employee's retention risk).

### Pattern 4: Customer Concentration Discovery
**Frequency:** 20%–25% of deals
**What happens:** Top customer is 25%–35% of revenue (higher than represented or initially understood).

**Response:**
- Quantify the exposure: If top customer leaves, what happens to DSCR and profitability?
- Request customer interview/reference (with seller introduction)
- Price adjustment: typically 0.3x–0.5x multiple reduction for 25%–35% concentration
- Consider holdback: 10%–15% of price held in escrow for 12 months contingent on customer retention

### Pattern 5: The Re-Trade War
**Frequency:** 5%–10% of deals (but disproportionately memorable)
**What happens:** Buyer finds issue in DD, requests significant price reduction. Seller refuses. Buyer threatens to walk. Seller calls bluff (or doesn't).

**Resolution paths:**
1. Buyer walks (and should, if the finding is material)
2. Compromise: adjust price by agreed amount, add escrow or holdback
3. Split the difference: seller reduces price 50% of requested amount
4. Creative solution: issue addressed through rep & warranty, insurance, or structural change

### Pattern 6: The Two-Buyer Standoff
**Frequency:** 5%–8% of deals
**What happens:** Two strong buyers with similar offers. Seller can't decide. Process stalls.

**Resolution:**
- Set a firm BAFO deadline (5 business days)
- Evaluate on 5 dimensions (not just price)
- If still tied: choose the buyer with higher certainty of close
- If STILL tied: choose the buyer whose values align best with your legacy goals

## Section 12: Language Bank — Exact Phrases That Work

### Opening Negotiation
- "We've reviewed the opportunity carefully and believe there's a strong fit. Here's our thinking on value and structure."
- "Based on our analysis of comparable transactions and the business's financial profile, we believe a fair range is $X to $Y."
- "We're prepared to move quickly and have our financing [committed/in process]. What timeline works for you?"

### Responding to a Low-Ball Offer
- "I appreciate the offer. The gap between your number and where comparable businesses are trading is significant. Can you walk me through how you arrived at your valuation?"
- "That number doesn't reflect the value that [specific differentiators] bring to this business. I think we're looking at different comparable sets."
- "I'm interested in understanding your thinking. At that price point, I'd want to explore what structural improvements might bridge the gap."

### Presenting a DD Finding Without Attacking
- "During our review, we identified [specific finding] that wasn't reflected in the original financial presentation. We'd like to understand the context."
- "Our analysis shows [specific data point] that differs from what was represented. We don't think this is intentional, but we need to reconcile the numbers."
- "This finding represents approximately $X in additional risk that we'd like to address through [price adjustment / escrow / representation]."

### Requesting a Timeline Extension
- "We're progressing well through DD but need an additional [X days] to complete [specific item]. We remain fully committed to the deal."
- "Our lender requires [specific document] which has a [X-day] processing time. Can we extend exclusivity by [X days] to accommodate this?"

### Walking Away Without Burning Bridges
- "After careful consideration, we've concluded this isn't the right fit for us at this time. We genuinely appreciate your openness and professionalism throughout this process."
- "The risk profile of this particular opportunity exceeds our investment criteria. We wish you the best and would welcome the chance to explore future opportunities."

### Accepting Terms With Dignity
- "We've reviewed your final offer and we're prepared to accept at those terms. Let's get the purchase agreement drafted and move toward closing."
- "While this isn't exactly where we started, we believe the deal as structured is fair and we're committed to making it work."

### Delivering Bad Financing News
- "Our lender has come back with a commitment that's [$X] below what we anticipated. We have several options to bridge the gap and would like to discuss them with you."
- "The SBA process has identified [specific issue] that affects our loan amount. We're exploring alternatives including [seller note increase / equity partner / deal restructure] and would like your input."

### Asking for Seller Note Accommodation
- "Given the current lending environment, we'd like to discuss adjusting the seller note terms. Specifically, [longer term / interest-only period / reduced amount]. This improves our DSCR and actually increases the probability of your note being repaid in full."

### Declining a BAFO
- "After careful analysis, we've decided not to submit a revised offer. We believe our current proposal fairly reflects the value, and stretching beyond it wouldn't be responsible for our investors/partners."

### Closing When Both Sides Are Tired
- "We've been through a thorough process. I think we're both tired of negotiating. Here's what I propose: [final clean offer]. This is a fair deal that works for both of us. Let's close and start building the future of this business."

### Responding to Earnout Proposals
- "We're open to an earnout component, but only if the metrics are within our control and objectively measurable. Revenue-based earnouts work for us; EBITDA-based earnouts don't, because the buyer controls expenses."
- "We'd want the earnout measured on gross revenue with clear accounting methodology agreed in advance, an independent third-party review mechanism, and quarterly payment against annual targets."

### Handling Non-Compete Negotiations
- "I'm comfortable with a reasonable non-compete — 3 years within the metro area where the business operates. I'm not comfortable with a nationwide 5-year restriction that prevents me from earning a living in my industry."
- "The non-compete scope should be proportional to the deal value. For a business this size, the standard in our industry is [X years / Y mile radius]."

### Addressing Working Capital Disputes
- "Let's agree on the methodology now, not at closing. Trailing 12-month average NWC, with defined included/excluded accounts, and a true-up within 60 days of close. Both sides' accountants should agree on the calculation method before we sign the LOI."
- "Working capital is the business's operating fuel. It's not bonus cash for the seller to sweep or an opportunity for the buyer to underfund the business. Let's set a fair target and stick to it."

### Negotiating Transition Period Terms
- "I'm happy to stay for 6 months to ensure a smooth transition. My rate is $[X]/month, consistent with what you'd pay a GM-level consultant. After 6 months, I'm available by phone for another 6 months at no charge."
- "The transition is the most important part of this deal for your success. Let's structure it properly: 3 months full-time, 3 months half-time, 6 months phone availability. This gives you 12 months of support with a natural wind-down."

# APPENDIX A: NEGOTIATION PSYCHOLOGY — UNDERSTANDING THE OTHER SIDE

## The Seller's Emotional Journey

Understanding where the seller is emotionally changes how you negotiate:

### Stage 1: "Testing the Water" (Pre-Market)
**Emotional state:** Curious but not committed. May be exploring value out of curiosity or early retirement thinking.
**What they need:** Validation that their business is valuable and the market is receptive.
**Yulia's approach:** Affirm value, present realistic range, discuss preparation steps without pressure.

### Stage 2: "Getting Serious" (Market Preparation)
**Emotional state:** Committed but anxious. Worried about confidentiality, employee reactions, customer reactions.
**What they need:** Confidence that the process protects them. Control over information flow.
**Yulia's approach:** Structured process, clear confidentiality protocols, emphasize that they control the timeline.

### Stage 3: "The Thrill of Interest" (Receiving IOIs)
**Emotional state:** Validated, excited, sometimes overwhelmed. Tendency to anchor to highest number without evaluating structure.
**What they need:** Objective evaluation framework. Reminder that headline price ≠ net proceeds.
**Yulia's approach:** Run the effective net proceeds calculation. Score each IOI on 5 dimensions. Keep them grounded.

### Stage 4: "The Grind" (Due Diligence)
**Emotional state:** Frustrated, exposed, vulnerable. Every DD question feels like an attack on their life's work.
**What they need:** Perspective. Reminder that DD is normal, not personal. Support in organizing responses.
**Yulia's approach:** Normalize the process. Help them prepare organized DD responses. Flag when pushback is warranted vs when it's standard.

### Stage 5: "Seller's Remorse" (Pre-Closing)
**Emotional state:** Second thoughts. "Am I making a mistake?" "What will I do after?" "Is the price enough?"
**What they need:** Affirmation that they've negotiated a fair deal. Reminder of WHY they decided to sell.
**Yulia's approach:** Reinforce the reasons. Review the effective net proceeds. Discuss post-close life planning. DO NOT push — let them process.

### Stage 6: "Relief and Grief" (Post-Closing)
**Emotional state:** A mixture of relief (it's done), excitement (new chapter), and grief (loss of identity).
**What they need:** Acknowledgment that this is a major life transition. Permission to feel conflicted.
**Yulia's approach:** Celebrate the achievement. Provide referrals to financial advisors, tax planners, and wealth managers. Check in at 30/90 days post-close.

## The Buyer's Emotional Journey

### Stage 1: "Deal Fever" (Search Phase)
**Risk:** Falling in love with the first business they see. Over-paying out of excitement.
**Yulia's approach:** Force discipline. Run comparable analysis. Set walk-away number BEFORE making offer.

### Stage 2: "Analysis Paralysis" (Evaluation Phase)
**Risk:** Analyzing so many opportunities that they never make an offer. Perfect is the enemy of done.
**Yulia's approach:** Set decision criteria. Force ranking. Limit active evaluation to 3 targets simultaneously.

### Stage 3: "Buyer's Anxiety" (Post-LOI)
**Risk:** Panic during DD. Finding issues (which always exist) and wanting to walk from an otherwise good deal.
**Yulia's approach:** Remind them that every business has issues. Use the three-tier finding system. Focus on quantifiable impact, not emotional reaction.

### Stage 4: "The Re-Trade Temptation" (Late DD)
**Risk:** Using DD findings as excuse to renegotiate a deal they should have walked from earlier — or re-trading a perfectly good deal because they can.
**Yulia's approach:** Only re-trade if findings are material and quantifiable. If you wouldn't have done the deal at this price knowing what you know now, walk. Don't re-trade just because you have leverage.

### Stage 5: "Closing Jitters" (Pre-Close)
**Risk:** Cold feet. "What if I'm wrong?" "What if the business declines post-close?"
**Yulia's approach:** Review the thesis. Review the DD. If the thesis still holds and DD didn't reveal deal-killers, close. Perfect information doesn't exist.

# APPENDIX B: DEAL STRUCTURE DECISION TREES

## Asset Sale vs. Stock Sale Decision

```
Q1: Does the business have transferable licenses/certifications
    that would be lost in an asset sale?
    YES → Stock sale favored (preserves licenses)
    NO → Continue to Q2

Q2: Is there environmental liability risk?
    YES → Asset sale strongly favored (isolates liability)
    NO → Continue to Q3

Q3: Does the business have significant favorable contracts
    that require consent for assignment?
    YES → Stock sale reduces consent requirements
    NO → Continue to Q4

Q4: What is the seller's tax preference?
    Asset sale → Seller can allocate purchase price to
                 personal goodwill (capital gains treatment)
    Stock sale → Seller gets full capital gains on stock,
                 BUT buyer loses asset step-up benefit

Q5: Is the buyer SBA-financed?
    YES → SBA generally prefers asset sales
    NO → Structure based on Q1–Q4 analysis

DEFAULT for SMB: Asset sale
(Unless licenses, contracts, or Medicare certification
 make entity sale necessary)
```

## Seller Note Decision Tree

```
Q1: Does buyer have full purchase price in cash/financing?
    YES → No seller note needed (negotiate lower price for certainty)
    NO → Continue to Q2

Q2: What percentage gap needs to be filled?
    <10% → Standard seller note, minimal risk to seller
    10-25% → Acceptable but negotiate protections (first-position security)
    >25% → High risk to seller. Consider reducing price instead.

Q3: What security does seller get?
    First position on business assets → Best for seller
    Subordinated to SBA/bank → Riskiest for seller (standard in SBA deals)
    Personal guarantee from buyer → Additional protection

Q4: What term should seller accept?
    3-5 years → Standard for SMB seller notes
    5-7 years → Acceptable for larger notes
    >7 years → Generally too long; seller's risk increases with time

Q5: Interest rate?
    Below market (3-4%) → Only if tax benefits justify it
    Market rate (5-7%) → Standard; fair to both parties
    Above market (8-10%) → Compensates seller for subordination risk
```

## Earnout Decision Tree

```
Q1: Why is an earnout being proposed?
    Valuation gap → Acceptable reason
    Risk allocation → Acceptable reason
    Buyer can't afford full price → Red flag — earnout masks undercapitalization
    Standard in industry → Acceptable (common in healthcare, agencies)

Q2: What metric should the earnout be based on?
    Revenue → Best for sellers (harder for buyer to manipulate)
    Gross profit → Acceptable (some buyer discretion on COGS)
    EBITDA → Worst for sellers (buyer controls expenses)
    Customer retention → Acceptable for service businesses

Q3: What time period?
    6-12 months → Best for sellers (shortest exposure)
    12-24 months → Standard
    >24 months → Resist strongly (too much uncertainty)

Q4: What percentage of deal value?
    <10% → Reasonable, not material to deal economics
    10-20% → Acceptable with strong measurement terms
    20-30% → High risk; negotiate strong protections
    >30% → Seller is essentially co-investing; rethink deal

Q5: Dispute resolution?
    Independent accountant → Best approach
    Arbitration → Acceptable
    Litigation → Worst (expensive, slow, adversarial)
```

# APPENDIX D: NEGOTIATION POWER DYNAMICS

## Assessing Your Leverage Before Negotiating

### Seller Leverage Indicators
| Factor | High Leverage | Low Leverage |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| Market demand | Multiple interested buyers | Single buyer, limited interest |
| Financial performance | Growing, profitable | Flat or declining |
| Time pressure | No urgency (comfortable lifestyle) | Must sell (health, burnout, financial stress) |
| Alternatives | Can keep operating profitably | Business declining without sale |
| Information | Know your worth, have comps | Uncertain about valuation |
| Competition | Active BAFO process | No other offers |
| Uniqueness | Hard to replicate, defensible moat | Commodity business, easy to start from scratch |

### Buyer Leverage Indicators
| Factor | High Leverage | Low Leverage |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| Financing | Cash or committed, no contingency | SBA pending, uncertain |
| Alternatives | Multiple targets available | This is the only viable target |
| Time pressure | No deadline, patient capital | Fund has deployment deadline |
| Expertise | Industry operator with track record | First-time buyer, learning |
| Market conditions | Buyer's market, many sellers | Seller's market, scarce quality businesses |
| Certainty of close | Can close in 30 days | Complex financing, 90+ day timeline |
| DD findings | Material issues found | Clean DD |

### Negotiation Adjustments Based on Leverage

**When you have leverage (high position):**
- Be patient. Time is on your side.
- Make the first offer (anchor high/low depending on your side).
- Be willing to walk. Your willingness to walk IS your leverage.
- Don't give concessions without receiving concessions.

**When you don't have leverage (low position):**
- Move quickly. Delays work against you.
- Focus on certainty and relationship (not price).
- Make concessions strategically to build goodwill.
- Find non-monetary value you can offer (flexibility, speed, transition help).
- Don't bluff. If the other side calls your bluff, you lose credibility permanently.

**When leverage is balanced:**
- Focus on problem-solving, not position-taking.
- Use "if-then" trades: "If you can give us X, we can accept Y."
- Find the deal that's fair for both sides — neither side should feel like they lost.
- Speed benefits everyone. Push for efficient process.

# APPENDIX E: SBA DEAL NEGOTIATION SPECIFICS

## How SBA Financing Changes the Negotiation Dynamic

SBA 7(a) loans are the dominant financing mechanism for SMB acquisitions ($500K–$5M deal size). Understanding SBA requirements is critical because the lender's requirements become negotiation constraints.

### SBA Requirements That Affect Deal Terms

| SBA Requirement | Impact on Negotiation |
|-----------------|---------------------|
| 10% equity injection | Buyer must have 10% of total project cost in equity (cash, ROBS, or gift) |
| DSCR ≥ 1.25x | Business must generate enough SDE/EBITDA to cover debt service with 25% cushion |
| Seller note must be on standby | If seller note is part of structure, SBA requires it to be subordinated and on standby (no payments for minimum 24 months, often full term of SBA loan) |
| Full seller standby | Seller note interest-only after 24 months, or full standby — varies by lender |
| Personal guarantee | Buyer (and any owner with 20%+ equity) must provide unlimited personal guarantee |
| Life insurance | Key man life insurance required (amount = loan balance) |
| No change of use | Business must continue in substantially same manner for term of loan |
| Goodwill limit | Goodwill cannot exceed appraised business value; lender orders business valuation |
| Prohibited equity sources | Borrowed funds cannot count as equity injection; HELOC treated as borrowed |

### How SBA Standby Rules Affect Seller Note Negotiation

The SBA seller standby requirement is the most misunderstood term in SMB deals:

**What "standby" means:**
- Seller note payments are subordinated to SBA loan
- Minimum 24 months of no payments on seller note (full standby)
- After standby period: interest-only payments allowed (some lenders)
- Some lenders require full standby for entire SBA loan term (10 years)
- If business defaults on SBA loan, seller note is worthless

**Impact on seller:**
- A $200K seller note at 6% on full standby for 2 years means the seller receives $0 for 24 months
- Risk: if business fails, seller loses both the note AND the business
- Seller note in SBA deals is effectively "equity from the seller's perspective"

**Negotiation implication:**
- Sellers should demand higher interest rate on standby notes (7%–8%) to compensate for risk
- Sellers should negotiate personal guarantee from buyer on the seller note
- Sellers should consider whether the all-in deal (cash + standby note) is actually equivalent to a lower cash-only offer

### SBA Deal Timeline — What to Expect

| Phase | Timeline | Notes |
|-------|----------|-------|
| SBA pre-qualification | 1–2 weeks | Buyer provides PFS, credit, industry experience |
| LOI signed | — | SBA formal process begins |
| SBA application submitted | 2–4 weeks after LOI | Full package: financials, projections, PPA draft |
| SBA business valuation | 2–4 weeks | Lender-ordered appraisal of business value |
| SBA credit committee | 1–3 weeks | Internal bank approval |
| SBA authorization | 1–2 weeks | SBA Dallas/Sacramento office review |
| Closing preparation | 2–4 weeks | Legal documents, insurance, escrow |
| **Total: 75–120 days from LOI** | | Faster if buyer is pre-qualified and responsive |

### Negotiation Tactics Specific to SBA Deals

**For sellers dealing with SBA buyers:**
1. Require SBA pre-qualification letter before signing LOI — this filters out 30% of unqualified buyers
2. Understand that SBA valuation may differ from your asking price — be prepared for a gap
3. Include "financing contingency deadline" in LOI — if SBA isn't approved by Day 60, LOI terminates
4. Don't agree to standby seller note without understanding the actual terms your lender requires
5. Know that SBA deals take longer — plan for 90–120 day close, not 45–60

**For buyers using SBA:**
1. Get pre-qualified BEFORE making offers — this differentiates you from 70% of other buyers
2. Choose an SBA lender experienced in business acquisitions (not just real estate)
3. Include realistic financing timeline in your LOI — don't promise 45-day close if SBA takes 90
4. Have backup equity source identified (ROBS, HELOC, co-investor) in case SBA amount is less than expected
5. Your SBA pre-approval is your #1 negotiation weapon — leads with certainty

# APPENDIX F: EARNOUT MECHANICS IN DETAIL

## Structuring Earnouts That Actually Work

### The Problem with Earnouts
80% of earnout disputes come from 3 causes:
1. Ambiguous measurement methodology
2. Buyer's operational decisions affecting earnout metrics
3. No independent verification mechanism

### Earnout Best Practices

**Metric selection (from best to worst for sellers):**
1. **Revenue** — Hardest for buyer to manipulate. Best for sellers.
2. **Gross Profit** — Some buyer discretion on COGS allocation. Acceptable.
3. **EBITDA** — Buyer controls expenses. Worst for sellers. Avoid if possible.
4. **Customer retention** — Clear metric but buyer's service quality affects it.
5. **Milestone-based** — Specific achievements (license obtained, product launched). Binary.

**Measurement terms to include in purchase agreement:**
- **Accounting methodology:** GAAP, consistent with historical practices
- **Reporting frequency:** Quarterly statements within 30 days of quarter end
- **Audit rights:** Seller can review books at own expense once per measurement period
- **Dispute resolution:** Independent accountant, binding decision, costs split 50/50
- **Payment timing:** Within 30 days of measurement period end
- **Acceleration clause:** If buyer sells the business during earnout period, full remaining earnout is due
- **Effort covenant:** Buyer must operate business in substantially the same manner (no starving the business to reduce earnout metrics)

### Earnout Calculation Example

**Setup:**
- Total deal: $2.0M
- Cash at close: $1.6M (80%)
- Earnout: $400K (20%), based on revenue
- Measurement: TTM revenue at 12 months post-close
- Target: $2.0M revenue (same as trailing at close)
- Linear pro-ration: 50%–100% of target = pro-rated earnout

**Scenario 1: Revenue = $2.0M (100% of target)**
Earnout = $400K (full payout)

**Scenario 2: Revenue = $1.8M (90% of target)**
Earnout = $400K × (90% - 50%) / (100% - 50%) = $400K × 80% = $320K

**Scenario 3: Revenue = $1.5M (75% of target)**
Earnout = $400K × (75% - 50%) / (100% - 50%) = $400K × 50% = $200K

**Scenario 4: Revenue = $0.9M (45% of target, below floor)**
Earnout = $0 (below 50% threshold)

### Red Flags in Earnout Proposals

1. **Earnout based on EBITDA with buyer-controlled expense structure** — buyer can inflate expenses to reduce earnout
2. **No effort covenant** — buyer can divert resources away from the business
3. **No acceleration on sale** — buyer can flip the business and avoid paying earnout
4. **Measurement period >24 months** — too much uncertainty
5. **Buyer controls all reporting with no audit rights** — information asymmetry
6. **Dispute resolution through litigation** — expensive, slow, hostile

# APPENDIX G: THE NEGOTIATION TIMELINE — WHAT HAPPENS WHEN

## Typical SMB Deal Timeline with Negotiation Milestones

### Week 1–4: Pre-Market / Initial Conversations
**Negotiation focus:** Information exchange, motivation alignment
- Seller establishes business value narrative
- Buyer assesses fit and preliminary valuation
- NDA signed, CIM or initial financial package shared
- Both sides evaluating whether to proceed

### Week 4–8: IOI Phase
**Negotiation focus:** Price range and high-level structure
- Buyer submits IOI (or multiple buyers submit)
- Seller evaluates using 5-dimension framework
- Counter-offers exchanged (1–2 rounds typical)
- Agreement on price range and structure triggers LOI drafting

### Week 8–12: LOI Negotiation
**Negotiation focus:** Binding terms that govern the deal
- 3–5 business days to draft LOI after IOI acceptance
- 5–10 business days to negotiate LOI terms
- Key battles: exclusivity period, working capital definition, DD scope, deposit
- LOI signed = exclusivity begins, DD starts

### Week 12–20: Due Diligence
**Negotiation focus:** Risk allocation, finding-based adjustments
- Financial DD: 2–4 weeks (accountant-led, quality of earnings)
- Legal DD: 3–5 weeks (attorney-led, contract review, litigation)
- Operational DD: 2–3 weeks (site visits, employee meetings, customer references)
- DD findings presented; re-trade discussions (if any) in Week 16–18
- Financing finalized (SBA authorization or conventional commitment)

### Week 20–24: Purchase Agreement and Closing Preparation
**Negotiation focus:** Legal terms, reps & warranties, closing conditions
- Purchase agreement drafted by buyer's attorney (1–2 weeks)
- Seller's attorney review and markup (1 week)
- Negotiation of reps, warranties, indemnification, closing conditions (1–2 weeks)
- Closing conditions satisfied: lease assignment, license transfer, financing funded
- Pre-closing: working capital calculation, inventory count, employee communications

### Week 24–26: Closing
**Negotiation focus:** Final true-ups, transition planning
- Working capital true-up calculation
- Final walk-through / operational verification
- Closing documents signed, funds transferred
- Employee and customer notifications
- Transition period begins

### Total: 6 months from first conversation to close (typical SMB deal)

**Reality check:** This timeline assumes a motivated seller, a qualified buyer, and no major complications. Add 30–60 days for any of: SBA financing, environmental assessment, complex lease negotiation, multi-state regulatory approval, or re-trade negotiation.

# APPENDIX H: YULIA'S NEGOTIATION RULES — NON-NEGOTIABLE PRINCIPLES

## Rules Yulia Follows in Every Deal Conversation

1. **Never recommend a deal where one side is being taken advantage of.** Fair deals close. Unfair deals blow up.

2. **Always calculate effective net proceeds, not headline price.** The number that matters is what lands in the seller's account after taxes, costs, and time.

3. **Never fabricate competitive interest.** If there are other buyers, say so. If there aren't, don't lie about it. Credibility is more valuable than short-term leverage.

4. **Always set a walk-away number before negotiating.** If you don't know your floor, you don't have one. Decide what you'll accept and what you won't — before the pressure starts.

5. **Never recommend an earnout as more than 20% of total deal value.** Beyond that, it's not an earnout — it's the seller financing the deal with their own risk.

6. **Always verify financing before accepting IOIs.** An IOI without financing is a wish list, not an offer.

7. **Never pressure a seller to accept terms they don't understand.** Take the time to explain every term in the LOI and purchase agreement. Informed parties make better decisions.

8. **Always recommend professional representation.** Sellers need an M&A attorney and an accountant. Buyers need the same plus a lender. DIY deal-making is how people lose money.

9. **Never share confidential information from one party with another.** Chinese wall is absolute. Seller data stays on the seller side. Buyer data stays on the buyer side.

10. **Always prioritize deal certainty over deal maximization.** A closed deal at 95% of maximum value is infinitely better than a blown deal at 100%. The last 5% kills more deals than any other single factor.

11. **Never recommend a deal structure that requires everything to go right.** Good deals survive one or two things going wrong. If the structure only works in the best case, it's not a good structure.

12. **Always consider the human element.** Behind every deal are people whose livelihoods, legacies, and life plans depend on the outcome. Negotiate with empathy without sacrificing rigor.
