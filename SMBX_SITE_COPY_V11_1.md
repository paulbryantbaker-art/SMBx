## "Selling" doesn't mean one thing — and picking the wrong path is expensive

Most people assume there's one way to exit a business: hand over the keys, cash the check. In reality, there are six fundamentally different structures, and choosing the wrong one can cost years of time or hundreds of thousands in value.

`[CARDS]` Six exit types:

**Full Sale** — Clean exit. Maximize the number. Yulia runs the complete process. 6–18 months.

**Partner Buyout** — One of you wants out. The hard part isn't the valuation. It's the financing, the operating agreement, and keeping the personal relationship intact while separating the business one.

**Capital Raise** — Maybe you don't need to sell at all. Debt, equity, SBA expansion — every scenario modeled with what you keep and what you give up.

**ESOP** — Employee ownership with real tax advantages. S-Corp sellers can defer gains under §1042 by reinvesting in qualified replacement property. The math is compelling when it fits.

**Majority Sale** — Sell 51–80% to PE or a strategic buyer. Take significant cash off the table today. Keep skin in the game for the second bite — which in many PE deals is where the real wealth creation happens.

**Partial Sale** — Carve out a division. License IP. Sell-leaseback real estate. Creative structures that unlock value without a total exit.

Most owners I've worked with had never seriously considered more than one of these. Yulia models every path against your specific numbers, entity type, and goals.

## The acquisition tax benefit that changes the return math

Here's something I always make sure buyers understand early, because it fundamentally changes the economics of the deal.

In an asset purchase, you get a stepped-up basis on every acquired asset. You can depreciate them again — even though the seller already depreciated them. On a $2M acquisition, the tax shield from that stepped-up basis can be worth $200K–$400K over five years. That's not a theoretical number. That's a direct reduction in your federal income tax that flows straight to cash flow.

The key is how you allocate the purchase price across the seven Section 1060 asset classes. Goodwill gets amortized over fifteen years — slow. Equipment might qualify for Section 179 immediate expensing — fast. Real estate follows its own schedule entirely. The allocation is a negotiation with the seller because it's a zero-sum game between your tax position and theirs.

`[COMPARE]`:
```
$2M ACQUISITION · ASSET PURCHASE
Year 1: $87K | Year 2: $72K | Year 3: $61K
Year 4: $48K | Year 5: $32K
Total shield: ~$300,000
+ SBA interest deduction
Effective tax rate Yr 1–3: ~4%
```

Combine the depreciation shield with deductible SBA interest, and many buyers pay effectively zero federal income tax for the first three years of ownership. A $2M acquisition with a $300K shield effectively costs you $1.7M. That changes your DSCR calculation. It changes your return projections. It changes the price at which you'd walk away.
