## SECTION 1: VALUATION METHODOLOGY — THE COMPLETE FRAMEWORK

### 1.1 Seller's Discretionary Earnings (SDE) — The Main Street Standard

Seller's Discretionary Earnings is the gold standard metric for valuing owner-operated
businesses with less than approximately $5M in EBITDA. SDE represents the total financial
benefit available to a single full-time owner-operator of the business. It answers the
fundamental question every buyer asks: "If I buy this business and run it myself, how
much money will I make?"

**The precise definition:**
SDE = Net Income + Owner's Salary + Owner's Benefits + Depreciation & Amortization +
Interest Expense + One-Time/Non-Recurring Expenses + Discretionary Expenses +
Non-Cash Charges + Verified Add-Backs

The reason SDE is used rather than EBITDA for smaller businesses is straightforward:
in an owner-operated business, the owner IS the primary employee and their compensation
is a discretionary choice, not a fixed operating cost. A business that pays its owner
$400K and shows $100K in net income is economically identical to one that pays its
owner $200K and shows $300K in net income. SDE normalizes this by adding all owner
compensation back.

**When SDE stops being the right metric:**
SDE becomes less useful when a business has professional management in place and the
owner is not an active operator. The threshold is roughly $3-5M in EBITDA or $10M+
in revenue, though the real test is whether the owner could be replaced by a hired
manager at market salary. If yes, EBITDA (with the management salary as an operating
expense) is the better metric because the buyer isn't buying a job — they're buying
a cash-flowing asset.

**The Add-Back Philosophy:**

Add-backs are the heart of SDE calculation and the most frequently disputed element
in any SMB transaction. The fundamental principle is: an add-back is any expense that
would not be incurred by a new owner operating the business at its current capacity.

Legitimate add-backs fall into several categories:

**Owner Compensation Above Market:**
The owner's total compensation package — salary, bonus, distributions, payroll taxes,
health insurance, retirement contributions, and any other personal benefits paid by
the business. Market salary ranges by industry for reference:

- HVAC company manager: $85K–$120K
- Dental practice manager (non-dentist): $65K–$90K
- IT/MSP manager: $90K–$130K
- Restaurant general manager: $55K–$75K
- Landscaping company manager: $50K–$70K
- Auto repair shop manager: $60K–$85K
- Accounting firm manager: $80K–$110K
- Staffing agency branch manager: $70K–$95K

When the owner takes $250K from an HVAC company that would pay a manager $100K,
the add-back is $250K (the full amount, because under SDE we add back ALL owner
compensation, not just the excess).

**Personal Expenses Run Through the Business:**
- Vehicle: personal use percentage of auto lease, fuel, insurance, maintenance
- Cell phone: personal lines on company plan
- Health insurance: owner and family (often $15K–$30K annually)
- Life insurance: owner policies paid by business
- Travel: personal trips coded as business travel
- Meals and entertainment: personal dining, club memberships
- Home office: if owner expenses personal home office through business
- Personal legal fees: estate planning, personal matters billed to company
- Family member perks: spouse's car, children's phone plans

**One-Time Non-Recurring Items:**
- Lawsuit settlement or legal defense costs
- Equipment replacement after catastrophic failure
- Moving expenses (office relocation)
- Natural disaster recovery costs
- COVID-related extraordinary expenses
- One-time consulting projects (ERP implementation, website redesign)
- Severance payments for terminated employees
- Write-off of bad debt from a single large customer default
- Costs to open or close a location

**Non-Cash Charges:**
- Depreciation on owned assets
- Amortization of intangibles
- Stock-based compensation (if any)
- Amortization of loan fees

**Owner's Family Member Compensation Above Market:**
Many SMBs employ the owner's spouse, children, or relatives at above-market rates
or for minimal actual work. If the owner's spouse earns $60K as a "bookkeeper" but
a bookkeeper costs $35K, the add-back is $25K. If the owner's college-age child
earns $30K for summer filing work, the full amount is typically added back.

**Charitable Contributions:**
Donations made through the business are discretionary and would not continue under
a new owner unless they chose to. Add back 100%.

**Rent Normalization:**
If the owner owns the real estate and charges the business rent, that rent must be
normalized to fair market value. If the owner charges $3K/month but market rent is
$5K/month, adjust SDE downward by $24K annually (the business is understating its
true cost). If the owner charges $8K/month but market is $5K/month, add back $36K.

**Interest Expense:**
All interest is added back for SDE because the buyer will have their own capital
structure. The buyer's financing cost is their concern, not the business's.

**Red Flag Add-Backs — What Sellers Try That Doesn't Belong:**

Experienced buyers and their advisors will challenge these:
- "We're going to grow 30% next year" — projected revenue is not an add-back
- Ongoing employee positions labeled as "discretionary" — if you need the person, it's not discretionary
- Marketing expense that drives current revenue — you can't add back the cost while keeping the revenue it generates
- Owner "below market rent" when the property needs $200K in repairs — the below-market rent reflects the property's condition
- Contractor costs for work the owner claims they'll do themselves — unless the buyer has verified skills
- "We don't really need that truck" — if it's on the route, it's operational

**How Buyers Verify Add-Backs:**

The most effective verification methods:
1. **Bank statement reconciliation:** 12 months of business bank statements compared to every line item on the P&L. Unexplained deposits or withdrawals surface immediately.
2. **Tax return cross-check:** The P&L the seller presents should reconcile to their tax returns within 5%. Larger gaps demand explanation.
3. **Credit card statement review:** Business credit cards reveal actual spending patterns — personal charges become visible.
4. **Payroll records:** Verify owner compensation, family member roles, and actual hours worked.
5. **Lease and contract review:** Verify rent normalization claims against actual agreements.
6. **Industry benchmarking:** If the seller claims 45% SDE margins in an industry where 25% is normal, every add-back needs third-party verification.

**Single-Owner vs. Multi-Location SDE Adjustment:**

For a single-location, single-owner business, SDE is straightforward — add back
the one owner's total compensation. For multi-location businesses with one owner who
oversees all locations, SDE must be carefully calculated: the owner's salary is added
back, but any replacement manager costs at individual locations are real operating
expenses. A two-location pest control company where the owner manages one location
and has a manager at the other has an SDE that includes only the owner's compensation
as an add-back — the other manager's salary stays as an expense.

**Why the Same Business Has Different SDE Depending on the Buyer:**

SDE is theoretically objective, but in practice, different buyers will calculate
slightly different SDE based on their specific situation:
- A buyer who is also a licensed electrician can add back the cost of the lead electrician
  the business currently employs (they'll do that work themselves)
- A buyer who owns adjacent real estate may not need the business's current warehouse lease
- A buyer with an existing back office can eliminate the bookkeeper position
- A strategic buyer acquiring a competitor may eliminate duplicate management

This is why Yulia presents SDE with "buyer-specific" adjustments when working with
buyers in the valuation stage.

**Worked Example: Cleaning Company**

ABC Commercial Cleaning — Raw financials:
- Revenue: $1,800,000
- Net Income (per tax return): $142,000

Add-backs:
- Owner salary: $180,000
- Owner health insurance: $22,000
- Owner vehicle (100% personal use): $18,000
- Owner cell phone (2 personal lines): $3,600
- Owner's wife — "office manager" ($45K salary, actual work: ~5 hrs/week, market rate for that: $12K): $33,000
- Depreciation: $28,000
- Interest on line of credit: $14,000
- One-time legal fee (slip-and-fall lawsuit, settled): $35,000
- Charitable donations to church and Little League: $6,000
- Country club membership: $8,400

Raw net income: $142,000
Total add-backs: $347,000 (rounded)
**Adjusted SDE: $489,000** (rounded)

But wait — the owner also runs personal Amazon purchases through the business card
and they net out to about $12K/year. That's not on the P&L as a line item, but
bank statement review reveals it as cost of goods adjustments. Corrected add-back
brings **SDE to approximately $501,000**.

At 2.5x–3.5x SDE for commercial cleaning, the value range is **$1.25M–$1.75M**.

### 1.2 EBITDA — The Institutional Standard

EBITDA — Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization — is the
standard metric for businesses above the Main Street threshold. It becomes the
primary metric when:

- The business has $3M+ in EBITDA (roughly $15M+ revenue in most industries)
- A professional management team runs operations without the owner
- The buyer is institutional (PE, family office, strategic acquirer)
- The seller is not the primary operator

The key difference from SDE: EBITDA does NOT add back owner compensation. Instead,
it assumes the business pays a market-rate CEO/GM salary as an operating expense.
This matters enormously in valuation — a business with $800K SDE might only have
$500K EBITDA once you account for a $300K replacement CEO.

**Adjusted EBITDA:**

The "adjusted" in adjusted EBITDA refers to normalizations that make EBITDA
comparable across transactions:
- Non-recurring revenue or expenses (one-time events)
- Related-party transactions at non-market rates (rent, services)
- Stock-based compensation (non-cash)
- Management fees to holding company (if any)
- Run-rate adjustments (if the business changed materially mid-year)
- Pro-forma adjustments for completed acquisitions or divestitures

Institutional buyers care deeply about "quality of EBITDA" — whether the adjusted
number is sustainable, repeatable, and growing.

**Quality of Earnings (QoE) Reports:**

A QoE is a financial due diligence report prepared by an independent accounting firm
(not the company's regular accountant). It typically costs $30K–$100K depending on
deal size and complexity. The QoE firm:

1. Verifies revenue recognition policies and confirms revenue is real
2. Analyzes gross margin sustainability (are margins improving or deteriorating?)
3. Validates every add-back with supporting documentation
4. Identifies non-recurring items the seller didn't disclose
5. Analyzes working capital trends and seasonal patterns
6. Assesses the quality of the customer base (concentration, churn, contract terms)
7. Reviews the accounting policies for consistency with GAAP
8. Calculates a "QoE-adjusted EBITDA" that both parties negotiate from

The buyer pays for the QoE in virtually all cases. The report typically takes 3–6
weeks to complete. It is the single most important piece of financial due diligence.

For deals under $5M, a full QoE may not be cost-effective. Instead, a "mini QoE" or
"agreed-upon procedures" engagement (costing $8K–$20K) covers the most important
areas without the full scope.

**EBITDA Margin Benchmarks by Industry:**

- SaaS/Software: 20%–40% (best-in-class: 50%+)
- Professional services: 15%–30%
- Healthcare services (dental, vet): 20%–35%
- Essential services (HVAC, pest): 12%–22%
- Manufacturing: 8%–18%
- Restaurants: 8%–15%
- Retail: 5%–12%
- Trucking/logistics: 6%–14%

These ranges are for businesses with $5M+ revenue. Smaller businesses tend to have
higher variance because the owner's compensation choice dramatically affects margins.

**The Bridge from EBITDA to Value:**

EBITDA × Multiple = Enterprise Value
Enterprise Value - Net Debt + Excess Cash = Equity Value
Equity Value / Shares Outstanding = Price Per Share (for stock deals)

For asset deals, the calculation is simpler: EBITDA × Multiple = Purchase Price,
subject to working capital adjustments and allocation of purchase price to assets.

**Why PE Uses EBITDA and Individual Buyers Use SDE:**

PE firms use EBITDA because they are not buying a job — they are buying cash flow
to service acquisition debt and generate returns for investors. The CEO is an expense.
Individual buyers use SDE because they ARE buying a job plus the business's cash flow.
Their total economic benefit is SDE, not EBITDA.

This structural difference is why PE-backed add-on acquisitions in consolidating
industries (HVAC, dental, pest control) can pay higher multiples than individual
buyers: they're measuring a different metric. A business with $600K SDE and $300K
EBITDA (owner takes $300K salary) looks like a 5x SDE deal at $3M but only a 10x
EBITDA deal — which is expensive. A PE buyer sees the same business as a 6x EBITDA
add-on if they can cut the owner salary and replace with a $100K manager, lifting
EBITDA to $500K and making the effective multiple 6x — reasonable for a platform add-on.

### 1.3 Revenue Multiples

Revenue multiples are used when earnings metrics are unreliable or when the business
model emphasizes growth over current profitability. The most common applications:

**SaaS/Software:**
Revenue multiples dominate SaaS valuation because many SaaS businesses prioritize
growth over profitability. The metric is typically ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue)
or MRR × 12. As of 2025-2026:
- High-growth SaaS (>30% YoY): 5x–10x ARR
- Moderate-growth SaaS (15%–30%): 3x–6x ARR
- Stable/slow-growth SaaS (<15%): 2x–4x ARR
- Declining SaaS: 1x–2x ARR

Net Revenue Retention (NRR) is the key modifier: NRR >110% adds 1-2x; NRR <90% subtracts 1-2x.

**Professional Services:**
When a professional services firm has volatile earnings but stable revenue:
- Accounting/CPA firms: 0.8x–1.5x revenue (higher for recurring tax/bookkeeping)
- Insurance agencies: 1.5x–3.0x revenue (based on commission book)
- Staffing agencies: 0.3x–0.8x revenue (very labor-intensive, low margins)
- Marketing agencies: 0.5x–1.2x revenue (highly dependent on client retention)

**Certain Retail and Distribution:**
- Pharmacies: 0.3x–0.5x revenue (regulated margins, PBM pressures)
- Distribution: 0.3x–0.8x revenue (commodity business, margin-dependent)

**Limitations of Revenue Multiples:**
Revenue multiples can be deeply misleading because they ignore profitability. A $5M
revenue business at 5% margin is worth far less than a $5M revenue business at 25%
margin, even if both are in the same industry. Revenue multiples should always be
cross-checked against earnings-based valuation to ensure the implied margin assumption
is reasonable. If a revenue multiple implies EBITDA margins the business hasn't
demonstrated, the multiple is too high.

**Gross Margin Adjustments:**
For businesses with widely varying margins, applying revenue multiples to gross profit
(revenue minus COGS) can be more meaningful. A distribution company with 15% gross
margins valued at 0.5x revenue is really being valued at 3.3x gross profit — a more
useful comparison point.

### 1.4 Asset-Based Valuation

Asset-based valuation determines a business's worth based on its underlying assets
rather than its earnings power. It serves as a valuation floor — what the business
would be worth if you stopped operating it and sold the pieces.

**When Asset Value Matters:**

1. **Asset-heavy businesses:** Manufacturing, trucking, equipment rental, construction.
   The equipment, vehicles, and real estate may be worth more than the earnings suggest.
2. **Real estate-included transactions:** A business with $300K SDE might be worth $900K
   based on earnings (3x SDE), but if it sits on commercial real estate worth $1.2M,
   the total deal value must account for both.
3. **Inventory-heavy businesses:** Retail, distribution, wholesale. Inventory at cost
   is part of the purchase price, usually on top of the goodwill/earnings-based value.
4. **Distressed businesses:** When earnings are negative or negligible, asset value
   becomes the primary (and sometimes only) valuation approach.
5. **Holding companies:** Businesses that own assets (real estate portfolio, equipment
   fleet) without significant operating earnings.

**Book Value vs. Fair Market Value vs. Liquidation Value:**

- **Book value:** What the assets are recorded at on the balance sheet (cost minus
  accumulated depreciation). Often bears little relationship to actual value, especially
  for older assets that are fully depreciated but still productive.
- **Fair market value (FMV):** What a willing buyer would pay a willing seller for the
  asset in an orderly transaction. Usually determined by appraisal. FMV of equipment
  is typically 40%–70% of replacement cost depending on age and condition.
- **Liquidation value:** What the assets would bring at auction or forced sale. Typically
  50%–70% of FMV. This is the absolute floor of value.

**Normalized Net Asset Value Calculation:**
Total assets at FMV - Total liabilities = Normalized Net Asset Value
Add: Goodwill (if earnings support premium over asset value)
Less: Contingent liabilities and off-balance-sheet obligations

**When Goodwill Dominates:**
In most service businesses, goodwill represents 70%–90% of total value because the
assets (computers, furniture, vehicles) are modest compared to the earnings power of
the customer relationships, brand, and workforce. A marketing agency with $500K EBITDA
might have $50K in tangible assets — virtually all value is goodwill.

**When Goodwill is Minimal:**
Asset-intensive businesses can see goodwill represent as little as 10%–30% of value.
A trucking company with $300K EBITDA and a fleet worth $2M has most of its value in
the iron, not the relationships. Buyers in these industries often negotiate harder on
price because they can replicate the business by simply buying similar equipment.

### 1.5 Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) for SMBs

DCF is the theoretically "correct" valuation method — it values a business based on
the present value of its future cash flows. In practice, DCF is rarely the primary
valuation method for SMB transactions, but it serves important roles.

**When DCF is Appropriate for SMBs:**
- Businesses with highly predictable cash flows (maintenance contracts, subscriptions)
- When both buyer and seller want a "mathematically rigorous" approach
- For businesses with significant growth expected (the multiple approach undervalues growth)
- As a reasonableness check on multiples-based valuation
- When financing requires a formal valuation (SBA lenders sometimes request it)

**When DCF is NOT Appropriate:**
- Businesses with volatile or unpredictable revenues
- Very small businesses where the assumptions overwhelm the analysis
- When there isn't at least 3 years of historical financials to project from
- When the business is declining (DCF requires an honest terminal value assumption)

**Simplified DCF Approach for SMBs:**

Step 1: Project free cash flow for 5 years
- Start with trailing 12 months SDE or EBITDA
- Apply a conservative growth rate (use 3-year historical CAGR, capped at 10%)
- Subtract maintenance capex (use historical average or industry benchmark)
- Subtract estimated taxes on the income

Step 2: Calculate terminal value
Two methods:
- **Gordon Growth Model:** Terminal FCF × (1 + long-term growth rate) / (discount rate - growth rate)
  Long-term growth rate should be 2%–3% for most SMBs (GDP-ish growth).
- **Exit Multiple:** Terminal Year EBITDA × assumed exit multiple
  The exit multiple should be the same or lower than the current implied multiple.

Step 3: Discount to present value
Use an appropriate discount rate. For SMBs, this is much higher than public companies:

**Discount Rate Selection for Private Businesses:**

The build-up method is most commonly used:
- Risk-free rate (10-year Treasury): ~4.3% (March 2026)
- Equity risk premium: 5.0%–6.0%
- Size premium (micro-cap): 4.0%–6.0%
- Industry risk premium: 1.0%–4.0% (varies by industry)
- Company-specific risk: 2.0%–8.0% (owner dependency, concentration, etc.)
- **Total discount rate: 16%–28%** for most SMBs

At a 20%–25% discount rate, the DCF typically produces a value BELOW what comparable
transactions suggest. This is because the theoretical discount rate for private
businesses captures risks that are real but also reflected in how multiples are
set by the market.

**Using DCF Strategically:**
- For sellers: DCF often produces a lower value than multiples, so it's useful as a
  floor argument. "Even at a conservative 22% discount rate, the DCF says my business
  is worth at least $X."
- For buyers: DCF is useful to model returns. "At $X purchase price, my IRR is 25%
  and I break even by Year 3."
- As a negotiation tool: both sides can adjust assumptions (growth rate, discount rate,
  terminal multiple) to show how the value changes under different scenarios.

### 1.6 The Seven-Factor Scoring System

Yulia uses a seven-factor system to adjust multiples from the industry baseline.
Each factor is scored on a scale that determines premium or discount applied to the
base multiple for the industry and size.

**Factor 1: Financial Performance Quality (Weight: 20%)**

What Excellent Looks Like (Premium: +0.5x to +1.0x):
- 3+ years of audited or reviewed financials matching tax returns within 2%
- Revenue growing at 8%+ CAGR over 3 years
- Consistent or improving margins year-over-year
- Revenue >50% recurring (contracts, subscriptions, maintenance agreements)
- Strong cash conversion (free cash flow >80% of EBITDA)
- Minimal seasonality or well-managed seasonal patterns

What Poor Looks Like (Discount: -0.5x to -1.5x):
- Tax returns and P&Ls don't reconcile (gap >10%)
- Declining revenue trend (any negative CAGR)
- Margin compression year-over-year
- All project-based revenue with no recurring base
- Significant uncollected receivables (DSO >60 days)
- Cash flow doesn't match reported earnings

How It Affects Multiple: Clean, growing, recurring financials are the single biggest
multiple driver. A pest control company with $400K SDE and 15% revenue growth, 85%
recurring revenue, and clean books might trade at 4.5x SDE. The same SDE with flat
revenue, 30% recurring, and messy books might trade at 2.5x.

**Factor 2: Business Transferability (Weight: 20%)**

What Excellent Looks Like (Premium: +0.25x to +0.75x):
- Professional management team runs daily operations
- Owner works <20 hours/week on the business
- Key relationships are held by employees, not the owner
- Documented processes for all critical functions
- Owner has been out for 2+ weeks and nothing broke
- Brand identity is the company, not the owner's personal brand

What Poor Looks Like (Discount: -0.5x to -1.0x):
- Owner IS the business — all key relationships are personal
- No second-in-command or key employee who could step up
- Owner works 60+ hours/week and handles sales, operations, and finance
- Customers ask for the owner by name
- No SOPs, no documented processes
- The business would lose 30%+ of revenue within 6 months of owner departure

How It Affects Multiple: This factor has the steepest discount potential. A highly
owner-dependent business is the riskiest acquisition for any buyer because the revenue
may not transfer. Buyers facing high owner-dependency either reduce the multiple
significantly or structure earnouts to protect against customer attrition.

**Factor 3: Revenue Diversification (Weight: 15%)**

What Excellent Looks Like (Premium: +0.25x):
- No single customer >10% of revenue
- Top 10 customers represent <40% of revenue
- Diverse product/service mix (no single offering >50%)
- Multiple revenue streams (service + product + recurring)
- Geographic diversification across multiple markets

What Poor Looks Like (Discount: -0.25x to -0.75x):
- Single customer >25% of revenue
- Top 3 customers >50% of revenue
- Single product line or service offering
- Geographic concentration in one market or region
- Revenue dependent on one channel or platform

How It Affects Multiple: Customer concentration is the second-most-common deal killer
after owner dependency. A business where 40% of revenue comes from one customer is
priced at a significant discount because the buyer is essentially buying a single
customer relationship — if that customer leaves, the acquisition fails.

**Factor 4: Growth Potential (Weight: 15%)**

What Excellent Looks Like (Premium: +0.25x to +0.75x):
- Large addressable market with clear room to grow
- Proven expansion model (could add locations, territories, products)
- Competitive advantages that scale (brand, technology, network effects)
- Under-penetrated market with limited competition
- Evidence of demand exceeding current capacity

What Poor Looks Like (No premium, but rarely discounted unless declining):
- Mature market with no obvious growth levers
- Full market saturation in current geography
- Commodity business with no differentiation
- Technology disruption threatening the model
- Regulatory headwinds limiting expansion

How It Affects Multiple: Buyers pay for growth potential, but they pay more for
demonstrated growth than projected growth. A business growing at 15%/year gets a
real premium. A business claiming it "could" grow at 15% gets a hypothetical premium
that usually evaporates in negotiation.

**Factor 5: Systems and Documentation (Weight: 10%)**

What Excellent Looks Like (Premium: +0.25x):
- Modern software stack (cloud CRM, accounting, project management)
- Documented SOPs for all repeatable processes
- Employee training materials and onboarding procedures
- Financial reporting automated and timely (monthly P&L within 10 days)
- Technology-enabled service delivery (GPS tracking, scheduling, routing)
- Data-driven decision-making with dashboards and KPIs

What Poor Looks Like (Discount: -0.25x to -0.50x):
- Everything lives in the owner's head
- Paper-based or spreadsheet operations
- No CRM — customer list is a Rolodex or personal contacts
- Financial reporting is annual (tax time only)
- No employee handbook, no safety procedures
- Technology is outdated or proprietary with no support

How It Affects Multiple: Good systems reduce transition risk and post-close operational
disruption. Buyers (especially PE) price-in the cost and risk of upgrading systems —
a business that needs $50K in technology investment and 6 months of implementation
effectively costs that much more to acquire.

**Factor 6: Industry Health (Weight: 10%)**

What Excellent Looks Like (Premium: +0.25x to +0.50x):
- Industry growing at GDP+ rates
- Active consolidation by PE creating exit optionality
- Favorable secular trends (aging population for healthcare, housing stock for home services)
- High barriers to entry protecting existing operators
- Regulatory environment that favors incumbents

What Poor Looks Like (Discount: -0.25x to -0.50x):
- Industry declining or facing disruption
- Regulatory threats (new compliance costs, licensing changes)
- Technology displacement (retail, print media, traditional services)
- Labor shortages specific to the industry with no clear solution
- Commodity pricing pressure from new entrants or imports

How It Affects Multiple: Industry health is a macro factor that lifts or depresses
all businesses within a sector. HVAC companies in 2025-2026 trade at higher multiples
than they did in 2015 in part because PE consolidation has compressed supply and
increased buyer competition.

**Factor 7: Deal Risk Profile (Weight: 10%)**

What Excellent Looks Like (Premium: +0.25x):
- Long-term lease with renewal options at favorable terms
- All licenses and permits transferable
- No pending or threatened litigation
- Clean environmental history
- No regulatory compliance concerns
- Strong insurance coverage with clean claims history
- No key-man insurance dependency

What Poor Looks Like (Discount: -0.25x to -0.75x):
- Lease expiring within 12 months with no renewal option
- Licenses tied to the owner personally and may not transfer
- Active litigation or known regulatory investigation
- Environmental contamination risk
- Uninsured or underinsured operations
- Key vendor contracts with change-of-control provisions
- Zoning or permitting issues for the property

How It Affects Multiple: Deal risk factors can be binary — some are absolute deal
killers (non-transferable license, material litigation), while others are priced as
discounts. A business with an expiring lease trades at a meaningful discount because
the buyer may not be able to operate at the current location in 12 months.

### 1.7 Premium and Discount Drivers — What Makes the Same Business Worth More or Less

**Premium Drivers (each typically adds +0.25x to +0.75x multiple):**

1. **Recurring Revenue:** Maintenance contracts, subscriptions, retainers, or any
   revenue that automatically renews without active selling. A pest control company
   with 80% recurring revenue trades at a meaningful premium to one with 30% recurring.
   The premium is justified: recurring revenue is predictable, bankable, and reduces
   the new owner's sales burden.

2. **Customer Diversification:** No single customer >10% of revenue means the loss
   of any one customer is survivable. The more diversified, the more stable the cash
   flow, and the more willing lenders are to finance the acquisition.

3. **Management Team in Place:** A competent #2 or management team that runs daily
   operations is enormously valuable. It means the business can survive the ownership
   transition without disruption and gives the buyer optionality (semi-absentee, grow
   via acquisition, etc.).

4. **Geographic Scalability:** A proven business model that could be replicated in
   new geographies signals growth potential. A pest control company with one territory
   that demonstrably works could add territories at known unit economics.

5. **Proprietary Technology or Processes:** Any IP, proprietary software, trade secrets,
   or unique processes that competitors cannot easily replicate. This creates a moat
   that protects margins and customer relationships.

6. **Certifications, Licenses, Permits:** Hard-to-obtain certifications (HVAC contractor
   license, healthcare provider credentials, government contractor clearances) create
   barriers to entry that protect the business's competitive position.

7. **Long-Term Customer Relationships with Contracts:** Multi-year contracts with
   automatic renewal provide revenue visibility that buyers and lenders love.

8. **Strong Reputation and Reviews:** In service businesses, online reputation is
   a tangible asset. A 4.8-star Google rating with 500+ reviews represents years of
   brand equity that a new competitor cannot replicate quickly.

9. **Clean Financials:** Three years of tax returns matching P&L statements, organized
   books, reasonable add-backs — this signals a serious seller and reduces DD risk.

10. **Real Estate Included:** When the transaction includes the real estate at fair
    value, it provides certainty for the buyer (no lease risk) and often enables better
    financing terms (real estate as additional collateral).

**Discount Factors (each typically subtracts -0.25x to -1.0x multiple):**

1. **High Owner Dependency:** The single biggest value destroyer. When the owner IS
   the business — the rainmaker, the key technician, the primary customer relationship —
   the revenue may not survive the transition. Discounts of 1.0x or more are common.

2. **Customer Concentration:** When >25% of revenue comes from one customer, the
   buyer is betting on that relationship continuing. Many buyers won't accept this risk
   at any price; those who do demand significant discounts or earnout protection.

3. **Deteriorating Revenue Trend:** Even slightly declining revenue (2-3% per year)
   signals a business past its peak. Buyers will project the decline forward and
   price accordingly. Three years of 5% annual decline dramatically reduces value.

4. **Undocumented Operations:** Tribal knowledge — processes that live only in
   employees' heads — creates massive transition risk. If key procedures aren't written
   down, they can be lost when employees change.

5. **Lease Expiring Soon:** A lease with less than 2 years remaining and no renewal
   option is a material risk. The buyer may need to relocate, which disrupts operations
   and costs money.

6. **Pending Litigation:** Active lawsuits create uncertainty about future liabilities.
   Even meritless litigation can cost $50K+ to defend and takes management attention.

7. **Weak Financial Records:** If the seller can't produce clean P&Ls, organized tax
   returns, and bank statements that reconcile — buyers assume the worst. Poor records
   don't just reduce the multiple; they shrink the buyer pool to opportunistic bargain-hunters.

8. **Declining Industry:** A business in a structurally declining industry (print media,
   brick-and-mortar retail without ecommerce) faces secular headwinds that reduce both
   the multiple and the buyer pool.

9. **Single Location with No Replication:** A business that works because of one
   specific location (corner restaurant, prime retail spot) has limited scalability
   and is vulnerable to lease changes.

10. **Technology Debt or Aging Equipment:** Buyers will estimate the cost to modernize
    and subtract it from value. A trucking company with a fleet averaging 12 years old
    will need $500K+ in replacements within 2 years — that comes off the price.

## SECTION 2: DEAL STRUCTURES — COMPLETE MECHANICS

### 2.1 Asset Sale vs. Stock Sale — The Decision That Changes Everything

The legal structure of the transaction — whether the buyer purchases the company's
assets or its equity (stock/membership interests) — has profound implications for
taxes, liability, and net proceeds. Understanding this distinction is essential for
advising both buyers and sellers.

**Why Most SMB Deals (<$5M) Are Asset Sales:**

Approximately 80% of SMB transactions are structured as asset sales for these reasons:

1. **Liability protection for the buyer:** In an asset sale, the buyer purchases
   specific assets and assumes only specified liabilities. Unknown liabilities (tax
   deficiencies, undisclosed lawsuits, environmental contamination) stay with the seller's
   entity. In a stock sale, the buyer acquires the entity AND all its liabilities.

2. **Tax basis step-up for the buyer:** When assets are purchased, the buyer gets a
   new tax basis equal to the purchase price, allocated across the assets. This means
   the buyer can depreciate/amortize the full purchase price over time, creating tax
   shields that improve after-tax cash flow. In a stock sale, the assets retain their
   old (often fully depreciated) basis.

3. **SBA preference:** SBA lenders strongly prefer asset sales because they're cleaner
   and carry less liability risk for the buyer (and therefore less credit risk for the lender).

**Why Sellers Prefer Stock Sales:**

1. **Capital gains treatment on the entire proceeds:** In a stock sale of a C-corp,
   the seller pays capital gains tax on the entire gain (currently 20% federal for
   long-term gains, plus state taxes and the 3.8% NIIT). In an asset sale of a C-corp,
   the corporation first pays tax on the gain from selling assets (at corporate rates,
   currently 21%), then the shareholder pays capital gains tax when the proceeds are
   distributed — resulting in double taxation.

2. **Clean break:** In a stock sale, the seller transfers the entity and walks away.
   No need to individually transfer contracts, licenses, leases, or permits.

3. **Contract assignment:** Some contracts (government contracts, healthcare provider
   agreements, franchise agreements) have anti-assignment clauses that make asset sales
   complicated or impossible. A stock sale avoids triggering these clauses because the
   entity (the contract holder) doesn't change — only its ownership does.

**The Tax Math — Worked Example:**

Assume: C-corp, $2M purchase price, seller's basis in stock is $200K.

*Stock Sale:*
- Gain: $2M - $200K = $1,800,000
- Federal LTCG tax: $1,800,000 × 20% = $360,000
- NIIT: $1,800,000 × 3.8% = $68,400
- State tax (assume 5%): $1,800,000 × 5% = $90,000
- **Total tax: $518,400**
- **Net proceeds: $1,481,600**

*Asset Sale (C-corp):*
- Corporate level: $2M - $500K (asset basis) = $1,500,000 gain
  Corporate tax: $1,500,000 × 21% = $315,000
  After-tax corporate proceeds: $2M - $315,000 = $1,685,000
- Shareholder level (liquidating distribution):
  $1,685,000 - $200K basis = $1,485,000 gain
  LTCG + NIIT + state: $1,485,000 × 28.8% = $427,680
- **Total tax: $742,680**
- **Net proceeds: $1,257,320**

The seller nets $224,280 more from the stock sale. This is why C-corp sellers push hard
for stock sale treatment — and why the price difference between the two structures
is a central negotiation point.

**S-Corps and LLCs Change the Math:**

For S-corps and LLCs (pass-through entities), the double-taxation problem doesn't
exist. In an asset sale, the gain flows through to the owner's personal return and is
taxed once. The tax difference between asset and stock sale is smaller, making sellers
more willing to agree to asset sales.

However, there's still a difference: asset sale allocation. The portion of purchase
price allocated to personal goodwill is taxed as capital gains, but portions allocated
to equipment may be subject to ordinary income recapture (depreciation previously
deducted). Sellers want more allocated to goodwill (capital gains rate); buyers want
more allocated to depreciable assets (faster write-offs).

**Purchase Price Allocation in Asset Sales:**

The purchase price must be allocated across seven classes of assets per IRS rules
(Section 1060):
- Class I: Cash and cash equivalents
- Class II: Actively traded securities
- Class III: Accounts receivable, mortgages
- Class IV: Inventory
- Class V: All other tangible and intangible assets (equipment, furniture, vehicles)
- Class VI: Section 197 intangibles except goodwill (non-compete, customer lists, trade names)
- Class VII: Goodwill and going-concern value

Both buyer and seller must use the same allocation on their tax returns (reported on
Form 8594). Negotiating this allocation is often as contentious as negotiating the total
price because it directly affects each party's after-tax outcome.

**Section 338(h)(10) Election:**

This is the bridge between stock sale form and asset sale tax treatment. Available for
S-corps, the 338(h)(10) election allows the deal to be structured as a stock purchase
(for legal purposes) but treated as an asset purchase (for tax purposes). The buyer
gets the step-up in basis; the seller avoids the hassle of transferring individual
assets and contracts. The tax treatment follows asset sale rules.

This election requires both parties to agree and is commonly used in middle market
transactions ($5M+). It's the preferred structure when contract assignment is
problematic but both parties want efficient tax treatment.

### 2.2 Seller Notes — The Tool That Closes Deals

A seller note (also called seller financing, seller carry, or vendor take-back) is a
loan from the seller to the buyer for a portion of the purchase price. It is the single
most common deal structure tool in SMB M&A, appearing in approximately 60-70% of all
Main Street transactions.

**Why Seller Notes Exist:**

The fundamental problem in SMB M&A is the financing gap. A business worth $2M might
have a buyer who can get an SBA loan for 80% ($1.6M) and has $200K in equity (10%).
The remaining $200K (10%) is covered by a seller note. Without the seller note, the
deal doesn't close because the buyer doesn't have enough cash.

**Typical Seller Note Terms:**
- Amount: 10%–20% of purchase price (15% is most common)
- Interest rate: 5%–8% (often negotiated to 6%)
- Term: 5–7 years
- Payment: Monthly interest and principal (amortizing)
- Security: Subordinated to senior debt (SBA or bank loan)
- Personal guarantee: Usually required from the buyer
- UCC filing: Seller files a UCC-1 security interest in the business assets

**SBA Rules on Seller Notes:**

When the transaction is financed with an SBA loan, seller note rules are strict:
- **Full standby for 24 months:** The seller cannot receive any principal or interest
  payments on the note for the first 24 months after closing. This rule exists to
  protect the SBA loan — the lender wants all available cash flow going to debt service
  on the senior loan.
- **Partial standby after 24 months:** After the standby period, the seller note can
  begin payments, but the terms must not create a DSCR problem for the SBA loan.
- **Interest accrues during standby:** Interest typically accrues during the standby
  period and is added to the principal balance, so the amount owed increases.
- **No balloon provisions allowed** during standby (SBA SOP 50 10 7.1).

**Why Sellers Accept Seller Notes:**

1. **Tax deferral:** Under the installment sale method (IRC Section 453), the seller
   only recognizes gain as payments are received. A $200K seller note at 6% over 5 years
   means the seller spreads $200K of capital gains recognition over 5 years instead of
   recognizing it all at closing.
2. **Getting the deal done:** The seller has been trying to sell for months. The best
   buyer needs a note to close. Refusing the note means starting the process over.
3. **Interest income:** 6% on a fully secured note is a reasonable return, especially
   when the seller knows the business well and can assess the risk.
4. **Skin in the game signals confidence:** A seller willing to carry a note is telling
   the buyer "I believe this business will perform well enough for you to pay me back."
   This is a meaningful signal that can justify a higher purchase price.

**Seller Note Risks and Protections:**

The primary risk for the seller is buyer default — the buyer runs the business poorly,
can't make payments, and the seller is left holding a subordinated note behind the SBA
lender. Protections include:
- UCC-1 filing (gives the seller a recorded security interest)
- Personal guarantee from the buyer
- Financial reporting covenants (buyer must provide quarterly financials)
- Default triggers (missed payments, material adverse change)
- Acceleration clauses (entire balance due if buyer violates terms)
- Right of offset: seller can offset note payments against any indemnification claims

**Negotiating Seller Note Size:**

The 10%–15% range is market standard. Buyers who ask for more than 20% are signaling
weak financial capacity. Sellers who refuse any note are limiting their buyer pool to
well-capitalized buyers and PE — which may be fine for larger deals but eliminates most
individual buyers for Main Street transactions.

**When to Push for Full Cash Close:**

Sellers can insist on full cash at close when:
- Multiple qualified buyers are competing (leverage favors seller)
- The business is in a hot industry (HVAC, pest control) with PE buyer interest
- Deal size is large enough to attract institutional buyers ($5M+)
- The buyer is PE-backed or has ample equity

Sellers should accept a note when:
- Only one or two buyers are qualified
- The deal has been on market for 6+ months
- The buyer's SBA pre-approval requires a note
- The note provides favorable tax treatment

### 2.3 Earnouts — The Bridge Over Valuation Gaps

An earnout is a portion of the purchase price that is contingent on the business
achieving specified performance milestones after closing. Earnouts exist because
buyers and sellers sometimes cannot agree on what the business is worth today, but
can agree on what it should be worth if the seller's projections come true.

**When Earnouts Are Used:**

1. **Growth-stage businesses:** The seller claims the business will grow 30% next year
   based on a new product launch. The buyer says "prove it." The earnout bridges the gap.
2. **Disputed projections:** Seller values the business on projected earnings; buyer
   values it on trailing earnings. The earnout says "if your projections are right,
   you'll get your price."
3. **Key customer relationships:** When a seller's relationship with major customers
   is critical to future revenue, an earnout can protect the buyer by tying a portion
   of payment to customer retention.
4. **Post-pandemic recovery:** Businesses recovering from disruption where current
   financials don't reflect normalized earning power.

**Earnout Structure Mechanics:**

A well-structured earnout specifies:
- **Measurement period:** Typically 12-24 months post-close, measured quarterly or annually
- **Performance metric:** Revenue, gross profit, EBITDA, or SDE — each has different implications
- **Threshold and cap:** Minimum performance to trigger any payment, and maximum total payout
- **Payment schedule:** When earned amounts are paid (quarterly, annually, at period end)
- **Accounting method:** How the metric is calculated, who prepares the statements, dispute resolution
- **Operational covenants:** What the buyer must do (or not do) to give the earnout a fair chance

**Revenue vs. EBITDA vs. Gross Profit Earnouts:**

- **Revenue earnout:** Simplest to measure, hardest to manipulate, but doesn't account for
  profitability. A buyer could maintain revenue while cutting margins. Best for: seller who
  just needs to show growth happened.
- **EBITDA earnout:** Most comprehensive, but gives the buyer significant control over costs.
  A buyer who loads expenses onto the business can reduce EBITDA and avoid payment. Best for:
  deals where profitability, not just revenue, matters.
- **Gross profit earnout:** Middle ground — captures both revenue and direct margin, but
  less susceptible to buyer manipulation of overhead allocation. Best for: distribution,
  manufacturing, or product businesses.

**The Principal-Agent Problem:**

This is the fundamental challenge with earnouts: after closing, the seller has
incentives to hit targets but no longer controls the business. The buyer controls the
business but has incentives NOT to hit targets (because they'd have to pay more).

Common conflicts:
- Buyer reduces marketing spend → revenue drops → seller loses earnout
- Buyer adds expenses to the business (management fees) → EBITDA drops → seller loses earnout
- Buyer changes pricing strategy → short-term revenue rises but margins drop (or vice versa)
- Buyer diverts key customers to another entity → revenue drops

**How to Structure an Earnout Sellers Will Accept:**

1. Use revenue (not EBITDA) as the metric — less subject to manipulation
2. Keep the measurement period short (12 months, not 36)
3. Include operational covenants: buyer must maintain current marketing spend, staffing
   levels, and pricing (within defined bands)
4. Grant the seller inspection rights on the financial statements used for measurement
5. Include a dispute resolution mechanism (independent accountant, mediation)
6. Cap the earnout at a reasonable percentage (15-25% of total deal value)

**Practical Advice: Avoid Earnouts if Any Other Structure Works**

Earnouts are the most litigated provision in M&A agreements. They create ongoing
conflict between buyer and seller during a period when cooperation is most needed
(the transition). If the valuation gap can be bridged with a seller note, price
adjustment, or modified deal structure, that is almost always preferable to an
earnout for both parties.

### 2.4 SBA Financing — Complete Mechanics

The Small Business Administration's 7(a) loan program is the dominant financing
vehicle for Main Street M&A, financing approximately 60-70% of all transactions
under $5M. Understanding SBA mechanics is essential for anyone in the SMB M&A space.

**SBA 7(a) vs. 504 — When Each Applies:**

- **7(a) loans:** General purpose, used for business acquisitions, working capital,
  equipment, and real estate. This is the primary M&A loan. Maximum: $5M per loan.
- **504 loans:** Specifically for real estate and large equipment purchases. Less common
  for M&A, but used when the transaction includes significant real estate. Involves two
  lenders: a bank provides 50%, a Certified Development Company (CDC) provides 40%,
  and the buyer provides 10% equity.

For most business acquisitions, the 7(a) is the right product. The 504 comes into
play when the real estate component is significant and the buyer wants better terms
on the property portion.

**The SBA's DSCR Requirement:**

DSCR (Debt Service Coverage Ratio) is the most important underwriting metric for SBA
loans. The SBA requires a minimum of 1.25x global DSCR, meaning the business's
cash flow must be at least 125% of total annual debt service.

"Global" means ALL debt — not just the SBA loan, but also any seller note (after
standby period ends), the buyer's personal debt obligations, and any other business
debt. The global DSCR calculation:

Global DSCR = (Business Cash Flow + Buyer's Other Income) / (SBA Loan Payment +
Seller Note Payment + Buyer's Personal Debt Obligations)

In practice, most lenders want to see 1.25x-1.35x to provide a cushion. Borderline
DSCRs (1.20x-1.25x) can still work but require stronger borrower profiles, additional
collateral, or other mitigating factors.

**SBA Equity Injection:**

The SBA requires a minimum 10% equity injection from the buyer for business
acquisitions. This equity can come from:
- Cash savings
- ROBS (Rollover for Business Startups) — using 401K/IRA funds without early withdrawal penalty
- Home equity (via HELOC, used as equity injection)
- Gift funds (with documentation)
- Another investor's contribution

The equity injection must be documented as the buyer's actual contribution. It cannot
come from the seller (that would be a circular transaction).

**SBA Loan Limits:**

- Standard 7(a): $5,000,000 maximum
- SBA Express: $500,000 maximum (faster processing, less documentation)
- Community Advantage: $350,000 maximum (for underserved markets)

For larger acquisitions, buyers can combine an SBA 7(a) with a conventional loan,
seller note, and equity to reach higher total deal values.

**Eligible vs. Ineligible Businesses:**

SBA-eligible:
- Most operating businesses in the US
- Must be for-profit
- Must operate in the US or its territories
- Must be independently owned and operated
- Must meet SBA size standards (varies by industry, usually <$8M revenue or <500 employees)

SBA-ineligible:
- Gambling businesses (>33% of revenue from gambling)
- Lending/investment businesses (banks, finance companies)
- Multi-level marketing operations
- Real estate investment/speculation
- Businesses with delinquent federal debt
- Life insurance companies
- Certain adult entertainment businesses
- Cannabis businesses (federal law conflict)
- Foreign-based businesses

**The SBA Credit Underwriting Process:**

What lenders actually look at:
1. **Borrower credit score:** 680+ is standard; 700+ preferred. Below 680 is challenging.
2. **Borrower experience:** Industry or management experience relevant to the acquisition
3. **Business cash flow:** Historical SDE/EBITDA trending and DSCR calculation
4. **Equity injection source and documentation:** Where the 10% comes from
5. **Collateral:** Business assets + personal assets (house, savings)
6. **Business plan:** How the buyer plans to operate and grow the business
7. **Seller note terms:** Must comply with SBA standby requirements
8. **Environmental risk:** Phase 1 environmental assessment for most deals

**Typical SBA Structure for a $2M Acquisition:**

Purchase price: $2,000,000
- Buyer equity: $200,000 (10%) — cash + ROBS
- SBA 7(a) loan: $1,600,000 (80%)
- Seller note: $200,000 (10%) — full standby 24 months

SBA loan terms:
- Rate: WSJ Prime + 2.75% = approximately 10.75-11.25% (as of March 2026)
- Term: 10 years for business acquisition (25 years if real estate >51% of assets)
- Monthly payment: approximately $21,800 (at 11% on 10-year term)
- Annual debt service: approximately $261,600

DSCR check: If SDE is $600,000
- DSCR = $600,000 / $261,600 = 2.30x ✓ (well above 1.25x minimum)

After 24-month standby, seller note payments begin:
- Seller note: $200K at 6%, 5-year term → approximately $3,867/month
- Combined annual debt service: $261,600 + $46,400 = $308,000
- New DSCR = $600,000 / $308,000 = 1.95x ✓ (still comfortable)

**Current SBA 7(a) Rate Formula:**

For loans with variable rates (most acquisition loans):
- Loans >$50K, term >7 years: WSJ Prime Rate + 2.75% maximum
- WSJ Prime Rate as of March 2026: approximately 7.75%-8.00%
- All-in rate: approximately 10.50%–10.75%

Fixed-rate SBA loans are available but uncommon for acquisition financing. The rate
is typically 50-75 basis points higher than variable.

**DSCR Worked Example at Current Rates:**

$2M deal, $1.6M SBA loan at 11%, 10-year term:
- Monthly payment: ~$22,000
- Annual debt service: ~$264,000
- Required SDE for 1.25x DSCR: $330,000
- Required SDE for comfortable 1.5x DSCR: $396,000

Compare to 3 years ago at 8%:
- Monthly payment: ~$19,400
- Annual debt service: ~$232,800
- Required SDE for 1.25x DSCR: $291,000

The difference: a business needed $291K SDE to qualify at 8% rates vs. $330K at 11%.
This means ~12% of previously-qualifying deals no longer make the DSCR cut at current
rates, which has compressed deal flow and forced creative structuring.

**What Happens When DSCR is Borderline (1.2x):**

Deals with borderline DSCR can still close through:
1. Larger seller note (reduces SBA loan size, reducing annual payments)
2. Extended SBA term (if real estate is included, 25-year amortization)
3. Buyer's outside income added to the DSCR calculation
4. Historical growth trend showing improving cash flow
5. Collateral coverage exceeding 1:1 on the loan
6. Stronger borrower profile (780+ credit, substantial assets)

**Pre-Approval vs. Conditional vs. Full Approval:**

- **Pre-approval (2-4 weeks):** Based on preliminary review of borrower profile and
  estimated deal terms. Not binding. Useful for making IOIs with financing confidence.
- **Conditional approval (4-8 weeks):** Based on detailed review of business financials,
  borrower profile, and deal terms. Subject to conditions (appraisal, environmental,
  lease assignment, etc.). This is the approval that matters for LOI purposes.
- **Full approval (8-12 weeks from start):** All conditions satisfied, documents
  prepared, ready to close. This is when funds are committed.

**SBA Affiliation Rules:**

PE-backed buyers and search fund operators must navigate SBA affiliation rules.
If the buyer is "affiliated" with other entities that together exceed SBA size
standards, the loan may be denied. Affiliation triggers include:
- Owning >50% of another business
- Having common management with another business
- Being part of an identity of interest group
- Having a management agreement, franchise agreement, or joint venture

Search fund operators with multiple investors must structure carefully to avoid
affiliation issues. Most SBA-experienced attorneys can navigate this, but it adds
time and complexity to the process.

### 2.5 Conventional / Non-SBA Financing

While SBA is dominant, conventional financing plays an important role in SMB M&A,
especially for deals that don't fit the SBA box.

**Community Banks and Regional Banks:**

Local banks are often the best source of conventional acquisition financing because:
- They understand local businesses and markets
- Relationship lending (the banker knows the business or the buyer)
- Flexibility on structure (can hold the loan on their balance sheet)
- Faster processing than SBA (no government paperwork)

Typical conventional terms:
- LTV: 60%–75% (vs. 80-90% for SBA)
- DSCR requirement: 1.35x–1.50x (higher than SBA's 1.25x)
- Rate: Prime + 1.0% to Prime + 3.0% (often similar to SBA)
- Term: 5–7 years (shorter than SBA's 10-year)
- Personal guarantee: Almost always required
- Collateral: Business assets + personal assets + sometimes real estate

**When Conventional Beats SBA:**

1. **Deals with real estate:** If the property is worth more than the business, a
   conventional loan with 25-year amortization on the real estate portion can produce
   better terms than SBA.
2. **Speed:** Conventional loans can close in 30-45 days vs. 60-90 for SBA.
3. **Repeat buyers:** Banks who have an existing relationship with the buyer may offer
   better terms and faster processing.
4. **Larger deals:** For deals above $5M (SBA cap), conventional is the only option
   outside of PE financing.

**Mezzanine Financing:**

For middle market deals ($5M+), mezzanine debt fills the gap between senior debt
and equity. Mezzanine is subordinated debt — junior to the bank loan but senior to
equity. It carries higher rates (12%-18%) but provides the capital to bridge larger
transactions. Mezzanine lenders typically want:
- Warrants or equity kickers
- Board observation rights
- Financial covenants and reporting
- Priority in the payment waterfall behind only senior secured debt

**Hard Money / Bridge Lending:**

Occasionally appears in SMB M&A when:
- The buyer needs to close quickly and SBA processing is too slow
- The deal doesn't qualify for SBA or conventional (distressed acquisition)
- Short-term bridge to expected permanent financing (bridge until SBA closes)
- Rate: 12%–18%, term 6-24 months, interest-only

### 2.6 Working Capital and the Working Capital Peg

Working capital in an M&A context refers to the current operating assets (accounts
receivable, inventory, prepaid expenses) minus current operating liabilities (accounts
payable, accrued expenses, customer deposits). It represents the cash invested in
day-to-day operations that the business needs to function.

**The Working Capital Peg:**

Almost all SMB transactions are done on a "cash-free, debt-free" basis with a working
capital peg. This means:
- Cash on the balance sheet stays with the seller
- Debt on the balance sheet stays with the seller
- Working capital is delivered at a "normal" level at closing

The "peg" is the agreed-upon level of normal working capital, typically calculated as
the trailing 12-month average of net working capital (excluding cash and debt). The
idea: the buyer is buying a business that needs a certain amount of working capital
to operate, and the seller should leave that amount in the business at closing.

**How the Peg is Calculated:**

1. Calculate monthly net working capital for the trailing 12 months
   (Current assets - Current liabilities, excluding cash and debt each month)
2. Average the 12 monthly figures
3. Exclude outlier months (if one month has unusual seasonality)
4. The average is the peg

Example:
- Average A/R: $180,000
- Average inventory: $45,000
- Average prepaid expenses: $12,000
- Average A/P: $95,000
- Average accrued expenses: $42,000
- **Working capital peg: $100,000**

**The Working Capital True-Up:**

At closing, the actual working capital is compared to the peg:
- If actual working capital at closing is $115,000 and the peg is $100,000, the seller
  receives an additional $15,000 (they left more working capital than required)
- If actual working capital is $85,000, the buyer receives a $15,000 credit (the seller
  didn't leave enough working capital)

The true-up is typically calculated within 60-90 days after closing, once the actual
closing balance sheet is prepared and reviewed. Disputes are resolved by an independent
accountant whose decision is binding.

**Common Disputes:**

- Seller accelerates A/R collections before closing (reducing receivables) and delays
  paying vendors (increasing payables) to extract cash — the buyer ends up with less
  working capital than normal.
- Buyer argues certain receivables are uncollectible and shouldn't count.
- Disagreement on whether certain liabilities are "operating" (included in the peg)
  or "non-operating" (excluded).
- Seasonal businesses where the closing date significantly affects working capital
  levels.

**Practical Advice:**

Define working capital explicitly in the LOI — what's included, what's excluded,
how it's calculated, what period is used for the average, and how disputes are
resolved. The time to negotiate this is before signing the LOI, not at the closing
table when both sides are committed and anxious.

## SECTION 3: THE DEAL PROCESS — FROM FIRST CONVERSATION TO CLOSE

### 3.1 The Sell-Side Process

**Stage 1: Decision to Sell and Preparation (2-6 Months Before Market)**

The preparation phase is where most of the value is created or destroyed. A well-
prepared seller can command 10-20% higher multiples than an unprepared one.

Key preparation activities:
- **Get financials clean:** Reconcile P&L to tax returns. Identify and document every
  add-back with supporting evidence. Prepare 3 years of adjusted financial statements.
  If the books are messy, hire a bookkeeper or fractional CFO to clean them up. This
  alone can take 2-3 months.
- **Build the add-back schedule:** Document every add-back with a source document
  (receipt, contract, bank statement). A well-documented add-back schedule builds
  buyer confidence and speeds due diligence.
- **Assemble the data room:** Organize all documents a buyer will request — financial
  statements, tax returns, contracts, leases, employee records, customer lists,
  insurance policies, equipment lists, licenses and permits. Having this ready before
  going to market signals professionalism and can shorten the deal timeline by weeks.
- **Decide on process:** Direct deal (no broker), broker-led process, or investment
  banker. For businesses under $2M, a business broker is typical (commission: 8-12%).
  For $2M-$10M, an M&A advisor or lower-middle-market investment banker (commission:
  4-8% + minimum fee). For $10M+, a full-service investment banker.
- **Set realistic price expectations:** The #1 reason deals fail is price gap. Sellers
  who go to market at 5x when the market says 3x waste everyone's time and may end up
  selling for 2.5x after 18 months of frustration. Getting an independent valuation
  before going to market is a worthwhile investment.

**Stage 2: Marketing the Business (Weeks 1-8)**

- **Teaser creation:** A one-page anonymized summary of the business (industry, revenue
  range, geography, employee count, asking price). Distributed to potential buyers
  without revealing the business identity. Include enough detail to generate interest
  but not enough to identify the business.
- **NDA collection:** Interested buyers sign NDAs before receiving the CIM. Standard
  NDA duration: 2-3 years. Key provisions: non-solicitation of employees and customers,
  confidentiality of all information provided.
- **CIM preparation:** The Confidential Information Memorandum is the business's
  marketing document. For Main Street deals, 10-20 pages. For middle market, 30-60 pages.
  Includes: executive summary, business overview, financial performance, growth
  opportunities, management team, competitive advantages, and deal terms.
- **Management calls / site visits:** Serious buyers request a management call (usually
  30-60 minutes with the seller) and then a site visit. These are typically scheduled
  after the buyer has reviewed the CIM and expressed continued interest.
- **IOI collection:** Buyers who remain interested submit Indications of Interest —
  non-binding offers that state a price range, proposed structure, and financing approach.

**Stage 3: LOI Selection and Exclusivity (Weeks 8-12)**

Selecting the right IOI is a multi-dimensional decision:
1. **Price:** The obvious factor, but not always the most important
2. **Certainty of close:** Does the buyer have financing? Are they pre-approved?
3. **Terms:** Seller note size, transition period, non-compete scope
4. **Buyer quality:** Do they have M&A experience? Do they understand the industry?
5. **Timeline:** How quickly can they close? SBA adds 30-60 days.

Exclusivity (also called "no-shop period") is granted in the LOI. Typical period:
45-60 days, during which the seller agrees not to market the business or negotiate
with other buyers. Granting exclusivity too early or for too long gives the buyer
leverage — they know you can't talk to anyone else.

**Stage 4: Due Diligence (Weeks 12-20)**

DD is the period where the buyer verifies everything the seller has represented:
- Financial DD: Verify revenue, expenses, add-backs, working capital, debt
- Legal DD: Review contracts, litigation, regulatory compliance
- Operational DD: Visit facilities, meet key employees, review processes
- Tax DD: Review tax returns, identify tax risks, confirm no outstanding tax liabilities

Re-trades happen when DD reveals something that wasn't disclosed or was misrepresented.
The seller's best defense against re-trades is full disclosure upfront — surprises in
DD are the #1 cause of re-trades and deal failures.

**Stage 5: Closing (Weeks 20-24)**

- Purchase agreement negotiation: 2-4 weeks of attorney back-and-forth
- Escrow setup: Typically 5-10% of purchase price held in escrow for 12-18 months
  to cover indemnification claims
- Transition planning: Agreed-upon seller involvement post-close
- Final closing: Wire transfer of funds, document signing, key handover

The wire on closing day: the buyer's attorney collects all funds (SBA loan proceeds,
buyer equity, seller note documentation), the seller's attorney confirms receipt,
documents are recorded, and ownership transfers.

### 3.2 The Buy-Side Process

**Thesis Development:**

The most successful buyers start with a clear thesis before they start searching.
A thesis answers: What industry? What geography? What size? Why am I the right buyer?

Good thesis example: "I'm looking for a commercial HVAC company in Texas or Oklahoma,
doing $1M-$3M revenue, with at least 30% recurring maintenance revenue. I'm the right
buyer because I ran HVAC operations for 8 years and know how to optimize route density."

Bad thesis example: "I want to buy a small business somewhere in the Southeast."

**Sourcing — Where Serious Buyers Find Businesses:**

1. **Business brokers:** The primary channel for Main Street deals. Brokers list on
   BizBuySell, BusinessesForSale, DealStream. Build relationships with 5-10 brokers
   in your target geography and industry.
2. **Direct outreach:** Sending letters or making calls to business owners who aren't
   actively selling. Lower success rate but less competition. Works best in fragmented
   industries (HVAC, landscaping, cleaning).
3. **Professional networks:** CPAs, attorneys, wealth advisors who work with business
   owners considering retirement. They're often the first to know about exit intent.
4. **Industry associations:** Trade groups, industry conferences, and peer groups
   where business owners congregate. Useful for building relationships before a deal.
5. **Online platforms:** BizBuySell, BusinessBroker.net, Axial, DealStream — varies
   by deal size. Quality varies enormously.

**The First Call — What to Ask:**

In 30 minutes with a seller or broker, cover:
1. Why is the owner selling? (motivation and timeline)
2. How long has the business been operating? (stability)
3. Approximate revenue and owner compensation? (size and earnings)
4. How many employees and what does the owner do day-to-day? (transferability)
5. Is there a lease and what are the terms? (facility risk)
6. What percentage of revenue is from the top 3 customers? (concentration)
7. What makes this business defensible? (competitive advantage)

**Financing Pre-Approval:**

Getting SBA pre-approval before submitting LOIs is a competitive advantage. It tells
the seller: "I can close this deal. The financing is not speculative." Sellers strongly
prefer buyers with financing confidence, sometimes even accepting a slightly lower
price from a pre-approved buyer over a higher price from an unfinanced one.

### 3.3 Key Documents — What Every Document Is and Does

**NDA (Non-Disclosure Agreement):**
The gateway document. Signed before any confidential information is shared. Standard
provisions: 2-3 year confidentiality term, no solicitation of employees or customers,
return/destroy information if deal doesn't proceed. Watch for: overly broad definitions
of "confidential information" that could restrict the buyer's future activities,
non-compete provisions disguised as NDA terms, and one-sided enforcement mechanisms.

**CIM (Confidential Information Memorandum):**
The business's marketing document. A good CIM tells a compelling story supported by
data. A bad CIM is either too thin (5 pages of fluff) or too long (80 pages of padding).
Contents: executive summary, business description, financial performance (3+ years),
customer overview (without names), competitive analysis, growth opportunities,
management team, facility and equipment overview, deal terms, and asking price.

**IOI (Indication of Interest):**
A non-binding expression of interest from a buyer. Contains: proposed price range,
proposed deal structure (asset vs. stock, financing approach), timeline to close,
key conditions, and buyer qualifications. The IOI is NOT an offer — it's an invitation
to negotiate. Multiple IOIs allow the seller to compare buyers.

**LOI (Letter of Intent):**
The document that matters. Part binding, part non-binding. Binding provisions typically
include: exclusivity, confidentiality, and expense allocation. Non-binding provisions
include: purchase price, deal structure, working capital terms, reps and warranties
scope. A good LOI addresses all material terms so the purchase agreement is largely
a documentation exercise rather than a new negotiation.

**APA (Asset Purchase Agreement):**
The definitive agreement for asset sales. Key sections: definitions, purchase price
and payment terms, asset identification, assumed vs. excluded liabilities, representations
and warranties (seller represents facts about the business), indemnification (who pays
if representations turn out to be wrong), covenants (what each party must do/not do
between signing and closing), closing conditions, and termination provisions.

**SPA (Stock Purchase Agreement):**
Similar to APA but for stock/equity sales. Key difference: the buyer acquires the
entity, so all assets AND liabilities transfer. R&W scope is typically broader (seller
must represent the condition of the entire company, including liabilities the APA
buyer wouldn't assume). Indemnification is typically more protective for the buyer.

**Non-Compete Agreement:**
Prevents the seller from competing with the business after closing. Enforceability
varies by state, but general principles: must be reasonable in scope (geography,
activities), duration (typically 3-5 years for SMB M&A), and not broader than
necessary to protect the buyer's investment. Overly broad non-competes may be
unenforceable. Compensation for the non-compete is typically part of the purchase
price allocation (and is taxed as ordinary income to the seller, not capital gains).

**Employment/Consulting Agreement:**
Governs the seller's post-close involvement. Typical terms: 3-12 month transition
period, defined hours and responsibilities, compensation (usually $0 or modest stipend
for Main Street deals, meaningful salary for larger deals), scope of authority (limited
to training and introductions, not decision-making).

**Working Capital Statement:**
Defines what constitutes working capital for the deal, how the peg is calculated,
the true-up mechanism, and the dispute resolution process. Usually an exhibit to the
purchase agreement.

**Escrow Agreement:**
Governs the holdback — typically 5-10% of purchase price held by an escrow agent for
12-18 months. Released to the seller after the escrow period unless the buyer has
made indemnification claims. The escrow protects the buyer against breaches of
representations or unknown liabilities discovered post-closing.

## SECTION 4: DUE DILIGENCE — THE COMPLETE FRAMEWORK

### 4.1 Financial Due Diligence

Financial DD is the most critical workstream and the one that most often changes deal
terms or kills deals. Every finding must be classified and quantified.

**Quality of Earnings (QoE) Analysis:**

A QoE goes beyond the income statement to answer: "Is the reported earnings number
sustainable, repeatable, and growing?" Key analyses:
- Revenue recognition: Is revenue recorded when earned or when cash is received?
  Are there channel stuffing or bill-and-hold arrangements?
- Gross margin analysis: Are margins consistent, improving, or deteriorating? Why?
- Expense normalization: What's the true run-rate cost structure after removing
  one-time items and owner-related expenses?
- EBITDA quality score: What percentage of adjusted EBITDA is from recurring/predictable
  sources vs. one-time or volatile sources?

**Revenue Quality Assessment:**

Classify all revenue into buckets:
- Contractual/recurring (maintenance contracts, subscriptions): High quality
- Repeat but non-contractual (regular customers without contracts): Medium quality
- Project-based/one-time: Low quality
- Concentration risk: Any customer >10% of revenue needs individual analysis

**Customer Concentration Analysis:**

Calculate the Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) for customer concentration:
- Sum of squared market shares of each customer
- HHI < 1,500: Low concentration (good)
- HHI 1,500-2,500: Moderate concentration (acceptable with mitigation)
- HHI > 2,500: High concentration (material risk)

In practice, the simple metric matters most: what happens if your largest customer
leaves? If losing your #1 customer means revenue drops 30%, that's a deal risk that
must be priced.

**Cash Flow Analysis:**

Compare reported EBITDA to actual cash generation:
- Free Cash Flow = EBITDA - CapEx - Changes in Working Capital - Taxes
- If EBITDA is $500K but free cash flow is $200K, something is consuming cash
  (growing receivables, inventory build, deferred maintenance capex)
- Healthy conversion: FCF should be 70%+ of EBITDA for stable businesses

**Tax Return vs. P&L Reconciliation:**

Compare the income tax return (Schedule C, 1120, 1120S, or 1065) to the P&L for
each of the last 3 years. Differences reveal:
- Revenue not reported on tax returns (unreported cash income — a red flag)
- Expenses on P&L not on tax returns (or vice versa)
- Timing differences between accrual and cash basis
- Owner's personal expenses that don't appear on the P&L but are deducted on returns

**Bank Statement Analysis:**

12 months of bank statements compared to reported revenue:
- Monthly deposits should approximate reported monthly revenue
- Unexplained deposits need investigation (customer prepayments, loan proceeds,
  owner capital infusions, non-business income)
- Cash payments not flowing through the bank (unreported revenue)
- Auto-drafts and recurring payments revealing expenses not on the P&L

**AR/AP Aging:**

- Accounts Receivable aging reveals collection health. If 30%+ of AR is >60 days old,
  there may be collection problems or disputes. Days Sales Outstanding (DSO) above
  industry norms is a red flag.
- Accounts Payable aging reveals whether the business is paying vendors on time.
  If payables are being stretched (aging >60 days), the business may be using vendors
  as a financing source — which is unsustainable.

### 4.2 Legal Due Diligence

**Corporate Records:**
Confirm entity formation, good standing, authorized shares/members, operating agreement
or bylaws, board minutes (if applicable), and cap table. For LLCs, confirm the operating
agreement allows transfer of membership interests without unanimous consent.

**Contracts:**
Review all material contracts (typically >$50K annual value or strategic importance):
- Assignment provisions: Can the contract be assigned without consent? If not, is
  consent likely to be granted?
- Change of control triggers: Some contracts terminate or allow renegotiation upon
  change of ownership
- Term and renewal: How long is the contract? Auto-renewal or negotiable?
- Key customer contracts: What are the terms, termination provisions, and pricing

**Intellectual Property:**
Verify ownership of all IP: trademarks (registered vs. common law), patents, trade
secrets, proprietary software, domain names, social media accounts. Confirm no
third-party claims, no license agreements that restrict use, and that employee
inventions are assigned to the company via employment agreements.

**Litigation:**
Review all pending, threatened, and historical litigation. Assess potential financial
exposure. Even resolved cases can reveal patterns (employment disputes, customer
claims, contract breaches) that signal ongoing risk.

**Real Estate:**
Lease review is critical for businesses that depend on their location:
- Remaining term and renewal options
- Assignment clause (can the lease be assigned to the buyer?)
- Personal guarantee (does the current lease require the seller's personal guarantee?)
- Rent escalations and comparison to market rates
- Maintenance and repair obligations
- Exclusivity provisions (for retail locations)

**Employment:**
Review employment agreements, non-compete and non-solicitation agreements with key
employees, benefit plans, and any pending employment disputes. Identify employees
who might leave after the transition and assess the risk.

**Regulatory Compliance:**
Industry-specific licenses, permits, certifications, and regulatory requirements.
Verify transferability — some licenses are personal to the owner and cannot be
transferred (requiring the buyer to obtain their own, which may take months).

---

### 4.3 Operational Due Diligence

**Technology and Systems:**
Audit the technology stack: CRM, ERP, accounting software, industry-specific software,
website and ecommerce platform, communication tools. Assess: age of systems, adequacy
for current operations, scalability for growth, data migration requirements, and any
proprietary systems that create vendor lock-in.

**Equipment:**
Inventory all major equipment and vehicles. Assess: age, condition, remaining useful
life, replacement cost, maintenance history. Calculate capital expenditure requirements
for years 1-3 post-close. A fleet of trucks averaging 8 years old may need $200K+
in replacements within 24 months.

**Key Employees:**
Interview (or at least profile) key employees: tenure, compensation, role criticality,
flight risk, and development potential. The top 3-5 employees are often as important
to the acquisition's success as the financial performance.

**Vendor Relationships:**
Identify key vendors: who they are, contract terms, pricing stability, alternatives.
Vendor concentration (>30% of purchases from one vendor) creates dependency risk.

**Process Documentation:**
Evaluate the quality and completeness of SOPs, training materials, and operational
procedures. A well-documented business transfers more smoothly and retains more value.

### 4.4 Industry-Specific DD Frameworks

**Service Businesses (HVAC, Pest Control, Landscaping, Plumbing, Electrical):**
1. Recurring revenue percentage and contract terms (auto-renewal vs. annual)
2. Route density and geographic coverage (revenue per truck per day)
3. Technician turnover and licensing (are key techs licensed? will they stay?)
4. Vehicle and equipment condition (fleet age, maintenance records)
5. Seasonal revenue patterns and cash flow management

**Healthcare (Dental, Optometry, Veterinary, Physical Therapy):**
1. Provider credentialing and license transferability
2. Payer mix (insurance vs. cash vs. government) and reimbursement rates
3. Patient retention rates and active patient count
4. Facility compliance (ADA, OSHA, state health department)
5. Technology and equipment age (X-ray, chairs, lab equipment)

**Professional Services (Accounting, Law, Staffing, Consulting):**
1. Client retention rate and engagement duration
2. Revenue per professional and utilization rates
3. Key person dependency (which relationships walk out the door?)
4. Backlog and pipeline visibility
5. Professional liability insurance and claims history

**Manufacturing and Distribution:**
1. Customer concentration and contract terms
2. Equipment condition, capacity utilization, and maintenance capex
3. Supply chain risk (single-source suppliers, import dependency)
4. Inventory management (turns, obsolescence, valuation method)
5. Environmental compliance and remediation liabilities

**Software/SaaS:**
1. Net Revenue Retention (NRR) and gross/net churn
2. Customer acquisition cost (CAC) and lifetime value (LTV) ratio
3. Technical debt assessment and platform scalability
4. IP ownership and open-source license compliance
5. Hosting infrastructure and security posture

**Restaurants and Food Service:**
1. Lease terms (location is the business for most restaurants)
2. Health department inspection history
3. Liquor license transferability
4. Staff turnover rates (particularly kitchen staff)
5. Delivery platform dependency and margin impact

**Retail:**
1. Lease terms and foot traffic trends
2. Inventory composition and turnover
3. Ecommerce percentage and growth
4. Vendor relationships and margin stability
5. Customer acquisition and retention metrics

## SECTION 5: TAX STRATEGY IN M&A

### 5.1 Tax Structure Overview

The tax structure of a business sale directly impacts how much the seller keeps and
how much the buyer effectively pays. Tax planning should start 2-3 years before a
contemplated sale for maximum benefit.

**The Fundamental Tension:**

Buyers want asset sales because they get a step-up in tax basis — they can depreciate
and amortize the full purchase price, creating tax shields that improve after-tax
returns. This is worth 15-25% of the purchase price in present-value tax savings.

Sellers want stock sales because the entire gain is taxed at long-term capital gains
rates (currently 20% federal + 3.8% NIIT + state). In an asset sale, portions of the
gain allocated to inventory, non-compete agreements, and depreciation recapture are
taxed at ordinary income rates (up to 37% federal).

**Capital Gains Rates (2025-2026):**

- Long-term capital gains (assets held >1 year): 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income
  (most business sellers are in the 20% bracket)
- Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT): 3.8% surtax on investment income for high earners
- State capital gains rates: 0% (FL, TX, WA, NV, etc.) to 13.3% (CA)
- Short-term capital gains: Taxed as ordinary income (up to 37% federal)

**The Installment Sale Method:**

Under IRC Section 453, sellers who receive payments over time (seller notes, earnouts)
can recognize capital gains proportionally as payments are received rather than
recognizing the entire gain at closing. Benefits:
- Spreads tax liability over multiple years
- May keep the seller in a lower tax bracket each year
- Defers tax on seller-financed portions until principal is actually received
- Interest income on the note is taxed as ordinary income when received

**Allocation of Purchase Price — Where Each Party Wins and Loses:**

| Asset Class | Seller's Tax Treatment | Buyer's Benefit |
|------------|----------------------|-----------------|
| Goodwill | Capital gains (20% + NIIT) | Amortize over 15 years (Section 197) |
| Non-compete | Ordinary income (up to 37%) | Amortize over agreement term |
| Equipment | Depreciation recapture (ordinary) + capital gains | Depreciate (Section 179 or MACRS) |
| Inventory | Ordinary income | Cost basis (deducted when sold) |
| Real estate | Capital gains (with depreciation recapture) | Depreciate over 39 years (commercial) |
| Customer lists | Capital gains | Amortize over 15 years |
| Trade name | Capital gains | Amortize over 15 years |

**Tax Strategies for Sellers Before Going to Market:**

1. **C-corp to S-corp conversion:** S-corps avoid double taxation on asset sales, but
   the S-corp must hold the election for 5 years to avoid the Built-In Gains (BIG) tax.
   If the owner is planning to sell in 5+ years, converting now can save hundreds of
   thousands in taxes. If selling sooner, the BIG tax applies to the built-in gain
   at the time of conversion (at the corporate rate of 21%).

2. **QSBS (Qualified Small Business Stock) — Section 1202:** If the business is a
   C-corp, the stock was acquired at original issuance, and the company is a qualified
   small business (assets <$50M), the seller may exclude up to $10M or 10x their basis
   (whichever is greater) of capital gains from federal tax. This is one of the most
   powerful tax provisions available to business sellers and is often overlooked.

3. **Opportunity Zone reinvestment:** Sellers can defer and partially reduce capital
   gains by reinvesting sale proceeds into a Qualified Opportunity Zone Fund within
   180 days of the sale. The deferred gain is recognized when the investment is sold
   or by December 31, 2026 (whichever is earlier).

4. **Charitable Remainder Trust (CRT):** For sellers with estate planning goals, a
   CRT can receive the business stock before the sale, sell the stock tax-free (the
   trust is tax-exempt), invest the proceeds, and pay the seller an income stream for
   life. The remainder goes to charity at the seller's death. Combines tax deferral,
   income generation, and charitable giving.

**Tax Strategies for Buyers:**

1. **Section 179 expensing:** Buyers can immediately expense the cost of tangible assets
   (equipment, vehicles, furniture) up to $1.22M (2024 limit, adjusted annually for
   inflation). This creates a large first-year tax deduction.

2. **Bonus depreciation:** Allows additional first-year depreciation on qualifying
   assets. The rate has been phasing down: 100% through 2022, 80% in 2023, 60% in 2024,
   40% in 2025, 20% in 2026. Still valuable but less so than in prior years.

3. **338(h)(10) election:** As discussed above, allows stock purchase treated as asset
   purchase for tax purposes, giving the buyer the step-up in basis.

### 5.2 Estate Planning and Exit

Many business sellers — particularly Baby Boomers — have estate planning
considerations that intersect with their exit strategy.

**The Step-Up in Basis at Death:**

Under current law, when a person dies, their assets receive a "step-up" in tax basis
to fair market value at the date of death. This means if an owner dies holding $5M
in business equity with a $100K basis, the heirs receive the equity with a $5M basis
and can sell it immediately with zero capital gains tax. This creates a perverse
incentive to hold business equity until death rather than sell during lifetime.

Sellers considering a lifetime sale should compare: (a) the after-tax proceeds from
selling now, invested in a diversified portfolio, vs. (b) the economic benefit of
holding the business until death and passing it to heirs with a stepped-up basis.
Factor in: the risk of holding a concentrated, illiquid asset; the opportunity cost
of the owner's time; and the business's growth trajectory.

**Gifting Strategies Before a Sale:**

Owners can gift business equity to family members or trusts before a sale to shift
capital gains to lower-tax individuals. The annual gift tax exclusion ($18,000 per
recipient in 2024) and lifetime gift tax exemption ($13.61M in 2024) enable
significant wealth transfer. However, gifts of business interests require a qualified
appraisal, and the IRS scrutinizes gifts made shortly before a sale as potential
"step-transaction" abuse.

**Trusts and Family Limited Partnerships:**

FLPs and LLCs used for estate planning allow the owner to transfer equity to family
members at discounted valuations (minority interest and lack-of-marketability discounts
of 20-35% are common). This technique is being used less aggressively due to IRS
scrutiny, but remains valid when properly structured with real business purpose.

**When to Involve an Estate Attorney:**

Any seller with >$5M in total estate value should involve an estate attorney alongside
their deal attorney. The estate attorney can structure the sale to minimize both income
taxes and estate taxes — often saving 10-20% of total tax burden through techniques
the deal attorney wouldn't typically consider.

## SECTION 6: EXIT TYPES — DEEP MECHANICS

### 6.1 Full Sale / Complete Exit

The most common exit: the owner sells 100% of the business and walks away after a
transition period. Buyer universe includes individual operators, search fund operators,
PE add-on acquirers, and strategic buyers. Deal structure is typically an asset sale
with SBA financing (for deals under $5M) or conventional financing plus equity (for
larger deals). Seller note of 10-15% is standard.

Timeline: 6-12 months from going to market to closing. Tax treatment depends on entity
type and deal structure (asset vs. stock). The key negotiation points are price,
transition period length, non-compete scope, and seller note terms.

Common complications: owner psychological unreadiness (they've built this for 30 years),
key employee reactions to ownership change, customer concerns about continuity, and
the "identity crisis" many sellers experience post-close. Yulia's advice: prepare
for the emotional dimension as much as the financial dimension. Have a plan for what
you'll do after the sale — sellers with a clear "what's next" negotiate more rationally
and close more reliably.

### 6.2 Majority Sale (Partial Exit / PE Recapitalization)

The owner sells 60-80% of the business to a PE firm or strategic buyer, retains
20-40% equity, and continues operating. The "first bite" provides liquidity and
de-risks the owner's personal wealth. The "second bite" comes when the PE firm sells
the business (typically 4-7 years later), and the owner's retained equity participates.

Structure: Stock sale to PE buyer. Seller rolls equity into the acquiring entity
(usually a new holding company). Management incentive plan (MIP) provides additional
upside if performance targets are met.

Key negotiation: the terms of the roll — liquidation preferences, governance rights,
drag-along and tag-along provisions, and the management agreement. A 20% roll at the
wrong terms can be worth less than a 10% roll at good terms.

Timeline: 9-18 months. DD is more extensive because institutional buyers have teams
of analysts. Tax: the cash portion is taxed at closing; the rolled equity is tax-
deferred (no gain recognized until the second sale).

### 6.3 Capital Raise (Growth Equity)

Not a sale — the owner brings in investors to fund growth. The company issues new
equity (diluting the owner) in exchange for growth capital. Common for businesses with
demonstrated product-market fit and a clear use of funds.

Investor universe: growth equity funds, family offices, PE minority investors, angel
syndicates. Deal structure: preferred equity with liquidation preferences, anti-
dilution provisions, board representation, and information rights.

Key negotiation: valuation (pre-money and post-money), liquidation preferences (1x
non-participating preferred is standard; participating preferred or multiples >1x are
aggressive), anti-dilution mechanism (broad-based weighted average is standard; full
ratchet is aggressive), and board composition.

Timeline: 4-8 months from first investor meeting to close. Tax: no tax event for the
company or existing shareholders at the time of investment (new shares are issued, not
existing shares sold). The owner's basis in their equity doesn't change.

### 6.4 Partner Buyout

One partner buys out another's equity interest. Often triggered by disagreement,
retirement, divorce, or death. The buy-sell agreement (if one exists) typically governs
the process, price formula, and payment terms.

Structure: installment sale (seller-financed over 3-5 years) or SBA 7(a) loan taken
by the buying partner. Valuation is either per the buy-sell agreement formula or by
independent appraisal.

Key challenges: this is a relationship negotiation first and a financial negotiation
second. The parties must continue working together during the transition (and sometimes
after). Emotional dynamics frequently override financial logic. Yulia's advice: get
an independent valuation that both sides agree to, then negotiate structure and timing
around that number. Don't let the valuation itself become the battleground.

Timeline: 3-6 months if buy-sell agreement exists with a clear formula; 6-12 months
if negotiating from scratch. Tax: the selling partner recognizes capital gains on the
difference between sale price and their basis in the partnership.

### 6.5 Management Buyout (MBO)

Key employees or managers purchase the business from the owner, usually with
significant seller financing because the management team rarely has the capital for
a traditional acquisition. Often combined with SBA financing.

Structure: 10-20% equity from management team (often via personal savings, ROBS,
or HELOC), 60-70% SBA loan, 10-20% seller note. The owner may retain a small equity
position (5-10%) during the transition.

Key challenges: the management team is buying with very thin equity, creating high
leverage and DSCR pressure. The seller must be comfortable with significant seller
financing exposure. The transition is usually smoother than a third-party sale because
the buyers already know the business.

### 6.6 ESOP (Employee Stock Ownership Plan)

An ESOP is a qualified retirement plan that acquires company stock on behalf of
employees. The company borrows money (from a bank or the owner via seller note),
uses the loan to buy the owner's shares, and the shares are allocated to employee
accounts over time as the loan is repaid from company cash flow.

**ESOP Mechanics:**
1. The company establishes an ESOP trust
2. The trust borrows funds (SBA ESOP loan, bank loan, or seller note)
3. The trust uses the funds to purchase the owner's shares
4. The company makes annual contributions to the ESOP to repay the loan
5. As the loan is paid down, shares are allocated to employee accounts
6. Employees vest over time (typically 3-6 year cliff or graded vesting)

**Valuation Requirements:**
ESOP transactions require an independent appraisal by a qualified appraiser. The
trustee has a fiduciary duty to employees and cannot overpay. The valuation must
be at fair market value — no premium for control, no discount for minority (in the
initial transaction). This means ESOP prices are typically in line with strategic
sale value but not above it.

**Section 1042 Rollover:**
For C-corp sellers, Section 1042 allows the seller to defer capital gains tax
indefinitely by reinvesting ESOP sale proceeds into Qualified Replacement Property
(QRP) — essentially, publicly traded stocks and bonds of US operating companies.
The gain is deferred as long as the QRP is held (and can be eliminated entirely
if held until death, when the step-up in basis applies).

Requirements: the ESOP must own at least 30% of the company's stock after the
transaction, and the stock must be C-corp stock (S-corp sales don't qualify for
Section 1042).

**Feasibility Requirements:**
Not every company is a good ESOP candidate:
- Revenue: typically $5M+ (the costs of ESOP formation and administration are
  fixed costs that burden smaller companies)
- Cash flow: must be sufficient to service the ESOP loan (DSCR similar to SBA)
- Employee count: typically 20+ (enough employees to make the plan meaningful)
- Management team: must be capable of running the business post-transition
- Industry: stable, predictable cash flow businesses work best

**Timeline:** 12-18 months minimum. ESOP formation, trustee selection, valuation,
loan negotiation, IRS qualification, and closing all take longer than a traditional
sale.

**Financing:** SBA ESOP loans are available through the SBA 7(a) program. Banks also
lend directly to ESOP trusts. Seller financing is common (30-50% of deal value as a
seller note subordinated to the bank loan).

### 6.7 Asset Sale vs. Operating Business Sale

In some cases, the most valuable path is to sell the business's assets (customer list,
equipment, real estate, IP) separately rather than as a going concern. This is most
relevant when:
- The business is unprofitable but owns valuable assets
- The real estate is worth more than the operating business
- A strategic buyer wants specific assets (customer list, IP) but not the operations
- The business has liabilities that make a going-concern sale impractical

Structure is an asset purchase with specific identification of each asset being sold.
Price is negotiated asset-by-asset. No goodwill premium unless the assets together
generate earnings that exceed their individual values.

### 6.8 Structured Earnout / Staged Transition

A phased transaction where the owner sells a portion of the business initially and
the remainder over time, with the deferred portion often tied to performance. Common
in professional services (accounting, consulting) where client relationships must
transfer gradually.

Structure: 50-70% cash at closing, 30-50% paid over 2-3 years based on client
retention or revenue maintenance. The seller typically stays involved during the
earnout period to facilitate the transition.

Key risk: earnout disputes are the most litigated provision in M&A agreements. Both
parties must define the measurement methodology precisely and include independent
dispute resolution mechanisms.

## SECTION 7: NEGOTIATION — COMPLETE TACTICS AND PSYCHOLOGY

### 7.1 The Psychology of Business Sale Negotiations

Business sale negotiations are fundamentally different from other negotiations because
of the emotional weight involved. The seller isn't selling a commodity — they're selling
something they built, often over decades. Their identity, their employees' livelihoods,
and their financial future are all at stake.

**The Seller's Emotional Journey:**
- **Pride:** "This business is worth more than anyone could possibly offer." The initial
  anchor is always emotional, not financial. Sellers who built the business from nothing
  see value in every decision they made, every customer they won, every crisis they
  survived. The market doesn't pay for effort — it pays for results.
- **Fear:** "What if I sell too low? What if I can't find another purpose?" Fear of
  leaving money on the table and fear of post-sale identity loss are the two biggest
  emotional drivers for sellers.
- **Loss:** "I'm losing my baby." The transition from owner to former owner triggers
  genuine grief. Smart advisors acknowledge this rather than dismissing it.
- **Relief:** "It's finally done." By closing day, most sellers have been through
  months of stress and uncertainty. The dominant emotion is often relief, not celebration.

**The Buyer's Emotional Journey:**
- **Excitement:** "This is the one!" Buyers (especially first-time buyers) fall in
  love with businesses the way people fall in love with houses. Emotional attachment
  leads to overpaying and overlooking red flags.
- **Doubt:** "What if the numbers aren't real?" DD is when excitement gives way to
  scrutiny. Every finding amplifies doubt.
- **Anxiety:** "Am I making a $2M mistake?" The closer to closing, the higher the
  anxiety. "Buyer's remorse" before the wire transfer is extremely common.

**Anchoring Effects:**
The first number on the table has an outsized influence on the final outcome. Research
consistently shows that the anchor — even when both parties know it's arbitrary —
pulls the final outcome toward it. Sellers should always anchor high (but within the
range of defensibility), and buyers should anchor low (but not so low they're not
taken seriously). The party who names the first number typically gets an outcome 10-15%
closer to their anchor.

**Information Asymmetry:**
The seller knows everything about the business; the buyer knows very little. This
asymmetry starts at maximum and narrows through DD. The seller's advantage is early
(when negotiating the IOI/LOI); the buyer's advantage is late (when DD findings
provide leverage for re-trades). Smart sellers disclose voluntarily to maintain trust
and avoid late-stage surprises that kill deals.

---

### 7.2 Negotiating the IOI

**The First Number:**
In a competitive process, the first IOI submitted sets the tone. Bid too low and you're
eliminated. Bid too high and you overpay. The sweet spot: 85-95% of asking price with
strong terms (pre-approved financing, minimal conditions, short DD timeline).

**When to Bid at Ask vs. Under Ask:**
- At or above ask: When there are 5+ serious buyers, the business is in a hot industry
  (HVAC, pest control, PE-consolidation targets), and you genuinely want to win.
- 5-10% below ask: Standard negotiation posture for most deals. Shows interest while
  preserving room to negotiate.
- 15-25% below ask: Only when the asking price is clearly above market based on
  comparable transactions, or when you've identified significant issues that justify
  the discount.
- >25% below ask: Likely to be dismissed unless you have a compelling rationale and
  the business has been on market for 6+ months.

**Differentiating Your IOI Beyond Price:**
- Include a personal letter explaining why you're the right buyer (not just the highest bidder)
- Reference your financing pre-approval (SBA conditional approval letter)
- Propose a specific, realistic timeline with milestones
- Demonstrate industry knowledge and operating experience
- Address the seller's known concerns (transition, employees, legacy)

---

### 7.3 Negotiating the LOI

**The 10 Most Commonly Negotiated LOI Terms:**

1. Purchase price (obviously)
2. Deal structure (asset vs. stock)
3. Working capital peg and definition
4. Seller note terms (amount, rate, term, standby)
5. Exclusivity period (duration and conditions)
6. Non-compete scope (geography, duration, activities)
7. Transition period (length, seller's role, compensation)
8. Earnout terms (if applicable: metric, period, cap)
9. Escrow terms (percentage, duration, release conditions)
10. Representations and warranties scope (survival period, baskets, caps)

**Non-Compete Negotiations:**
- Geography: Should match the business's actual market. A local HVAC company's
  non-compete shouldn't cover the entire state — 50-mile radius is more reasonable.
- Duration: 3-5 years is market. 7+ years is aggressive. Under 2 years provides
  limited protection.
- Activities: Should cover the specific industry and role, not all business activity.
  A restaurant seller shouldn't be banned from opening a retail store.
- Compensation: The purchase price allocation to the non-compete is ordinary income
  for the seller. Sellers want to minimize this allocation; buyers want to maximize it.

**Exclusivity Period:**
Market standard is 45-60 days. Buyers want 90 days (more time, no competition).
Sellers should resist anything beyond 75 days unless the buyer has financing in hand
and a specific reason for the extra time. Include a breakup fee if the buyer walks
for non-DD reasons.

### 7.4 Negotiating During Due Diligence

**The Three-Step Finding Presentation:**

When presenting DD findings to the seller:

1. **State the finding factually:** "We found that your largest customer, representing
   22% of revenue, is on a month-to-month contract with a 30-day termination notice."
2. **Quantify the impact:** "If that customer were to leave, the business would lose
   approximately $330K in annual revenue, reducing SDE by an estimated $115K."
3. **Propose the resolution:** "We'd like to address this by reducing the purchase
   price by $200K or by structuring a 12-month earnout tied to that customer's retention."

This approach is professional, specific, and gives the seller a path forward. Dumping
a list of 20 findings at once overwhelms the seller, creates an adversarial dynamic,
and often kills deals.

**Which Findings Justify Re-Trading:**
- Revenue is materially lower than represented (>5% variance from trailing 12 months)
- Customer concentration is worse than disclosed
- Key employee is planning to leave (discovered during DD)
- Lease terms are different from represented
- Pending litigation was not disclosed
- Equipment condition is significantly worse than represented

**Which Findings Do NOT Justify Re-Trading:**
- Minor discrepancies in expense categorization
- Known issues that were already priced into the deal
- Industry-standard risks that any informed buyer should expect
- "Findings" that are really just the buyer having second thoughts

---

### 7.5 BAFO (Best and Final Offer) Tactics

**When to Call for a BAFO:**
The seller (or their advisor) calls a BAFO round when:
- Multiple buyers have submitted IOIs or LOIs
- At least 2 buyers are credible and competitive
- The seller wants to create urgency and finality

**How to Run a BAFO:**
1. Notify all qualified buyers that the process is moving to BAFO
2. Set a specific deadline (typically 5-7 business days)
3. Provide clear instructions on what the BAFO should include
4. Do NOT reveal competitive positions or terms from other buyers
5. Evaluate BAFOs on the full package: price, terms, certainty, and timeline

**Buyer Tactics in a BAFO:**
- Differentiate on certainty: "Our financing is fully committed and we can close in 30 days"
- Include a personal touch: a letter to the seller explaining your vision for the business
- Show you've done your homework: reference specific aspects of the CIM or site visit
- Don't lowball — the BAFO is not the place to start negotiating from a low anchor
- Include a short exclusivity period in your BAFO (shows confidence)

## SECTION 8: DEAL RISK — WHAT KILLS DEALS AND HOW TO PREVENT IT

### 8.1 Seller-Side Deal Killers

**Financial Misrepresentation Discovered in DD:**
The #1 deal killer. When the buyer's accountant finds that revenue was overstated,
expenses were understated, or add-backs were fabricated, the deal dies immediately.
Prevention: honest, documented financials from day one.

**Owner Revealed as the Business:**
When DD reveals that the owner handles all sales, all key customer relationships, and
all critical decisions, buyers recognize that they're buying a job, not a business.
The revenue may not survive the transition. Prevention: begin delegating and building
a management team 2-3 years before selling.

**Lease Doesn't Transfer:**
If the landlord refuses to assign the lease or demands unfavorable terms for a new
lease, the buyer may not be able to operate at the current location. Prevention:
review your lease assignment clause before going to market. If it requires landlord
consent, secure preliminary agreement early.

**Critical License Not Transferable:**
Licenses and permits that are personal to the owner (contractor licenses, healthcare
provider credentials, government clearances) cannot be sold. The buyer must obtain
their own, which may take months or may not be possible. Prevention: verify
transferability of all licenses before going to market.

**Customer Concentration Worse Than Disclosed:**
If the seller represented "diversified revenue" but DD reveals that 35% comes from
one client, the buyer re-trades or walks. Prevention: disclose concentration upfront
and explain the mitigation (contract terms, relationship depth, historical stability).

**Pending Litigation Not Disclosed:**
Undisclosed lawsuits discovered in DD destroy trust and kill deals. Even if the lawsuit
is meritless, the failure to disclose signals dishonesty. Prevention: disclose all
litigation (pending, threatened, and historical) in the CIM or during early discussions.

**Key Employee Quits During DD:**
If the business's star salesperson, lead technician, or critical manager gives notice
during DD, the buyer questions whether the business will survive the transition.
Prevention: incentivize key employees to stay (retention bonuses, equity participation)
and have succession depth.

**Seller Gets Emotionally Attached and Won't Close:**
The most heartbreaking deal killer: the seller agrees to terms, goes through months
of DD, and then can't bring themselves to sign. They realize they don't know who they
are without the business. Prevention: counseling and preparation. Sellers need a plan
for their life after the sale.

### 8.2 Buyer-Side Deal Killers

**Financing Falls Through:**
SBA denial, bank pulls commitment, or the buyer's personal financial situation changes.
Most common reasons: credit issues discovered during underwriting, appraisal comes in
low, or lender gets cold feet on the industry. Prevention: work with an experienced
SBA lender, get pre-approved before submitting the LOI, and have backup financing options.

**Buyer Can't Close Equity Injection:**
The buyer committed to 10% equity but their HELOC was denied, their ROBS rollover hit
a snag, or their savings aren't as liquid as they thought. Prevention: verify equity
sources before submitting the LOI. Have documentation ready.

**Due Diligence Takes Too Long:**
Exclusivity expires and the seller starts talking to other buyers. The deal loses
momentum. Prevention: hire DD professionals (accountant, attorney) before the LOI is
signed. Have a detailed DD plan with milestones and deadlines.

**Buyer's Spouse/Partner Vetoes:**
More common than people admit. The buyer's spouse or business partner who wasn't
involved in the process decides they don't support the acquisition. Prevention:
involve all stakeholders from the beginning. Their input matters.

**Post-LOI Cold Feet:**
The closer the wire, the bigger the anxiety. Buyer's remorse before closing is
extremely common, especially for first-time buyers. Prevention: experienced advisors
who normalize the anxiety and keep the process moving.

### 8.3 Structural Deal Killers

**Valuation Gap Too Wide:**
After DD findings adjust the price downward, the gap between what the seller will
accept and what the buyer will pay may be unbridgeable. Prevention: realistic pricing
upfront and incremental negotiation throughout the process.

**Working Capital Dispute:**
The seller drained working capital before closing (collected all receivables, stopped
paying vendors) and the buyer discovers the business needs a cash infusion on day one.
Prevention: define working capital explicitly in the LOI with true-up mechanisms.

**Non-Compete That Seller Won't Accept:**
The buyer demands a broad non-compete (5 years, nationwide) and the seller refuses.
Prevention: negotiate non-compete scope in the LOI, not in the purchase agreement.

### 8.4 External Deal Killers

**Interest Rate Shock:**
Rates increase between LOI and closing, blowing up the DSCR and making financing
unavailable at the agreed terms. Prevention: rate locks where available, or structure
with floating-rate assumptions and a DSCR cushion.

**Key Customer Leaves During DD:**
The timing is terrible but it happens. A major customer decides to switch providers
during the 3-month DD process. Prevention: sellers should maintain or strengthen
customer relationships during the sale process. Do not check out early.

## SECTION 9: MARKET INTELLIGENCE — CURRENT STATE 2025-2026

### 9.1 Overall Market Conditions

The SMB M&A market in early 2026 is best characterized as "selective recovery." Deal
count is approximately 80-85% of the 2021 peak, having rebounded from the 2023 trough
when interest rate shock froze many transactions. Multiples have compressed 10-15% from
2021 highs across most sectors but remain above pre-pandemic (2019) levels.

**Key trends:**
- PE dry powder remains near all-time highs ($1.2T+ globally), pushing multiples higher
  in consolidation-target industries (HVAC, dental, pest control, veterinary)
- Baby Boomer exit wave is accelerating: approximately 10,000 business owners per day
  reach retirement age, creating a supply-side tailwind for buyers
- Credit conditions have stabilized but remain tighter than 2019-2021
- SBA lending volume has recovered to approximately 90% of 2021 levels
- Seller note prevalence has increased from ~55% to ~65% of deals as higher rates
  force creative structuring
- Quality businesses (clean books, growth, low owner-dependency) are still commanding
  premium multiples and selling quickly (30-60 days)
- Marginal businesses (messy books, declining, high owner-dependency) are sitting on
  market 6-12+ months and selling at significant discounts

### 9.2 Financing Environment

**Current SBA 7(a) Rates:**
WSJ Prime Rate + 2.75% for loans >$50K with terms >7 years. As of March 2026,
all-in rates are approximately 10.5%–11.5% depending on the lender and borrower profile.

**DSCR Headwinds:**
At 11% all-in rate on a 10-year SBA loan, monthly payments per $1M borrowed are
approximately $13,775 ($165,300 annual debt service). At 8% (three years ago), the
same $1M loan had $12,133 monthly payments ($145,600 annual). The 14% increase in
debt service means businesses need 14% more cash flow to qualify for the same loan
amount.

**Impact on deal structures:**
- More seller notes (larger percentages, longer terms) to reduce the SBA loan amount
- Shorter SBA loan terms (7 years instead of 10) for better rates in some cases
- More equity injection from buyers (15-20% instead of minimum 10%)
- Increased use of ROBS (Rollover for Business Startups) for equity injection
- More creative structures: combined SBA + conventional + seller note

**SBA Lender Appetite by Industry:**
- Favored: Essential services (HVAC, plumbing, pest control), healthcare, professional
  services with recurring revenue
- Neutral: Manufacturing, distribution, staffing, commercial cleaning
- Cautious: Restaurants (unless established with stable cash flow), retail (unless
  ecommerce-enabled), fitness/gym (post-pandemic caution)
- Challenging: New/trendy concepts, single-location food service, businesses with
  <2 years operating history

### 9.3 Industry-Specific Market Conditions

**Essential Services (HVAC, Pest Control, Plumbing):**
PE consolidation is in full swing. Multiples have expanded to 4-6x EBITDA for
platform-quality businesses (>$2M EBITDA). Even Main Street deals (2.5-3.5x SDE)
are competitive because PE-backed strategics are buying add-ons at these levels.
The Silver Tsunami is creating abundant supply of Boomer-owned service businesses.
Buyer competition: Dense. First-time buyers compete with PE-backed operators.

**Healthcare Services (Dental, Veterinary, Optometry):**
Dental and veterinary practices are the most actively consolidated healthcare
categories. DSO (Dental Service Organization) activity has compressed practice
availability. Veterinary is following the same path 3-5 years behind dental.
Multiples: 5-8x EBITDA for quality practices. SBA financing: strong for independent
practitioners. Regulatory environment: stable but credentialing requirements add
complexity.

**Professional Services (Accounting, Staffing, IT/MSP):**
Accounting firms trade on revenue multiples (0.8-1.5x) with strong transition
planning. IT/MSP is increasingly attractive to PE (4-6x EBITDA for managed services
with recurring revenue). Staffing is cyclical and currently cautious as employment
markets normalize from post-pandemic tightness.

**Manufacturing:**
Reshoring tailwinds are real but selective. Government incentives (CHIPS Act, IRA)
benefit specific sub-sectors. Multiples: 4-6x EBITDA for well-run manufacturers
with diversified customers. Equipment condition and capex requirements are critical
DD areas. SBA lenders approach manufacturing cautiously (asset-heavy, cyclical).

**Technology/SaaS:**
Significant multiple compression from 2021 peak (down 40-60% for sub-$10M ARR
companies). Current range: 3-6x ARR for growth businesses, 2-4x for stable/declining.
Rule of 40 (growth rate + profit margin ≥ 40%) still commands premium. Buyers are
more disciplined: unit economics and path to profitability matter more than pure growth.

**Restaurants:**
Credit headwinds and rising costs (labor, food, rent) have compressed multiples and
slowed transactions. Established multi-unit operators with proven concepts still trade
at 3-5x EBITDA. Single-location restaurants are challenging to sell — most trade at
2-2.5x SDE or below. Ghost kitchens and delivery-first models are attracting some
buyer interest.

**Retail:**
Lenders remain cautious. Ecommerce-enabled retail with omnichannel presence trades
at 3-4x SDE. Pure brick-and-mortar without ecommerce is difficult to finance and
sell. Specialty retail with defensible niches (hobby, outdoor, pet) performs better
than general merchandise. Lease terms are the make-or-break factor for most retail
acquisitions.
