## Section 1: THE ACQUISITION THESIS FRAMEWORK

### Why a Specific Thesis Beats a Generic Search

Buyers with a specific acquisition thesis close deals 70% faster than buyers who "just want to buy a business." Here's why:

1. **Focus accelerates evaluation.** When you know you want an HVAC business in Texas with $1M–$3M revenue, you can evaluate an opportunity in 30 minutes instead of 30 days.

2. **Brokers take you seriously.** A buyer who says "I want to buy a business" gets ignored. A buyer who says "I'm looking for a pest control company in the Southeast with $800K+ SDE and 65%+ recurring revenue" gets introduced to real deals.

3. **Financing is easier.** SBA lenders want to see industry knowledge and a clear thesis. "I'm buying a business" doesn't get pre-approved. "I'm buying a home services company where I'll apply my 15 years of operations management experience" does.

4. **Negotiation leverage improves.** When sellers see you understand their industry, they trust you more. Trust translates to better terms, smoother transitions, and post-close cooperation.

5. **Integration risk decreases.** The #1 cause of post-acquisition failure is buying a business you don't understand how to operate. A thesis grounded in your actual capabilities prevents this.

### The Four Dimensions of Thesis Development

**Dimension 1: Industry**
What type of business? Industry determines: multiple range, buyer competition, financing options, operational complexity, growth trajectory, and PE exit potential. Yulia helps narrow from 35 industries to 3–5 candidates.

**Dimension 2: Geography**
Where? Geography determines: labor market, customer base, regulation, cost structure, and competitive dynamics. For SBA buyers, you need to be within driving distance of the business (2-hour radius max). For PE-backed or semi-absentee, broader geography is possible.

**Dimension 3: Size**
How big? Revenue range determines: league classification, valuation multiples, financing options, and buyer pool. The relationship between size and multiple is non-linear — bigger businesses trade at higher multiples (the PE arbitrage).

**Dimension 4: Buyer Advantage**
Why you? What makes YOU the right buyer for THIS business? Industry experience, management skills, financial engineering capability, geographic presence, or strategic synergy. Your advantage determines which deals you can win and which you'll operate successfully.

### The "Unfair Advantage" Test

Before finalizing a thesis, every buyer should pass this test:

**Ask yourself:** "Why will I operate this business better than the current owner or another buyer?"

**Strong answers:**
- "I spent 12 years managing HVAC operations at a larger company — I know how to scale a service business"
- "I'm a CPA who has advised dental practices for 20 years — I understand the economics better than any buyer"
- "I run pest control routes in Atlanta and want to add routes in Savannah — I have the infrastructure"

**Weak answers:**
- "I've been a software engineer and want to try running a real business" (no industry expertise)
- "I have money and want passive income" (no operating advantage)
- "I just want to be my own boss" (motivation, not advantage)

A weak answer doesn't disqualify you, but it should shape your thesis toward simpler, more systematized businesses where operational complexity is lower.

### When to Broaden vs. Narrow Your Thesis

**Broaden if:**
- You've been searching for 6+ months with no viable targets in your niche
- Your geographic constraint eliminates 90%+ of opportunities
- Your industry target has very few sellers (tight supply)
- You're flexible on industry and can apply your skills broadly

**Narrow if:**
- You're seeing 20+ opportunities per month and can't evaluate them all
- You're making offers on businesses you don't fully understand
- Brokers are sending you everything instead of curated opportunities
- You're spreading your DD resources across too many targets

**The sweet spot:** 3–5 target industries, 2–3 target geographies, clear revenue range. This generates a manageable pipeline of 3–8 qualified opportunities per quarter.

## Section 3: BUYER TYPE FIT MATRIX

### 1. First-Time Buyer, Own Capital ($150K–$500K equity, no M&A experience)

**Best industries:** Commercial cleaning, laundromat, residential cleaning, pool service (Sun Belt), small HVAC ($500K–$1.5M revenue), small franchise

**Red lines:** No manufacturing, no healthcare (unless licensed), no staffing (WC intensity), no trucking (complexity), no SaaS (no SBA)

**Financing approach:** SBA 7(a) is your vehicle. 10% equity injection, 10-year term. Get pre-qualified before searching. Your equity range supports deals from $1.5M–$5M total.

**Typical timeline:** 6–12 months from search start to close

**Common mistakes:**
- Falling in love with the first business they see
- Not getting SBA pre-qualified before making offers
- Underestimating working capital needs post-close
- Buying a business that requires skills they don't have
- Skipping legal and accounting representation to save money

### 2. First-Time Buyer, SBA-Financed ($300K–$800K equity plus SBA)

**Best industries:** HVAC, plumbing, pest control, dental (if dentist), auto repair, insurance agency, CPA firm, IT/MSP, childcare

**Red lines:** Nothing over $8M total deal value (SBA cap), no businesses where SBA won't lend (real estate investment, SaaS without assets)

**Financing approach:** SBA 7(a) with possible SBA 504 for real estate component. At $500K equity, you can acquire businesses up to $5M. Consider ROBS (Rollover for Business Startups) to use 401(k) as equity injection.

**Typical timeline:** 6–12 months (longer with SBA processing)

**Common mistakes:**
- Choosing an SBA lender inexperienced in business acquisitions (most are real estate lenders)
- Underestimating SBA standby seller note requirements
- Not understanding that personal guarantee means unlimited personal liability
- Expecting 45-day close when SBA takes 90+

### 3. Experienced Operator Buying Second Business

**Best industries:** Adjacent or same industry as first business. The add-on acquisition strategy. Home services operators adding plumbing to HVAC, MSP operators adding another MSP, insurance agents adding another book.

**Red lines:** Don't diversify into an industry you don't understand just because you've "done this before"

**Financing approach:** Combination: SBA for the new acquisition if not maxed, conventional bank lending with existing business as partial collateral, seller financing

**Typical timeline:** 4–8 months (faster — you know the process)

**Common mistakes:**
- Assuming second acquisition is the same as first (every business is different)
- Over-leveraging: using first business's cash flow to service second business's debt
- Management attention split: who runs business #1 while you integrate business #2?

### 4. Corporate Executive / Professional (High income, limited operating experience)

**Best industries:** Insurance agency (semi-absentee after transition), franchise (built-in operations manual), commercial cleaning (manageable operations), CPA firm (if accounting background), IT/MSP (if tech background)

**Red lines:** Avoid anything requiring hands-on trade skills (HVAC, plumbing, auto repair) unless you plan to hire and retain skilled operators from day one

**Financing approach:** Strong personal income supports SBA application. HELOC and savings provide equity. Consider partnerships with experienced operators.

**Typical timeline:** 8–14 months (longer search due to learning curve)

**Common mistakes:**
- Buying a "cool" business instead of a profitable one (restaurants, breweries, fitness studios)
- Underestimating the lifestyle transition from corporate salary to business ownership
- Not building a personal financial runway (6–12 months of living expenses set aside)
- Trying to impose corporate management practices on a small business

### 5. Search Fund Operator (Finance background, seeking large first acquisition)

**Best industries:** Business services, IT/MSP, insurance, niche manufacturing, specialized healthcare services — businesses with $2M–$10M revenue, $500K–$2M EBITDA

**Red lines:** Avoid commodity businesses with no differentiation. Search funds need growth stories for investor returns.

**Financing approach:** Search fund investor capital (typically $400K–$600K search budget) plus deal equity from investors plus SBA or conventional debt. Total buying power: $5M–$20M.

**Typical timeline:** 12–24 months (search phase is long)

**Common mistakes:**
- Falling for "proprietary deal flow" without independent valuation
- Letting search timeline pressure drive them to bad deals
- Underestimating post-close operational complexity (finance background ≠ operational experience)
- Over-paying because investor capital reduces personal risk perception

### 6. Serial Acquirer (Closed 2+ deals, seeking add-ons or new platforms)

**Best industries:** Whatever you know. Your advantage is experience and infrastructure. Focus on industries where your operational playbook transfers.

**Red lines:** Don't acquire businesses outside your integration capability. More acquisitions ≠ better outcomes.

**Financing approach:** Existing lender relationships, possibly PE partnership for capital. Seller notes are powerful currency ("I close deals; here are my references from previous sellers").

**Typical timeline:** 3–6 months per acquisition (experienced, fast movers)

**Common mistakes:**
- Integration fatigue: doing too many deals too fast
- Assuming every business operates like the last one
- Neglecting existing businesses while chasing new acquisitions
- Overpaying based on synergy assumptions that don't materialize

### 7. PE-Backed Strategic (Owns platform, buying add-ons)

**Best industries:** Home services, healthcare practices, IT/MSP, insurance, staffing — any industry with your existing platform

**Red lines:** Add-ons must integrate operationally. Geographic adjacency matters for service businesses. Cultural fit with existing team is critical.

**Financing approach:** Platform leverage (existing debt facility), PE equity co-investment, seller notes. Budget: essentially unlimited within fund parameters.

**Typical timeline:** 2–4 months per add-on (streamlined process)

**Common mistakes:**
- Integration overwhelming platform operations team
- Paying "platform" multiples for add-on quality businesses
- Assuming culture integration is automatic
- Neglecting organic growth at platform while chasing acquisitions

### 8. Family Office (Patient capital, long hold, not extractive)

**Best industries:** Any essential service with stable cash flows. Insurance, home services, healthcare, childcare, funeral homes. Industries with demographic tailwinds and recurring revenue.

**Red lines:** Avoid high-growth/high-risk sectors. Family offices value stability over returns.

**Financing approach:** All equity or low leverage. Long hold period (10+ years). Focus on cash flow yield, not exit multiple expansion.

**Typical timeline:** Variable. No time pressure. Patient.

**Common mistakes:**
- Applying institutional PE playbook (leverage, cost-cutting) to a patient-capital strategy
- Not having operational management in place (family offices typically don't operate)
- Overpaying because "we're long-term" doesn't change day-one economics

### 9. International Buyer (Foreign national acquiring US business)

**Best industries:** Same as domestic — but stick to industries where cultural communication challenges are minimal. Avoid relationship-heavy businesses (insurance, CPA, healthcare).

**Red lines:** SBA requires US residency. E-2 visa requires treaty country citizenship and substantial investment. EB-5 has different requirements.

**Financing approach:** Conventional financing (not SBA unless permanent resident). Higher equity requirement (typically 30%–50% down). Some seller financing.

**Typical timeline:** 12–18 months (immigration and financing add complexity)

**Common mistakes:**
- Assuming SBA is available (it requires US permanent residency)
- Underestimating cultural adjustment in managing US employees
- Not engaging immigration attorney early in the process
- Buying businesses dependent on relationships they can't replicate

## Section 4: MACRO SIGNALS AND THEIR DEAL IMPLICATIONS (March 2026)

### Interest Rate Environment
**Current state:** Fed funds rate at 4.25%–4.50% after gradual cuts from 2024 peak. SBA 7(a) rates approximately 10.5%–11.5% (prime + 2.75%).

**Deal implications:**
- DSCR remains the binding constraint for most SBA deals. At current rates, a $1M SBA loan requires approximately $170K annual debt service, meaning DSCR 1.25x requires $213K+ SDE/EBITDA.
- Seller notes at 5%–6% are effectively below-market, making them cheaper than SBA or conventional debt. This creates incentive for creative seller financing.
- Rate cuts expected in 2026 will gradually improve DSCR, expanding the pool of businesses that qualify for SBA financing.

### PE Dry Powder
**Current state:** Estimated $2.5T+ in uninvested PE capital globally. Middle market and lower middle market funds have record dry powder.

**Deal implications:**
- PE platforms are competing aggressively for quality assets, driving up multiples in consolidating industries.
- PE's cost of capital is lower than SBA/individual buyers, giving them structural bidding advantage.
- The "PE arbitrage" (buy at 4x, sell at 8x through platform building) remains the dominant wealth creation strategy in SMB M&A.
- Individual buyers can still compete in deals below $2M where PE doesn't play.

### Baby Boomer Ownership Transfer
**Current state:** 10,000 Baby Boomers retire per day. Estimated 2.3M business owners in the transition zone (age 55+).

**Deal implications:**
- Supply of quality businesses for sale will increase steadily through 2030.
- Industries with highest Boomer ownership (plumbing, electrical, manufacturing, funeral homes, auto repair) will see the most supply.
- Not all Boomers are ready to sell — many are semi-retired and not motivated until health or exhaustion forces the issue.
- The businesses that WILL sell are increasingly well-prepared (brokers, M&A awareness up significantly).

### Credit Environment
**Current state:** SBA lending stable but cautious. Conventional lending moderately tightened from 2022–2023 but easing.

**Deal implications:**
- SBA lenders favoring: essential services, recurring revenue models, asset-backed businesses
- SBA lenders cautious about: restaurants, retail, highly seasonal, startup-adjacent
- Conventional lenders requiring: higher equity (25%+), stronger DSCR (1.5x+), shorter terms
- Alternative lenders (revenue-based, fintech): available but expensive (15%–25% effective rate)

### Labor Market
**Current state:** Unemployment at 4.1%. Skilled trades shortage persists. Minimum wage increases in 20+ states.

**Deal implications:**
- Labor-intensive businesses (cleaning, landscaping, childcare, home healthcare) face margin pressure from wage increases.
- Skilled trades shortage (HVAC, plumbing, electrical, machinist) creates natural moat for businesses with retained teams.
- Businesses with strong employee retention are more valuable than ever — proven ability to recruit and retain is a competitive advantage.
- Buyer's operational plan must include a labor strategy (compensation, benefits, culture) from day one.

### Technology Disruption
**Current state:** AI adoption accelerating across all industries. Remote work normalized for office-based businesses.

**Deal implications:**
- AI is NOT disrupting the core operations of most SMB service businesses (your plumber still needs to show up). But AI IS changing marketing, scheduling, customer communication, and back-office operations.
- Businesses that have adopted technology (CRM, scheduling, digital marketing) are more valuable than those operating with pen-and-paper.
- Remote/virtual delivery expanding addressable market for professional services (CPA, consulting, marketing, IT/MSP).
- eCommerce continues to grow but at normalized rates (not pandemic acceleration).

### Commercial Real Estate Impact
**Current state:** Office vacancy rates elevated (18%+). Industrial/warehouse demand strong. Retail stabilizing in suburban locations.

**Deal implications:**
- Businesses dependent on foot traffic (retail, salon, gym) face location risk from changing real estate dynamics
- Industrial/warehouse-based businesses (manufacturing, 3PL) benefit from strong demand for their facility type
- Owner-occupied commercial properties add tangible asset value to business acquisitions
- Sale-leaseback as transaction financing tool: seller sells real estate to investor, leases back to business, uses proceeds as part of deal structure
- Lease negotiations increasingly favor tenants in retail/office markets, creating opportunity for businesses to lock in favorable terms

### Regulatory Environment
**Current state:** Increased FTC scrutiny on non-competes (proposed ban in 2024 partially blocked). State-level minimum wage increases continuing. OSHA enforcement increasing.

**Deal implications:**
- Non-compete enforceability varies dramatically by state — structure accordingly
- Minimum wage increases directly compress margins for labor-intensive businesses (cleaning, childcare, food service)
- Regulatory compliance costs create barriers to entry that benefit established businesses
- Privacy regulations (state-level data privacy laws) affecting technology and healthcare businesses
- SBA lending rules stable but program availability depends on congressional authorization

### Industry Convergence and Platform Plays
**Current state:** Multi-trade home services platforms combining HVAC + plumbing + electrical. Healthcare platforms combining dental + ortho + specialty. Professional services combining CPA + wealth management + insurance.

**Deal implications:**
- "Platform premium" — businesses that can serve as a platform for multi-service expansion trade at 1.0x–2.0x EBITDA premium
- Cross-sell economics are real: HVAC customer who also needs plumbing and electrical = 3x LTV
- Platform building requires management infrastructure (CFO, HR, operations) that individual businesses don't have
- The platform playbook: buy one quality business, professionalize management, add 2–4 bolt-ons, sell the combined platform at 2–3x the per-unit entry multiple

## Section 4B: DEAL ECONOMICS — UNDERSTANDING THE MATH

### What a $2M SBA Deal Actually Looks Like

**Assumptions:**
- Business: HVAC company, $800K revenue, $300K SDE
- Purchase price: $900K (3.0x SDE)
- SBA 7(a) loan: $810K (90%)
- Buyer equity: $90K (10%)
- SBA rate: 11.0% (Prime + 2.75%)
- Term: 10 years

**Monthly economics:**
| Item | Monthly | Annual |
|------|---------|--------|
| SDE (what the business generates) | $25,000 | $300,000 |
| SBA debt service | -$11,168 | -$134,016 |
| Owner salary replacement | -$8,333 | -$100,000 |
| **Remaining cash flow** | **$5,499** | **$65,984** |
| DSCR | 1.87x | — |

**Year 1 buyer economics:**
- Salary: $100,000 (from business cash flow)
- Additional cash flow: $65,984 (available for growth investment, reserves, or distributions)
- Equity building: $60,000+ in principal paydown on SBA loan
- Business value growth: if business grows 10%, value increases $90K (at same multiple)

**5-year wealth creation:**
- Total salary received: $500,000
- Loan principal paid down: ~$350,000
- Business value appreciation (10% annual growth): $450,000+
- **Total wealth created: $1.3M+ from $90K initial investment**

This is why SBA-financed acquisition entrepreneurship is the most accessible path to wealth creation in America.

**Assumptions:**

- Purchase price: $3.6M (6.0x EBITDA)

- Seller note: $540K (15%, on standby)

| **Remaining cash flow** | **$3,875** | **$46,500** |
| DSCR (senior only) | 1.49x | — |

**5-year wealth creation (platform strategy):**
- Add 3 bolt-on acquisitions ($500K–$1M each) at 3–4x EBITDA
- Grow combined EBITDA to $1.5M through organic + acquisitions
- Exit platform at 8x EBITDA = $12M enterprise value
- Less debt paydown: net equity value ~$8M
- Buyer's 5% equity = $400K from $180K investment (2.2x cash-on-cash)
- But if buyer negotiated 15% equity (common for operating partners): $1.2M from $180K (6.7x)

## Section 5: THE "WHAT SHOULD I BUY?" CONVERSATION FRAMEWORK

### Yulia's 7-Step Thesis Development Process

**Step 1: Background Assessment (3 Key Questions)**
1. "What's your professional background — where have you spent your career?"
2. "Do you want to operate the business day-to-day, manage a team, or invest semi-passively?"
3. "What's your risk tolerance — conservative (steady cash flow), moderate (growth opportunity), or aggressive (high-growth but uncertain)?"

**Step 2: Capital and Financing Clarity**
1. "How much equity do you have available for this investment? Include cash, retirement (if willing to use ROBS), and any home equity you'd tap."
2. "Are you comfortable with SBA financing, which requires a personal guarantee?"
3. "What annual income do you need from this business in year one?"

**Step 3: Lifestyle Requirements**
1. "Are you willing to relocate or do you need to stay in your current metro?"
2. "Active operator (50+ hours/week), working owner (40 hours/week), or semi-absentee (10–20 hours/week)?"
3. "Any industries that are off-limits for personal, ethical, or lifestyle reasons?"

**Step 4: Industry Shortlist Generation**
Based on Steps 1–3, Yulia generates 3–5 industry candidates with rationale:
"Based on your operations background, $400K in equity, desire for an active operator role in the Southeast, and moderate risk tolerance, here are my top recommendations:

1. **HVAC** — Your operations experience transfers directly. $400K equity supports $3M–$4M deal via SBA. Strong PE exit potential in 5 years.
2. **Plumbing** — Similar thesis to HVAC but earlier in consolidation cycle = lower entry multiples.
3. **Commercial Cleaning** — Lower complexity, strong recurring revenue, faster path to semi-absentee.

I'd deprioritize: manufacturing (too operationally complex for first-time), SaaS (no tech background), healthcare (requires license)."

**Step 5: Industry Validation**
For each shortlisted industry, Yulia provides:
- Current market conditions (from Industry Intelligence)
- Realistic deal examples at their price point
- Key operational challenges they should understand
- Financing fit (SBA appetite for this industry)
- What they'd need to learn before making an offer

**Step 6: Thesis Statement Refinement**
Move from "I want to buy a business" to a specific thesis:

"I'm looking for an HVAC company in the Southeast (GA, FL, SC, NC, TN) doing $1.5M–$3.5M in revenue with at least 25% recurring maintenance contracts, a team of 8+ technicians, and an owner who is ready to transition within 12 months. I have $400K in equity and SBA pre-qualification for up to $3.5M."

**Step 7: Search Criteria and Deal Parameters**
Document specific search filters:
- Industry: HVAC (NAICS 238220)
- Geography: GA, FL, SC, NC, TN
- Revenue: $1.5M–$3.5M
- SDE: $350K+ (to support SBA at current rates)
- Recurring revenue: 25%+ from maintenance contracts
- Employees: 8+
- Owner transition: willing to stay 6–12 months
- Asking price: $1.2M–$3.0M
- Max I'll pay: $3.2M with strong terms

### Off-Market Sourcing (Where the Best Deals Are)

70% of the best businesses are never publicly listed. They sell through:

1. **Direct outreach** — Identify target businesses and send personalized letters/emails expressing interest. Response rate: 2%–5%. But the 2% who respond are genuine sellers.

2. **Business broker relationships** — Build relationships with 3–5 brokers in your target industries. They see deals before they hit public markets. Visit them, have coffee, prove you're a real buyer.

3. **Industry networks** — Join industry associations, attend conferences, talk to suppliers and distributors. They know who's thinking about selling.

4. **Professional advisors** — CPAs, attorneys, financial advisors, and wealth managers advise business owners. Let them know you're a buyer. They refer clients who mention "getting out."

5. **LinkedIn** — Identify and connect with business owners in your target industry/geography. Build relationships before proposing transactions.

6. **Proprietary search** — Hire a buy-side advisor or intermediary to run a targeted search on your behalf. Cost: $20K–$50K retainer plus success fee. Worth it for $3M+ acquisitions.

## Section 8: POST-ACQUISITION VALUE CREATION — THE FIRST 90 DAYS

### The Playbook Every Buyer Should Follow

**Day 1: Stabilize**
- Meet every employee individually. Reassure them. Address their #1 fear (job security).
- Don't change anything on Day 1. Your job is to listen, not fix.
- Contact top 10 customers personally. Introduce yourself. Ask how service can improve.
- Shadow every role for at least 2 hours. Understand how the business actually works.

**Days 2–14: Understand**
- Map all processes: how does revenue get generated? How does work get scheduled? How do customers get billed?
- Identify the "shadow org chart" — who actually runs things vs who has the title?
- Review every vendor contract and subscription. Eliminate redundancies.
- Inventory all technology: what's being used, what's not, what's missing?
- Meet with the departing seller daily. Extract all tribal knowledge.

**Days 15–30: Quick Wins**
- Implement the 3 highest-ROI improvements the seller wasn't doing (usually: raise prices, fire bad customers, fix the website).
- Set up financial reporting: monthly P&L, weekly cash flow, daily revenue dashboard.
- Formalize customer feedback: Google review strategy, Net Promoter Score.
- Review compensation: is your team fairly paid relative to market?
- Document the top 5 processes that exist only in the seller's head.

**Days 30–60: Strengthen**
- Hire for any critical gap identified in first 30 days.
- Implement technology the business should have had (CRM, scheduling, accounting).
- Build your 12-month operating plan with quarterly milestones.
- Begin transition of any remaining seller responsibilities.
- Review and renegotiate vendor contracts (you'd be surprised what 10 minutes of negotiation saves).

**Days 60–90: Accelerate**
- Launch growth initiative (new service offering, marketing channel, customer segment).
- Establish KPI dashboard and weekly management meeting cadence.
- Complete seller transition (seller moves to "on-call" availability only).
- Review staffing plan for next 12 months.
- Evaluate add-on acquisition opportunities (if platform strategy).

### The Most Common Post-Acquisition Mistakes

1. **Changing too much too fast.** The business works. Don't break it trying to improve it in the first month.
2. **Cutting costs before understanding them.** Every expense exists for a reason. Understand the reason before cutting.
3. **Ignoring employee concerns.** Your employees are scared. Address their fears explicitly and early.
4. **Losing key customers during transition.** Personal outreach to top customers in Week 1 is non-negotiable.
5. **Fighting with the seller post-close.** The seller knows things you don't. Treat them well even if the earnout creates tension.
6. **Under-investing in the business.** The worst buyers squeeze margins immediately. The best buyers invest in growth and reap returns in Years 2–5.
7. **Not having a financial reporting system on Day 1.** If you can't measure it, you can't manage it. Set up reporting immediately.

## Section 10: FINANCING OPTIONS COMPARED — YULIA'S GUIDE

### SBA 7(a) Loan
**Best for:** Individual buyers acquiring businesses $500K–$5M
**Down payment:** 10% of total project cost
**Rate:** Prime + 2.75% (currently ~11.0%)
**Term:** 10 years (25 years if real estate included)
**Max loan:** $5M
**Personal guarantee:** Required (unlimited)
**Key advantage:** Lowest equity requirement, longest term, most accessible
**Key disadvantage:** Personal guarantee, slow processing (75–120 days), standby seller note
**Eligibility:** US citizen or permanent resident, 650+ credit, industry experience helpful, business must meet SBA size standards

### SBA 504 Loan
**Best for:** Acquiring businesses with significant real estate or fixed asset component
**Down payment:** 10% buyer equity
**Structure:** 50% from bank (conventional), 40% from SBA (CDC debenture), 10% buyer
**Rate:** Bank rate on 50% portion, SBA debenture rate on 40% (typically below SBA 7a rate)
**Term:** 10 years (equipment), 20/25 years (real estate)
**Key advantage:** Lower rate on real estate component, longer real estate term
**Key disadvantage:** More complex, requires real estate or heavy equipment, two lenders to coordinate
**Eligibility:** Same as SBA 7(a); business must occupy 51%+ of the property

### Conventional Bank Loan
**Best for:** Experienced buyers, larger deals ($2M+), repeat acquirers
**Down payment:** 20%–30%
**Rate:** Prime + 1%–3% (currently ~9%–11%)
**Term:** 5–7 years (often shorter than SBA)
**Max loan:** Varies by bank and borrower
**Personal guarantee:** Usually required
**Key advantage:** Faster processing (30–60 days), fewer SBA restrictions, more flexible terms
**Key disadvantage:** Higher down payment, shorter term, higher DSCR requirement (1.5x+)

### Seller Financing (as primary financing)
**Best for:** When bank financing isn't available or when seller is motivated
**Down payment:** 20%–50% (negotiated)
**Rate:** 5%–8% (negotiated)
**Term:** 3–7 years (negotiated)
**Key advantage:** Flexible terms, no bank qualification, seller has skin in the game
**Key disadvantage:** Seller may require higher price to compensate for financing risk, personal guarantee from buyer, seller has security interest in business

### Revenue-Based Lending / Alternative Lenders
**Best for:** SaaS, eCommerce, businesses without hard assets
**Down payment:** Varies (often requires 30%+ equity)
**Rate:** 12%–25% effective annual rate
**Term:** 2–5 years
**Key advantage:** Accessible for businesses SBA won't finance (tech, online)
**Key disadvantage:** Expensive, short term, cash flow intensive debt service
**Providers:** Lighter Capital, Clearco, Pipe, various fintech lenders

### ROBS (Rollover for Business Startups)
**Best for:** Using 401(k)/IRA funds as equity injection without early withdrawal penalty
**How it works:** Form C-corp → create 401(k) plan → roll existing retirement into new plan → new plan purchases C-corp stock → C-corp uses funds for acquisition
**Amount:** Whatever is in your retirement accounts ($50K–$500K+ typically)
**Tax consequence:** No early withdrawal penalty, no income tax at rollover
**Key advantage:** Converts retirement savings into acquisition equity, avoids 10% early withdrawal penalty
**Key disadvantage:** Requires C-corp structure, complex compliance, DOL scrutiny risk, lost retirement diversification
**Yulia's advice:** ROBS is a legitimate tool but creates concentration risk (all your eggs in one basket). Use it to supplement other equity sources, not as your only equity.

## Section 11: DEAL FAILURE RECOVERY — WHAT TO DO WHEN A DEAL FALLS APART

### The Psychology of Deal Failure

50% of LOIs don't result in a closed deal. This is NORMAL. Don't let one failed deal end your acquisition search.

### Common Failure Reasons and Recovery Actions

**Financing denied:**
- Recovery: Ask lender specifically why. Fix the issue. Apply to different lender.
- Often fixable: different lender, different deal structure, larger down payment
- If SBA denied: try a different SBA lender (they have different appetites)

**DD found material issues:**
- Recovery: Validate that you walked for the right reason. Were you too cautious?
- Apply lessons: refine your pre-DD screening to catch these issues earlier
- The seller may fix the issue and re-list — stay in touch

**Seller backed out:**
- Recovery: This usually means the seller wasn't truly ready. Not your fault.
- Stay professional: the seller may come back in 6–12 months when they're truly ready
- Move on quickly: emotional energy spent on a reluctant seller is wasted

**Price gap couldn't be bridged:**
- Recovery: Were your expectations realistic? Check comparable data.
- Consider whether you need to adjust your thesis to target differently-priced businesses
- The right business at the wrong price is the wrong business

**Better buyer appeared:**
- Recovery: Strengthen your buyer profile. Get SBA pre-qualified. Write better IOIs.
- Speed matters: the buyer who moves fastest with confirmed financing wins
- Personal connection matters: sellers choose buyers they trust, not just the highest bidder
