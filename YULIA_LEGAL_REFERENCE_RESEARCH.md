## R.2 The Regulatory Line — smbx.ai's Software-Side Boundary

The architecture rests on **Section 15(a) of the Exchange Act** and the six-factor SEC test for "engaged in the business of effecting transactions in securities for the account of others." **Transaction-based compensation is the single most determinative factor.** This dictates four non-negotiable product rules:

1. **Pure SaaS subscription pricing.** Never tie fees to deal closing, capital raised, or transaction value. A success fee — even a small one — imports the entire broker analysis. *In re Neovest, Inc.*, Exchange Act Rel. No. 92291 (June 29, 2021), confirms that even passive software platforms can be charged as unregistered brokers when transaction-based comp is present.
2. **Yulia drafts; the user sends.** Every investor- or counterparty-facing artifact must be drafted *for* the user, sent *by* the user, under the user's name. This anchors the user as the speaker under Rule 3a4-1's issuer-personnel safe harbor.
3. **No custody, no negotiation-on-behalf-of, no soliciting specific investors for specific deals.** These are factors 3, 4, and 5 of the broker test. Each, even in isolation, can be sufficient.
4. **Disclaim adviser/fiduciary status persistently.** "Yulia is not your attorney, broker, investment adviser, fiduciary, financial planner, or M&A broker." Never use the term "AI advisor" or describe Yulia in fiduciary language. AI-washing enforcement (*In re Delphia*, *In re Global Predictions*, March 2024) reinforces that public claims about AI capabilities are themselves SEC enforcement targets under §17(a) and Marketing Rule 206(4)-1.

## R.3 The Defer-to-Counsel Triggers — Master Categories

Yulia must defer to a licensed attorney whenever any of the following are operative:

**A. Drafting that becomes the operative legal instrument.** Definitive purchase agreements; legal opinions; merger certificates; closing certificates; novation agreements; waivers of claims; releases. Yulia can produce a *first draft scaffold* but the executed instrument must be lawyer-reviewed and lawyer-finalized.

**B. Opining on enforceability for specific facts.** Whether *this* non-compete is enforceable, whether *this* MAE has occurred, whether *this* indemnification claim is preserved, whether *this* §220 demand has a proper purpose, whether *this* §15(b)(13) exemption covers *this* deal.

**C. Providing legal advice on a specific situation.** Recommending a specific course of action with legal consequences (vs. surfacing options); interpreting case law as applied to user's facts; opining on litigation strategy.

**D. Negotiating on behalf of the user with counterparties.** Yulia generates options and language; the user negotiates. Yulia never speaks to the counterparty as agent.

**E. Tax characterization of specific items.** §1202 QSBS qualification opinions; §382 NUBIG/NUBIL studies; QSub timing analysis; §280G shareholder-vote process; debt-vs-equity characterization; rollover §351/§721 structuring.

**F. Securities exemption opinions.** Whether a particular instrument is a security; whether a §4(a)(2) offering qualifies; whether bad-actor disqualification applies; §15(b)(13) eligibility opinion.

**G. Fiduciary-duty determinations.** Whether a stockholder is a "controller" (common-law or SB 21 statutory); whether MFW conditions were imposed *ab initio*; Revlon trigger analysis on borderline facts; Caremark adequacy assessments.

**H. Industry-specific clearance opinions.** HSR threshold close calls; Item 4(c)/(d) responsiveness; CFIUS TID determinations; Stark Law / Anti-Kickback FMV opinions; FOCI mitigation instrument selection; Form A insurance approvals; FAR 42.12 novation strategy.

**I. Privileged investigations and litigation matters.** Joint-defense / common-interest agreement scope; books-and-records demand responses; settlement strategy; mootness-fee negotiations.

**J. Multi-jurisdictional opinions.** Cross-border deals; multi-state non-compete enforceability; multi-state privacy law compliance; multi-state premerger notifications.

# PART 1 — TRANSACTION STRUCTURES & TAX [DOMAINS A & I]

## 1.1 The Three Architectures

| Structure | Liabilities | Consents | Tax | Vote |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Asset purchase** | Buyer takes only what is assumed; 5 successor-liability exceptions (de facto merger, mere continuation, fraudulent transfer, product line, substantial continuity) | Each contract, lease, license, IP, permit | Buyer cost basis (§1012); seller gain asset-by-asset; §1060 residual allocation | Seller §271 if "substantially all" (Gimbel v. Signal Cos., 316 A.2d 599) |
| **Stock purchase** | All target liabilities ride along | Change-of-control clauses in material contracts | Buyer cost basis in stock; carryover inside basis; §382 NOL limit | None at target; buyer NYSE/Nasdaq 20% rule |
| **Statutory merger** (§251 / §251(h) / §253) | All by operation of law (§259) | None mechanical (DGCL); change-of-control still relevant | Type A reorg if continuity-of-interest met (≥40% stock consideration) | Both constituents §251(c); appraisal §262 subject to market-out |

**Triangular variants.** Forward triangular (§368(a)(2)(D)): Target merges into Sub; ≥40% acquiror stock; "substantially all" test. Reverse triangular (§368(a)(2)(E)): Sub merges into Target; voting stock for ≥80%; preserves Target identity (contracts, licenses). Most common structure in friendly public deals.

**§251(h) two-step.** Eliminates back-end vote; requires public listing or ≥2,000 holders, any-and-all tender offer, no §203 interested-stockholder. Cleansing tender = *Corwin* vote (*In re Volcano*, Del. 2017).

## 1.2 §338(h)(10) and §336(e) Elections

**§338(h)(10)** (Treas. Reg. §1.338(h)(10)-1): joint buyer/seller election to treat qualified stock purchase as deemed asset sale; Form 8023 by 15th day of 9th month after acquisition. Eligible: S corps and consolidated subsidiaries. **All S-corp shareholders** must consent. ADSP/AGUB allocated under §1060 residual method. Drives 8–12% incremental tax to seller (recapture) — typically grossed up.

**§336(e)** (Treas. Reg. §1.336-1): seller-only election (no buyer consent); same eligibility as §338(h)(10) but buyer can be partnership/LLC/individual. Used in PE acquisitions of S-corps where buyer is a fund.

**F-reorganization (§368(a)(1)(F))** (Treas. Reg. §1.368-2(m); Rev. Rul. 2008-18): pre-transaction restructure for S-corp targets. Step 1: shareholders contribute Target stock to NewCo; Step 2: NewCo files Form 8869 QSub election. Target becomes disregarded; convert to LLC; PE acquires LLC interests for tax-asset treatment with rollover capability. **Default structure for closely-held S-corp targets in modern PE transactions.** QSub election must precede LLC conversion (PLR 201724013 trap).

## 1.3 §1060, §197, Bonus Depreciation, §179 — Post-OBBBA

**§1060 residual method** (Treas. Reg. §1.338-6): seven asset classes (I–VII); buyer/seller file consistent Form 8594. Allocation negotiated — sellers prefer goodwill (capital, no recapture); buyers prefer §1245 personal property (5/7-year MACRS, 100% bonus eligible).

**§197 amortization:** 15-year straight-line for acquired intangibles (goodwill, customer lists, workforce, trademarks, covenants not to compete). **Anti-churning §197(f)(9):** disallows amortization if asset held by related party (>20% common ownership) on/after July 25, 1991 — critical trap in PE rollover deals.

**§168(k) bonus depreciation (post-OBBBA, P.L. 119-21, July 4, 2025):** **100% permanently restored** for property acquired/placed in service after Jan 19, 2025. Notice 2026-11 (Jan 14, 2026) provides transition guidance. Property under binding contract pre-Jan 20, 2025 follows TCJA phase-down (40%/2025, 20%/2026). New §168(n) Qualified Production Property (manufacturing real estate) 100% expensing 2025–2031.

**§179 (post-OBBBA):** $2.5M deduction (2025); $4M phase-out threshold; full phase-out at $6.5M; permanently indexed.

**State conformity:** CA, NY, NJ, NC, PA, MA decouple — diligence state-by-state.

## 1.4 §1202 QSBS — Post-OBBBA Expansion

**Pre-OBBBA (stock issued before July 5, 2025):** $50M gross-asset cap; greater of $10M or 10× basis exclusion; 5-year hold required for 100% exclusion.

**OBBBA (issued after July 4, 2025):** **$75M gross-asset cap;** **$15M or 10× basis exclusion;** **tiered exclusion** (50%/3yr, 75%/4yr, 100%/5yr); AMT preference eliminated. **No-resetting:** pre-OBBBA stock cannot convert to post-OBBBA terms via §351 or reorganization (§1223 tacking).

## 1.5 §382 NOL Limitations

§382(a) limitation = FMV of old loss corp × long-term tax-exempt rate (monthly published; ~3.8–4.4% in 2025). Triggered by >50pp ownership change among 5%+ shareholders over 3-year testing window. Continuity of business enterprise required for 2 years (Treas. Reg. §1.382-7) — failure reduces limit to zero. NUBIG/NUBIL adjustments under Notice 2003-65 (modified by Notice 2018-30).

## 1.6 Earnout Tax

**Default §453 installment method.** Treas. Reg. §15A.453-1(c) three computation rules: stated maximum selling price; fixed period; default 15-year. **§453A interest charge** on installment obligations >$5M (universal at lower middle market+). **§483/§1274** imputed interest at AFR if deferred >6 months. **§1245 recapture** recognized in full year of sale regardless of payments. Election out under §453(d) is irrevocable.

## 1.7 Other Critical Tax Provisions

**§1374 BIG tax:** 5-year recognition period for C-to-S converters; corporate-level tax on net recognized built-in gain; standard reps require seller to confirm S-status validity >5 years. **§1411 NIIT:** 3.8% on passive gains; active business participation excludes. **§1061 carry:** 3-year hold for LTCG; OBBBA did not change. **§83(b):** 30-day jurisdictional deadline for rollover equity. **§280G/4999:** see Employment Domain. **§1031:** real property only post-TCJA.

Every acquisition agreement decomposes into: (1) preamble/recitals; (2) purchase mechanics; (3) reps and warranties; (4) covenants; (5) closing conditions; (6) indemnification; (7) termination; (8) miscellaneous. Yulia indexes by these.

## 2.2 The Standard Rep Set (Private-Target M&A)

**Fundamental reps (longer survival, higher cap, often no basket/materiality scrape):** corporate existence; authority/enforceability; capitalization/title; no brokers; tax (sometimes); FCPA increasingly.

## 2.5 MAE / MAC — The Akorn Framework

Two-tier definition + carve-outs (industry, market-wide, force majeure, **pandemics post-AB Stable**, change in law, deal-announcement effects). Disproportionate-impact exception returns risk to seller.

**Required showing** (Del.): durational significance ("years not months" — *In re IBP*, 789 A.2d 14); magnitude (*Akorn v. Fresenius*, 2018 WL 4719347 — 86% EBITDA drop, 5 quarters, regulatory fraud; first MAE finding); buyer bears heavy burden; carve-outs are seller-friendly defaults.

**AB Stable VIII v. MAPS Hotels** (Del. 2020/2021, COVID): pandemic fell within "calamities" carve-out (no MAE), but seller's *response* breached **ordinary course covenant** — buyer terminated on covenant breach. Ordinary course covenants now analytically distinct, lower threshold than MAE.

## 2.6 Earnouts and Specific Performance

**Earnout traps:** *Lazard Tech Partners v. QinetiQ*, 114 A.3d 193 (Del. 2015) — "intent" standard requires subjective bad faith; *Winshall v. Viacom*, 76 A.3d 808 (Del. 2013) — no implied duty to maximize; *Fortis Advisors v. J&J*, 2024 (Del. Ch.) — extracontractual fraud survives without explicit anti-reliance; *Airborne Health v. Squid Soap* — implied covenant inapplicable when express provision controls.

**Specific performance:** Delaware strong tradition (*In re IBP*; *Hexion v. Huntsman*, 965 A.2d 715). *Genuine Parts v. Essendant* (Del. Ch. 2019): termination fee not exclusive remedy where willful breach. Texas more restrictive. Modern agreements stipulate damages inadequacy + waive bond.

## 2.7 Working Capital True-Ups

90% of 2025-Study deals include PPA. Mechanics: peg → estimated → final → objection (30 days) → negotiation → independent accountant ("expert not arbitrator"). *Chicago Bridge & Iron v. Westinghouse*, 166 A.3d 912 (Del. 2017) — PPA mechanism is **exclusive** for true-up scope; cannot be used to litigate historic GAAP non-compliance (that goes through indemnification subject to cap). *Northern Data v. Riot Platforms* (Del. Ch. 2025) — accountant exceeded scope on items presenting R&W legal questions.

## 2.8 Fraud Carve-Outs — The ABRY Line

*ABRY Partners V v. F&W Acquisition*, 891 A.2d 1032 (Del. Ch. 2006) — sophisticated parties may bar negligent/reckless misrepresentation but **cannot** disclaim **intentional contractual fraud**. Anti-reliance clauses bar **extra-contractual** fraud only with both seller's disclaimer + buyer's affirmative non-reliance. *RAA Mgmt v. Savage Sports*, 45 A.3d 107 (Del. 2012); *Online HealthNow v. CIP OCL*, 2021 WL 3557857 (Del. Ch. 2021) — exclusive-remedy alone insufficient to bar extra-contractual fraud.

NY: *Danann Realty v. Harris*, 5 N.Y.2d 317 (1959) — disclaimers must be subject-matter specific. CA Civ. Code §1668: cannot exempt intentional fraud — broader than DE.

## 2.9 Deal Document Library

NDA (residuals clause; standstill); LOI (binding sections — exclusivity, expense, confidentiality; *SIGA Tech v. PharmAthene*, 67 A.3d 330 (Del. 2013) — non-binding LOI can create enforceable obligation to negotiate in good faith); Disclosure Schedules (post-SB 313 §268 — not part of merger agreement for §251 purposes); Voting/Support Agreements (capped <40% to avoid coercion under *Omnicare v. NCS Healthcare*, 818 A.2d 914); TSA (6–12 months, cost-plus 5–15%); IP assignments; Bills of sale; Closing certificates (officer's, secretary's, FIRPTA, §280G); Funds flow; Legal opinions (TriBar standards — existence, due authorization, enforceability, no conflicts; bankruptcy and equitable principles exceptions).

## 2.10 Forum, Arbitration, Damages

DGCL §115 exclusive Delaware forum (*Boilermakers v. Chevron*, 73 A.3d 934); federal-forum 1933 Act provisions (*Salzberg v. Sciabacucchi*, 227 A.3d 102 (Del. 2020)); FAA preemption (*AT&T Mobility v. Concepcion*, 563 U.S. 333; *Epic Systems v. Lewis*, 138 S. Ct. 1612). **Implied covenant** (Delaware narrow — *Nemec v. ## 3.2 SAFEs and Convertible Notes

SEC has stated SAFEs **are securities** (Investor Bulletin, May 2017). Y-Combinator post-money SAFE dominant (~90% pre-seed, 64% seed per Carta Q1 2025). Core terms: valuation cap, discount (10–25%), MFN, conversion on Equity Financing/Liquidity Event/Dissolution. Critical SEC-flagged risk: SAFE may **never convert** if no triggering event — material disclosure obligation. QSBS treatment of SAFEs uncertain — flag for tax counsel.

## 3.3 Anti-Fraud — Rule 10b-5 Always Applies

Applies to every securities transaction. Elements: material misrepresentation/omission + scienter (*Ernst & Ernst v. Hochfelder*, 425 U.S. 185) + connection with purchase/sale + reliance + economic loss + loss causation (*Dura Pharmaceuticals*, 544 U.S. 336). Materiality: *TSC v. Northway*, 426 U.S. 438; *Basic v. Levinson*, 485 U.S. 224. **Yulia must source-ground factual claims, flag forward-looking statements, prompt disclosure of risks, and avoid generating projections without disclaimers.**

## 3.4 The Broker-Dealer Line — Six-Factor Test

Section 15(a) prohibits unregistered broker activity. "Broker" (§3(a)(4)): "engaged in the business of effecting transactions in securities for the account of others." Six-factor test (*SEC v. Hansen*; *SEC v. Kramer*, 778 F. Supp. 2d 1320; *SEC v. Collyard*, 861 F.3d 760):

1. **Transaction-based compensation** — "the BIG ONE" (Brumberg Mackey & Wall NAL, 2010)
2. Holding oneself out as broker
3. Handling customer funds/securities
4. Involvement in negotiations
5. Recommending or providing advice
6. Prior securities-industry employment

**Rule 3a4-1** issuer's-associated-person safe harbor: protects natural persons associated with issuer (officers, directors, employees) who (a) are not statutorily disqualified, (b) **receive no transaction-based compensation**, (c) are not associated with a broker-dealer in prior 12 months, AND (d) meet one of three alternative conditions (regulated buyers; substantial non-securities duties + ≤1 offering per 12 months; written-only communications).

## 3.5 The §15(b)(13) M&A Broker Exemption

Effective March 29, 2023 (Consolidated Appropriations Act 2023). Covers privately-held company **transfer of control** transactions.

**Eligible privately-held company:** no §12-registered class AND (EBITDA <$25M OR revenue <$250M).

**Buyer must control AND be active in management** post-closing (≥25% control presumption federal).

**Critical limitations:**
- **Does NOT cover capital-raising / private placements** (the smbx.ai core use case).
- **Does NOT preempt state BD registration.** NASAA Model Rule (May 2024 amendment) state-by-state adoption patchy.
- No custody, no binding parties, no public-company transactions, no shell-company transactions.

**The 1985 Country Business NAL was withdrawn March 29, 2023.**

State IA registration <$100M AUM. Marketing Rule 206(4)-1 (effective Nov 2022): testimonials/endorsements with disclosure; no hypothetical performance to retail without resources/expertise.

**ESOP exception:** ≥51% ESOP exempts equity injection; retained-equity sellers must provide full unlimited 7(a) guaranty (statutory).

## 5.2 WARN — Federal & Mini

**Federal (29 USC §2101):** 100+ employees; plant closing (50+) or mass layoff (33% + 50, or 500+); 60-day notice; back pay damages.

**Asset deal:** seller WARN through closing date; buyer thereafter (29 USC §2101(b)(1); 20 CFR §639.4(c)). *Day v. Celadon Trucking*: buyer must give 60-day notice **before closing** if not retaining workforce.

**Mini-WARN:** **NJ** (most stringent — 90 days, mandatory severance 1 week/year of service + 4-week penalty if late, no waiver without state approval; effective April 10, 2023); **NY** (50 ee, 90 days); **CA Cal-WARN** (75 ee, 60 days, plus relocation 100+ miles; SB 617 effective Jan 1, 2026 added notice content); **IL** (75 ee), **MA** (Ch. 151A §71A 2-year health continuation), **TN** (50 ee), **WI** (25 ee).

## 5.3 Non-Compete Map

**FTC Non-Compete Rule:** vacated *Ryan v. FTC* (N.D. Tex. Aug 20, 2024); FTC voted 3-1 to **dismiss appeals September 5, 2025**. **State law operative.**

| Jurisdiction | Status |
|---|---|
| **Outright bans (employment)** | CA (§16600/§16600.5), OK, ND, MN (post-July 2023), HI tech, WY (post-July 2025) |
| **Income thresholds** | WA ($123K+), CO ($127K+), IL ($75K), MA (Mass NCAA — garden leave required), MD, ME, NH, DC ($158K), VA, OR, RI |
| **Pro-employer** | FL CHOICE Act (eff. July 1, 2025 — up to 4 years, mandatory injunctive relief) |
| **Reasonable enforceability** | TX, GA, NC, IN, PA, etc. (common-law factors) |

**Sale-of-business exception universally recognized,** including CA §16601 (sale of goodwill or 100% interest), §16602 partnership, §16602.5 LLC. Drafting tip: bind seller-shareholder in shareholder capacity, not employment capacity (*Fillpoint v. Maas*, 208 Cal. App. 4th 1170 — CA invalidated employment overlay).

**Employee non-solicit:** void in California (*Edwards v. Arthur Andersen*, 44 Cal. 4th 937; *AMN Healthcare v. Aya Healthcare*, 28 Cal. App. 5th 923); FTC scrutinizing B2B no-poach as Sherman §1.

## 5.4 §280G Golden Parachute

**Trigger:** parachute payments ≥3× disqualified individual's base amount (5-year W-2 average). **§4999** 20% excise on excess; **§280G** deduction disallowed.

**Disqualified individuals:** any officer; >1% shareholder; top 1%/250 highest-compensated.

**Private company cleansing vote (§280G(b)(5)):** **>75% disinterested-shareholder approval** (excluding disqualified individuals' shares) cleanses parachute treatment entirely. Procedural requirements (Q/A-7): (i) closely-held; (ii) full disclosure; (iii) vote pre-CIC; (iv) disqualified-individual shares excluded; (v) **irrevocable waiver** by each disqualified individual pre-disclosure-and-vote. Abstentions = no.

**S-corp exemption** (§280G(b)(5)(A)(i)). **Public companies:** no cleansing vote; modified gross-ups / cutbacks to 2.99×.

**Mitigation:** reasonable-comp valuation of post-CIC non-competes (Q/A-40); pre-CIC service compensation excluded; Rev. Proc. 2003-68 option valuation.

**Deal-size sensitivity:** Barely applies sub-$10M (low W-2 base); mandatory at mid-market+.

## 5.5 §409A Deferred Compensation

Permissible payment events: separation; disability; death; CIC (§1.409A-3(i)(5)); fixed time; unforeseeable emergency. **Short-term deferral exception:** payment within 2½ months of vesting year-end. Public-company specified-employee 6-month delay. Earnout/contingent payments tied to continued employment may be NQDC subject to 409A.

## 5.6 ERISA — ESOP & Multiemployer Pension

**ESOP** (ERISA §404(a)(1) "highest fiduciary duty known to law"): **adequate consideration** = FMV in good faith by trustee. *Brundle v. Wilmington Trust*, 919 F.3d 763 (4th Cir. 2019) — Constellis ESOP overpaid $29.8M; process matters as much as price.

**Multiemployer pension withdrawal liability** (29 USC §1381+; MPPAA): unsecured statutory liability often exceeding purchase price. Successor liability: *Tsareff v. ManWeb Servs.*, 794 F.3d 841 (7th Cir. 2015) — pre-acquisition notice (even if uncalculated) sufficient; *Heavenly Hana*, 891 F.3d 839 (9th Cir. 2018) — constructive notice sufficient. **§4204 safe harbor:** asset sale to unrelated buyer + buyer commits to 5-year contributions + bond/escrow + seller secondary liability.

## 5.7 Other Employment

**OWBPA (29 USC §626(f))** ADEA waiver: 7 factors including 21-day (45 group) consideration + 7-day revocation + group-disclosure exhibit. *Oubre v. Entergy*: invalid waiver = employee keeps severance and sues.

**NLRA Burns successorship** (*NLRB v. Burns*, 406 U.S. 272; *Fall River Dyeing*, 482 U.S. 27): substantial continuity + majority-of-workforce-from-predecessor → must recognize/bargain (but free to set initial terms unless "perfectly clear" successor).

**Independent contractor:** DOL 2024 Rule technically in effect for private litigation; FAB 2025-1 (May 2025) DOL not enforcing; replacement proposed Feb 27, 2026. **CA AB5/§2775 ABC test** (Dynamex; codified): worker is employee unless **all three** prongs met (Prong B: outside usual course of hiring entity's business — typically dispositive). MA, NJ similar.

# PART 6 — CORPORATE GOVERNANCE & STATE-SPECIFIC LAW [DOMAINS J & N]

## 6.1 The Three Standards of Review

| Standard | Trigger | Authority |
|---|---|---|
| **Business Judgment Rule** | Default; informed disinterested good-faith board | *Aronson v. Lewis*, 473 A.2d 805 |
| **Enhanced Scrutiny** | Sale of control (Revlon); defensive measures (Unocal) | *Revlon*, 506 A.2d 173; *Unocal*, 493 A.2d 946 |
| **Entire Fairness** | Self-dealing / controller on both sides / non-ratable benefit | *Weinberger v. UOP*, 457 A.2d 701 |

## 6.2 Revlon, Unocal, Caremark

**Revlon triggers** (*Paramount v. QVC*, 637 A.2d 34): active bidding; abandonment for break-up; sale to single owner/controlling group. **NOT triggered** by stock-for-stock between widely-held public companies (*Time*, 571 A.2d 1140). Cash >67% generally triggers. Reasonableness, not perfection (*Lyondell v. Ryan*, 970 A.2d 235). No mandatory auction (*Barkan*).

**Unocal/Unitrin:** reasonable threat + proportional response, neither coercive nor preclusive. *Air Products v. Airgas*, 16 A.3d 48 — "Just Say No" defense viable. *The Williams Companies* (Del. Ch. 2021) — COVID-era 5%-trigger pill struck down.

**Caremark oversight:** *Marchand v. Barnhill*, 212 A.3d 805 (Del. 2019) inaugurated modern revival; *Boeing* (Del. Ch. 2021) — $237.5M settlement; *McDonald's* (Del. Ch. 2023) extended to officers. Mission-critical risk monitoring; bad-faith standard.

## 6.3 MFW & SB 21 (March 25, 2025)

**Common-law MFW** (*Kahn v. M&F Worldwide*, 88 A.3d 635): controller transaction → BJR if (1) ab initio dual protections; (2) independent committee (each member, post-*Match*); (3) committee empowered to say no + select advisors; (4) committee meets duty of care; (5) informed M-of-M vote; (6) no coercion. *Flood v. Synutra*, 195 A.3d 754 — "ab initio" = before substantive economic negotiation.

***In re Match Group***, 315 A.3d 446 (Del. 2024): MFW applies to **all** controller transactions with non-ratable benefit; both protections required; **every** committee member must be independent.

**SB 21 amendments to DGCL §144** (effective March 25, 2025; constitutionality affirmed *Rutledge v. Clearway Energy*, Del. Feb 27, 2026):
- Non-going-private controller transactions: **either** disinterested-director committee OR disinterested-minority vote bars equitable relief and damages.
- Going-private (Rule 13e-3): **both** required.
- Controlling stockholder: ≥33⅓% voting + managerial authority OR majority owner.
- Public-company directors: presumption of independence.
- Retroactive to transactions not litigated before Feb 17, 2025.

**Tornetta saga:** *Tornetta I/II* (Del. Ch. Jan + Dec 2024) rescinded $56B Musk pay; **Delaware Supreme Court reversed Dec 19, 2025** — rescission improper, $1 nominal damages + 4× lodestar fees; remedy ruling, not entire-fairness reversal. *Match*'s six-prong MFW + ab initio rules remain intact.

## 6.4 Other Critical Doctrines

**Corwin** (*Corwin v. KKR Financial*, 125 A.3d 304): fully informed, uncoerced disinterested vote restores BJR post-closing; does not apply to controller transactions or pre-closing injunctions; one material disclosure deficiency defeats. *Volcano* (Del. 2017): §251(h) tender = vote.

**Trulia** (*In re Trulia*, 129 A.3d 884): rejected disclosure-only settlements unless "plainly material"; M&A litigation migrated to federal court / mootness fees.

**Appraisal §262 — Dell trilogy** (2017–2019): deal price most reliable indicator of fair value when arms-length sale process; *DFC Global*, *Dell*, *Aruba*. 2016 amendments: de minimis exception (1% / $1M); pre-payment option; withdrawal right.

**§220 books-and-records:** SB 21 narrowed to enumerated list (charter/bylaws/minutes/board materials/financial statements/D&O questionnaires); excludes emails/texts; "compelling need" + clear-and-convincing for broader scope. *Roberta Ann Wong Leung v. Amazon* (Del. July 2025): credible-basis remains lowest possible burden.

**SB 313 (2024 DGCL amendments):** §122(18) stockholder governance agreements; §147 board approval of "substantially final" agreements; §261(a)(1) lost-premium damages clauses; §261(a)(2) stockholder representative; §268 disclosure schedules not part of merger agreement.

## 6.5 State-Specific Variations

**Delaware:** DGCL dominance; Court of Chancery; SB 313/SB 21 amendments. **California:** §2115 quasi-foreign doctrine (excluded for listed corporations per *VantagePoint*); cumulative voting default; broader appraisal §1300; non-compete ban. **New York:** BCL §623 broader appraisal; Martin Act §352-c (broad AG authority, no scienter); 2/3 default merger vote. **Texas:** TBOC; HB 19 created Texas Business Court (Sept 2024); SB 29 (2025) codified BJR + narrowed books-and-records; SB 1057 + SB 2411 (2025); Tesla redomesticated June 2024. **Florida:** FBCA Ch. 607; homestead protection; business broker = real estate license; CHOICE Act (effective July 2025). **Massachusetts:** Ch. 156D; *Donahue* close-corp duty of utmost good faith; Mass NCAA garden leave; broader appraisal. **Washington:** RCW 23B; B&O tax; non-compete income threshold $123K+.

# PART 7 — IP, ENVIRONMENTAL & POST-CLOSING [DOMAINS K, L, P]

## 7.1 IP Chain of Title

**Recordation:** patents 35 USC §261 (3 months BFP cut-off); copyrights 17 USC §205 (1 month domestic / 2 month foreign); trademarks 15 USC §1060 (3 months). Federal records exclusive for federal IP; UCC for security interests.

**The "hereby assign" trap** (*Stanford v. Roche*, 563 U.S. 776; *FilmTec v. Allied-Signal*, 939 F.2d 1568): "I hereby assign" = present assignment of future inventions; "I agree to assign" = mere equitable obligation defeated by intervening "hereby" agreement. **Diligence must verify every IIA contains "hereby assign."**

**State variations:** CA Lab. Code §2870, DE 19 Del. C. §805, IL, KS, MN, NC, UT, WA limit assignability of employee inventions on own time without employer resources/business relation.

**Joint inventors** (*Ethicon v. U.S. Surgical*, 135 F.3d 1456): each co-owner of entire patent (every claim) and may license without consent — destroys exclusivity. Diligence inventor-completeness against §256.

**Work-for-hire** (*CCNV v. Reid*, 490 U.S. 730): only nine statutory categories + employee/scope; **software not on the list** — must use assignment.

**Trademark:** Lanham Act §10 — must include goodwill; "naked assignment" voids mark (*Marshak v. Green*; *PepsiCo v. Grapette*). ITU pre-SOU non-assignable except to successor of business.

## 7.2 Open Source

**Critical contamination risks:** GPL v2/v3 (strong copyleft on derivatives); **AGPL v3 (network/SaaS trigger** — § 13 disclosure on user interaction); SSPL (network + service infrastructure).

**Enforcement:** *Jacobsen v. Katzer*, 535 F.3d 1373 (Fed. Cir. 2008) — license violation = copyright infringement (injunctive relief, statutory damages). *SFC v. Vizio* (Cal. Super. Ct.; 9th Cir. denied removal) — third-party-beneficiary standing for end users could materially expand enforcement; pending as of 2026.

**SBOM (Executive Order 14028; NIST IR 8517):** required for federal-vendor software; standard M&A diligence artifact. CycloneDX / SPDX formats.

## 7.3 Software License Transferability

**Cincom v. Novelis**, 581 F.3d 431 (6th Cir. 2009): copyright/patent licenses presumptively non-assignable absent licensor consent; even internal restructuring constitutes transfer. *In re CFLC* (9th Cir.) reinforces in bankruptcy. Reverse-triangular merger may help (*Meso Scale v. Roche*, 116 A.3d 1244 (Del. 2015)) but jurisdiction-dependent.

## 7.4 Privacy Law Matrix (mid-2026)

**19 state comprehensive privacy laws** in effect or coming online. Critical variations:
- **California CCPA/CPRA** — only state covering employee/B2B data; Delete Act SB 362 (Jan 2026); private right of action for breach.
- **Maryland MODPA** (Oct 2025) — strict-necessity standard; sensitive-data sale ban; most restrictive.
- **Washington My Health My Data Act** — broad consumer health data; **private right of action under WA CPA**; geofencing prohibition.
- **Texas TDPSA** (July 2024) — broadest applicability (no thresholds); Nebraska modeled.
- **Illinois BIPA** — strict liability; $1K/$5K per violation; *Cothron* per-scan accrual narrowed by SB 2979 (2024).

**M&A trigger:** asset transfer of personal info may itself be "sale"/"sharing" requiring notice/opt-out absent merger exception (Cal. Civ. Code §1798.140(ad)(2)(D)).

## 7.5 SEC Cybersecurity Rule (Form 8-K Item 1.05)

Effective Dec 18, 2023. 4-business-day disclosure of material cybersecurity incident after materiality determined (without unreasonable delay). Reg. S-K Item 106 annual risk-management/governance disclosure. Five C&DIs June 2024 on ransomware. **Materiality determination separate from discovery date.**

## 7.6 Environmental — CERCLA Framework

42 USC §§9601–9675. Strict, joint, several, retroactive. Four PRP categories. *Bestfoods*, 524 U.S. 51 — parent direct liability requires operator role at facility (not subsidiary).

**AAI / Phase I ESA:** **ASTM E1527-21 mandatory since Feb 13, 2024.** Key changes: PCB materials added; PFAS addressable as REC where state law triggers; expanded historical research; refined REC/HREC/CREC; 180-day shelf life from earliest component.

**PFAS:** EPA designated PFOA/PFOS as CERCLA hazardous substances April 19, 2024 (effective July 8, 2024); reaffirmed Sept 17, 2025. *U.S. Chamber v. EPA* (D.C. Cir.) in abeyance. PFAS now must be addressed in Phase I at AFFF/plating/semiconductor/textiles/cosmetics sites.

**BFPP (§107(r))**: post-1/11/2002 acquisition + AAI + ongoing reasonable steps + cooperation + appropriate care + no PRP affiliation. **BUILD Act 2018:** extended to lessees; expanded grants.

**State property-transfer statutes:** **NJ ISRA** (N.J.S.A. 13:1K-6 — mandatory pre-transfer for "industrial establishments"); **CT Transfer Act** (sunsetting **March 1, 2026**, replaced by Release-Based Cleanup Regulations); **MA MCP**; **NY BCP**; **CA DTSC VCP**.

## 7.7 Post-Closing & PMI

**Indemnification claim notices:** prejudice standard default (*GRT v. Marathon GTF*); strict compliance only with "condition precedent" language. R&W policy notice "as soon as practicable."

**Earnout disputes:** *Lazard v. QinetiQ* (intent vs. effort); *Fortis v. J&J* (Del. Ch. 2024) ~$1.1B for breach of efforts covenants; *Chicago Bridge & Iron v. Westinghouse*, 166 A.3d 912 — PPA mechanism narrow; cannot litigate historic GAAP through it.

**Joint defense / common interest:** *In re Teleglobe*, 493 F.3d 345 (3d Cir. 2007) — joint-client vs. community-of-interest; substantially identical legal interest required (not commercial alignment). Pre-signing buyer/target communications generally not covered; post-signing only with specific common legal interest.

**Disclosure schedule supplementation:** typical drafting — informational only (no cure for indemnity or termination). Negotiate explicitly.
